### UNIT 4: TRADE THEORIES & FDI APPROACHES

#### 1. Importance and Classification of Trade Theories
*   **Why Study Trade Theories?** They explain international trade patterns, help governments formulate trade policies to maximize national welfare, and guide firms in identifying location-specific and comparative advantages.
*   **Classification:**
    1.  **Classical/Country-Based Theories:** Focus on country-level factors (Mercantilism, Absolute Advantage, Comparative Advantage, Opportunity Cost, Reciprocal Demand).
    2.  **Modern/Firm-Based Theories:** Focus on factor endowments and firm-specific advantages (Heckscher-Ohlin Theory, Product Life Cycle, Global Strategic Rivalry).
    3.  **FDI/MNC-Based Theories:** Explain why firms invest directly in foreign production rather than exporting or licensing (Market Imperfections, Eclectic Paradigm, Transaction Cost).

#### 2. Classical Trade Theories at a Glance
*   **Mercantilism (16th-18th Century):** Believed a nation's wealth and power are measured by its gold and silver reserves. Advoked protectionism, maximizing exports, and minimizing imports. It is a **zero-sum game** (one country's gain is another's loss).
    *   *Criticism:* Gold accumulation leads to domestic inflation, making exports uncompetitive. It ignores consumer welfare.
*   **Absolute Cost Advantage (Adam Smith, 1776):** A country should specialize in and export goods it can produce more efficiently (using less labor) than another country, and import goods where it has an absolute disadvantage.
    *   *Assumptions:* Labor is the only factor of production and is mobile domestically but immobile internationally; constant returns to scale; free trade.
*   **Comparative Cost Advantage (David Ricardo, 1817):** Trade is mutually beneficial even if one country has an absolute advantage in producing *both* goods. The country should specialize in the good where its relative efficiency is higher (greatest comparative advantage) and import the good where its relative efficiency is lower (least comparative disadvantage).
*   **Haberler's Opportunity Cost Theory (1936):** Overcame Ricardo's labor-only limitation by defining the cost of a good as the amount of another good that must be sacrificed to produce an additional unit of it. Illustrated using the **Production Possibility Curve (PPC)**.
*   **Mill's Theory of Reciprocal Demand (1921):** Determines the actual barter terms of trade (exchange ratio) between two nations based on the strength and elasticity of each country's demand for the other's product.

#### 3. Heckscher-Ohlin (H-O) Theory & The Leontief Paradox
*   **H-O Theory (Factor Endowment Theory):** Proposes that a country's comparative advantage is determined by its relative factor endowments. A country will export goods that intensively use its abundant, cheap factors (e.g., a labor-abundant country like India exports labor-intensive goods) and import goods that require its scarce factors (e.g., importing capital-intensive goods).
*   **The Leontief Paradox:** 
    *   *The Study:* Wassily Leontief (1947) tested the H-O model using US input-output data. 
    *   *The Paradox:* Despite the US being a highly capital-rich country, Leontief found that US exports were labor-intensive and its imports were capital-intensive, directly contradicting the H-O theory.
    *   *Explanations to Resolve the Paradox:*
        1.  **US Tariff Policy:** Tariffs heavily protected US labor-intensive industries, reducing labor-intensive imports.
        2.  **Human Capital Investment:** Highly skilled US labor (engineers, scientists) was so productive that US "labor" was effectively capital-intensive in terms of knowledge.
        3.  **Natural Resource Imports:** The US imported resource-heavy raw materials that required large capital investments to extract, skewing the data.

#### 4. Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) Theories
*   **Stephen Hymer's Market Imperfections Approach (1976):** Neoclassical trade theories assumed perfect competition, making foreign investment illogical (since local firms have a natural home advantage). Hymer argued that multinational enterprises (MNEs) exist because of *market imperfections* (monopolistic advantages like proprietary technology, brand name, economies of scale, and access to capital). These firm-specific ownership advantages offset the higher cost of operating in a foreign country.
*   **Vernon's Product Life Cycle (PLC) Theory:** Products go through three stages:
    1.  *New Product Stage:* Innovated and produced in the home country (high-income market) to meet local demand.
    2.  *Maturing Product Stage:* Production expands; exports begin. Competitors emerge, and MNEs set up production facilities in other developed nations to defend markets.
    3.  *Standardized Product Stage:* Production is completely standardized. MNEs shift factories to low-cost developing countries to minimize costs, and the home country eventually imports the product.
*   **Dunning's Eclectic Paradigm (The OLI Model):** A synthesis of FDI theories stating that a firm will undertake FDI only when three advantages coexist:

### UNIT 5: WTO AGREEMENTS

#### 1. Distinction between GATT and WTO
| Feature | GATT (General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade) | WTO (World Trade Organization) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Nature** | A multilateral treaty, not an organization. | A permanent international organization with legal personality. |
| **Scope** | Covered trade in goods only. | Covers trade in goods, services (GATS), and intellectual property (TRIPS). |
| **Commitment** | Applied on a provisional basis. | Agreements are full, permanent, and binding ("Single Undertaking"). |
| **Dispute Settlement** | Slow, non-automatic. Parties could block rulings (consensus-based). | Fast, automatic, with a binding Appellate Body (negative-consensus rule). |
| **Secretariat** | Small, ad-hoc secretariat. | Large, permanent secretariat in Geneva. |

#### 2. Key WTO Agreements & Business Implications
*   **Agreement on Tariffs:** Promotes reducing tariff rates and "binding" them (setting a ceiling rate that a nation cannot arbitrarily exceed). Establishes:
    *   *Most-Favoured-Nation (MFN) Tariffs:* Standard, non-discriminatory tariffs applied to all WTO members.
    *   *Preferential Tariffs:* Lower tariff rates applied under regional trade blocs (FTAs/RTAs).
*   **Agreement on Agriculture (AoA):** Aims to establish a fair and market-oriented agricultural trading system through three pillars:
    *   *Market Access:* Converting non-tariff barriers into tariffs and reducing them.
    *   *Export Subsidies:* Binding and phasing out subsidies that artificially lower export prices.
    *   *Domestic Support:* Reducing trade-distorting farm support, classified into "boxes":
        *   **Green Box:** Minimal or no trade distortion (e.g., R&D, environmental protection, crop insurance). Allowed without limits.
        *   **Blue Box:** Direct payments under production-limiting programs. Allowed with minimal restrictions.
        *   **Amber Box:** Trade-distorting subsidies (e.g., price supports, input subsidies like fertilizer/power). Subject to reduction commitments (De Minimis levels).
*   **TRIPS (Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights):** Sets minimum standards of protection and enforcement for copyrights, trademarks, patents, industrial designs, geographical indications (GI), and trade secrets.
*   **TRIMS (Trade-Related Investment Measures):** Prohibits host countries from applying investment rules that restrict trade (e.g., local content requirements or trade-balancing rules that force foreign firms to export a certain amount).
*   **SPS & TBT Measures:** 
    *   *Sanitary & Phytosanitary (SPS) Agreement:* Rules on food safety and animal/plant health protection.
    *   *Technical Barriers to Trade (TBT) Agreement:* Ensures technical regulations, packaging, and labeling standards do not create unnecessary obstacles to trade.

#### 3. GATS: Four Modes of Supply of Services
The General Agreement on Trade in Services (GATS) defines how services are traded across borders:
1.  **Mode 1: Cross-Border Supply:** The service crosses the border, but both the provider and consumer remain in their home countries.
    *   *Example:* An Indian IT company sending a software design or consultancy report via email to a client in Germany.
2.  **Mode 2: Consumption Abroad:** The consumer travels to the territory of another member nation to consume the service.
    *   *Example:* A French tourist visiting India (Tourism), or an American student studying at Oxford University (Education).
3.  **Mode 3: Commercial Presence:** A service supplier establishes a business or professional presence in another country.
    *   *Example:* Citibank opening branch offices in India, or KFC setting up outlets in Japan.
4.  **Mode 4: Presence of Natural Persons:** An individual travels to another country temporarily to provide a service.
    *   *Example:* An Indian software engineer traveling to Silicon Valley for a six-month onsite implementation project.

### UNIT 6: REGIONAL TRADE BLOCS

#### 1. Levels/Types of Economic Integration
Regional Trade Blocs (RTAs) represent agreements where countries offer preferential terms to one another. They follow a sequential level of integration:

```
[Preferential Trade Agreement (PTA)] -> Lower tariffs on selected positive-list items.
   ↓
[Free Trade Area (FTA)] -> Eliminate internal tariffs; members keep independent external tariffs. (e.g., NAFTA).
   ↓
[Customs Union (CU)] -> FTA + Common External Tariff (CET) against non-members. (e.g., SACU).
   ↓
[Common Market] -> Customs Union + Free movement of labor and capital. (e.g., CACM).
   ↓
[Economic Union] -> Common Market + Harmonized monetary/fiscal policies, common currency. (e.g., EU).
   ↓
[Political Union] -> Complete integration of political institutions, single foreign policy.
```

*   **Rules of Origin (RoO):** Crucial in FTAs. They prevent "backdoor" imports where a non-member tries to route goods through the lowest-tariff member of the FTA to access the rest of the block duty-free.

#### 2. Benefits of Regional Trade Blocs
*   **Economies of Scale & Scope:** Access to a larger combined market allows firms to scale up production, reducing unit costs.
*   **Greater Production Efficiencies:** Specialization based on comparative advantage ensures resources are allocated to their most productive use. Intense competition within the block drives innovation.
*   **Employment & Economic Growth:** Attracts foreign direct investment (FDI) due to the large, duty-free market size. Drives economic growth through increased consumer spending and higher wages.
*   **Trade Creation vs. Trade Diversion:** 
    *   *Trade Creation:* High-cost domestic production is replaced by lower-cost imports from a member country (welfare-enhancing).
    *   *Trade Diversion:* Lower-cost imports from a non-member country are replaced by higher-cost imports from a member country due to the tariff preferences (welfare-reducing).

#### 3. Role of the European Union (EU) in International Business
*   **The Model Union:** The EU represents the most integrated trade bloc in the world (Economic Union). 
*   **Key Contributions:**
    *   *Single Currency (Euro):* Eliminates exchange rate risk and transaction costs for 20 eurozone countries.
    *   *Unified Market:* A borderless market of 450+ million affluent consumers, making it a key destination for global exports.
    *   *Policy Standard-Setter:* The EU's strict regulations (e.g., GDPR on data privacy, REACH on chemicals) often become default global standards that MNCs must adopt worldwide.
