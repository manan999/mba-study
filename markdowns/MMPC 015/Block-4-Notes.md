### UNIT 11: ETHICS IN RESEARCH

#### 1. Concept of Research Ethics
Research ethics refers to the standards or norms of behavior that guide moral choices and relationships concerning others. The fundamental objective is to ensure that **no participant is harmed, deceived, or suffers negative consequences** from the research activity.

#### 2. Twelve Core Principles of Research Ethics
1.  **Honesty:** Truthfulness in reporting data, results, methods, and publication status. Do not fabricate or misrepresent.
2.  **Integrity:** Assuring sincerity, keeping promises, and adhering to agreements. Avoid raising unrealistic hopes.
3.  **Objectivity:** Actively minimizing bias in experimental design, data analysis, and interpretation.
4.  **Informed Consent:** Ensures participants join voluntarily, intentionally, and with a sensible understanding of the study's goals, risks, and benefits.
5.  **Beneficence:** Maximizing potential benefits to participants and society while minimizing any possible physical or psychological harm.
6.  **Protecting Subjects:** Upholding individual privacy, dignity, and autonomy.
7.  **Confidentiality:** Protecting participants' identity, records, and responses from unauthorized exposure.
8.  **Responsible Publication:** Avoid duplicate submissions, self-plagiarism, or premature publications.
9.  **Non-Discrimination:** Treating all participants and stakeholders equally regardless of gender, race, age, religion, or caste.
10. **Openness:** Being receptive to constructive feedback, critical review, and sharing resources.
11. **Carefulness:** Thoroughly checking and editing to avoid negligent errors and biases.
12. **Respect for Intellectual Property:** Honoring copyrights and patents. Never use others' ideas or work without explicit credit.

#### 3. Three Major Research Misconducts
*   **Fabrication:** Inventing or making up completely fake data or experiments and reporting them as genuine.
*   **Falsification:** Manipulating research materials, equipment, or processes, or changing/omitting data to force a desired outcome.
*   **Plagiarism:** Stealing and using another person’s ideas, text, processes, or results without giving proper credit or citation.
    *   *How to Avoid Plagiarism:* Always paraphrase extensively when using reference sources, use quotation marks for exact text matches, provide clear in-text citations, and use similarity check software prior to submission.

### UNIT 12: SUBSTANCE OF REPORTS

#### 1. Academic vs. Business Reports
*   **Academic Reports:** Focus on comprehensive, application-oriented learning and contributing to subject literature. Known as term papers, projects, theses, or dissertations.
*   **Business (Managerial) Reports:** Form the direct basis for executive decision-making. They must be concise, action-oriented, and highly focused on solving practical organizational problems.

#### 2. The Research Proposal
A proposal is an essential, cost-control blueprint written prior to starting a study. It ensures the researcher and sponsor are on the same page.
*   **Ten Key Components of a Proposal:**
    1. Descriptive Title of the Study.
    2. Names and background of researchers.
    3. Nature of the study (Problem definition, significance, scope, and target readership).
    4. Working hypotheses to be tested.
    5. Data parameters (Sources, collection procedures, analytical methodology).
    6. Special equipment or facilities required.
    7. Detailed scheduling and milestones (milestone chart/targets).
    8. Tentative outline of the final product.
    9. Comprehensive cost estimates.
    10. Initial bibliography.

#### 3. Three Categories of Reports
1.  **Information-Oriented Reports:** Focus on presenting a comprehensive, objective picture of an existing situation. Built by asking the **6 Ws + 1 H** iteratively: *Who does What to Whom, When, Where, How, and Why?*
2.  **Decision-Oriented Reports:** Adopt a systematic problem-solving approach. Key steps include:
    *   *Problem Definition:* Identifying symptoms vs. root causes, using Kepner-Tregoe analysis (sorting information under *Is / Is Not*).
    *   *Criteria Construction:* Building yardsticks using SWOT analysis (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) to evaluate options.
    *   *Option Evaluation:* Generating creative options, screening them, and scoring them against criteria.
    *   *Action and Contingency Plans:* Defining *who does what when*, and preparing a "parachute" plan to handle potential failures.
3.  **Research-Oriented Reports:** Designed to expand subject knowledge. Structured sequentially: Literature survey $\rightarrow$ Hypotheses $\rightarrow$ Methodology $\rightarrow$ Data Description $\rightarrow$ Findings $\rightarrow$ Conclusions $\rightarrow$ Recommendations.

#### 4. Six Areas for Reviewing a Draft
Before final submission, a draft report must be rigorously reviewed across:
1.  **Author's Purpose:** Is the communication objective explicit and unambiguous?
2.  **Reader's Profile:** Is the language tailored to the hierarchy (boss vs. colleague), cultural, educational, and interest level of the audience?
3.  **Content:** Is the focus sharp from the first paragraph? Are facts accurate and arguments logical?
4.  **Language and Tone:** Is it clear, simple, and free from unnecessary jargon?
5.  **Length:** Is it concise and respectful of the reader's time?
6.  **Appearance:** Is the layout, typography, and binding professional and neat?

### UNIT 13: FORMATS OF REPORTS

#### 1. Parts of a Report
The standard structure of a formal business or research report consists of:
*   **Cover and Title Page:** Title, target audience, date, purpose, author name, and circulation restrictions (e.g., *"Strictly Confidential"*).
*   **Introductory Pages:**
    *   *Foreword & Preface:* Context, scope, and introductory remarks.
    *   *Acknowledgements:* Expressing gratitude to contributors and sponsors.
    *   *Table of Contents:* Clear list of chapters and page numbers.
    *   *Lists of Tables & Illustrations:* Directories of raw data graphics.
    *   *Executive Summary:* A high-impact, standalone summary of the problem, findings, and recommendations.
*   **Text (Main Body):** Chapters with structured headings, quotations, footnotes, and exhibits.
*   **Reference Section:**
    *   *Appendices:* For bulky data sheets, questionnaire samples, or complex formulas.
    *   *Bibliography:* A list of books and articles consulted.
    *   *Glossary:* Definitions of technical terms used.

#### 2. Copyreading vs. Proofreading
*   **Copyreading:** Reviewing the raw manuscript *before* it is sent to the printer. The focus is on editing grammar, style, factual consistency, and paragraph transitions.
*   **Proofreading:** Reviewing the printed/galley proofs *after* typesetting. The focus is strictly on finding typographical and layout errors introduced by the printer.

#### 3. How to Proofread (Key Guidelines)
*   **Letter-by-Letter:** Proofs must be read letter-by-letter rather than word-by-word to catch spelling mistakes.
*   **Margin Marks:** Corrections must be indicated in the **margin against the line**, using standard symbols. The printer only looks at the margins; marks made inside the text line will be missed.
*   **Stages of Proofs:**
    1.  *Galley Proofs (First Proofs):* Long, single-column sheets (up to 24 inches) before page division.
    2.  *Page Proofs:* Divided into actual page templates to see layout and page numbers.
    3.  *Press Dummy:* The final mock-up showing the exact printed book version before printing begins.
*   **Do Not Edit:** Proofs are for *correcting printer errors*, not for re-writing sections. Large-scale author alterations (AA) at this stage are extremely slow and expensive.

### UNIT 14: PRESENTATION OF A REPORT

#### 1. Core Presentation Skills
An excellent report can fail if it is poorly presented. Success depends on understanding your audience, structuring your speech around a clear main message, managing delivery pacing, and projecting confident body language.

#### 2. Classification of Audio-Visual (AV) Aids
```
                           AUDIO-VISUAL AIDS
                                   │
         ┌─────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┐
         ▼                         ▼                         ▼
       AUDIO                    VISUAL                 AUDIO-VISUAL
  (Tape Recorder, CD)              │                 (Video, Film, TV)
                 ┌─────────────────┴─────────────────┐
                 ▼                                   ▼
           NON-PROJECTED                         PROJECTED
      (Blackboard, Flip Chart)            (Overhead, Slides, Epidiascope)
```

#### 3. Crucial Advantages of AV Aids
*   **Credibility & Clarity:** Recreates complex reality in miniature, making theories concrete.
*   **Retention & Recall:** Activates both visual and auditory senses simultaneously, increasing audience concentration and memory retention.
*   **Collapsing Distance & Time:** Allows you to present case studies and data from far-off places and historical times instantly.

#### 4. Essential Precautions for Presenting with AV Aids
*   **Avoid Screen-Staring:** Never turn your back to the audience to read the slides. Keep eye contact with the listeners; the slides are for *them*, not a script for you.
*   **The 6x6 Rule:** Avoid cluttered slides. Aim for a maximum of 6 bullet points per slide, and 6 words per bullet point.
*   **Legibility Check:** Ensure font sizes and contrast are large enough to be read clearly from the very back of the hall.
*   **Technical Rehearsal:** Arrive early to test slide transitions, projector compatibility, lighting, and sound equipment. Have a backup plan (e.g., printed handouts) in case of a power outage or equipment failure.
*   **Cue Cards:** Use small cue cards for your speaking notes rather than reading from a heavy folder or the screen. Keep transitions smooth and natural.
