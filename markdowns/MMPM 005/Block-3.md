8.1  INTRODUCTION

For service industries, it was observed that the traditional marketing mix
was inadequate because of three main reasons. The first reason was that the
original marketing mix was developed for manufacturing industries, which
implies that the services offered by service companies ought to be changed
in a more product like manner so that the existing marketing tools can be
applied. This was practically difficult.

The  second  reason  was  that  the  marketing  practitioners  in  the  service
sector found that the marketing mix does not address to their needs. They
observed that the services have certain basic characteristics, which in turn
have marketing implications (as discussed in Unit 2). For example, there is
a problem as regard to maintaining the quality due to lack of standardization
or services cannot be inventoried, patented or transferred.

The third reason was that since services are basically different in comparison
to physical products the marketing models and concepts have, therefore, to
be developed in direction of the service sector.

119

The above three observations suggest that a revised framework for service
marketing mix is required and dimension of each of the mix elements should
be redefined. The marketing mix has extended beyond 4Ps for marketing of
services. These additional Ps are added to meet the marketing challenges
posed by the characteristics of services.

Booms  and  Bitner  though  without  any  empirical  work  suggested  a  ‘7  P’
marketing mix model arising out of the above three observations. McGrath
and others endorsed such an approach, and a number of marketing research
studies supplement the relevance of each of the ‘7 Ps’.

In this unit we will discuss the fifth element of the services marketing mix
called People.

8.2  PEOPlE

People  constitute  an  important  dimension  in  the  management  of  services
in  their  role  both  as  performers  of  services  and  as  customers.  People  as
performers of services are important because, “A customer sees a company
through  its  employees.  The  employees  represent  the  first  line  of  contact
with the customer. They must, therefore, be well informed and provide the
kind of service that wins customer approval. The firm must recognise that
each employee is a salesman for the company’s service”. The importance
of customers in services stems from the fact that most services imply active
and involved customer-organisation interface.

8.2.1  Service Personnel
  Service  personnel  are  important  in  all  organisations  but  more  so  in  an
organisation  involved  in  providing  services.  The  behaviour  and  attitude
of  the  personnel  providing  the  service  is  an  important  influence  on  the
customer’s overall perception of the service and he can rarely distinguish
between the actual service rendered and the human element involved in it.

How often have you called a call center for any kind of service support and
had to wait before getting a response? What do you think the call center
employees have done to help that service company’s reputation?

Due  to  technology  and  the  availability  of  alternative  methods  of
communication and information seeking, including as email, chatbots, social
media sites, etc., the case of the call center employee is still under control
because it provides a low contact system. High or low contact is defined on
the basis of percentage of total time the customer has to spend in the system
compared with the relative time it takes to service him. By this definition,
restaurants,  hospitals,  schools  represent  a  high  contact  organisation.  The
other important distinction of service personnel is between those that are
visible to the customer and those that are not. In a restaurant, the waiters
are visible while the cook in the kitchen is not. As a marketing manager you
have  to  devote  more  time  training  the  visible  personnel,  since  they  have
greater responsibility in maintaining relations with the customer. The rice
dish which is not cooked properly is the cook’s fault but it is the waiter who
will have to bear the brunt of the customers’ anger. The manner in which the
waiter behaves with the customer will be an important determinant in the
restaurant losing that customer forever or retaining him as a regular client.

8.2.2  Customers
Customers  are  important  because  they  are  a  source  of  influencing  other
customers.  In  the  case  of  doctors,  lawyers,  consultants  one  satisfied
customer will lead to a chain reaction, bringing in his wake a number of other
customers. Thus, as a marketing manager, your first task should be to ensure
complete satisfaction of the existing customers. The kind of customers that
you  attract  exerts  an  important  influence  on  prospective  customers.  The
prospective customer may feel attracted towards the organisation (it may be
a restaurant, club, school, college) because it has his ‘type’ of customer or
the customer may turn away if he perceives the existing customers to be of a
kind with whom he would not like to associate. You have to decide about the
class of customers you would like to have and work towards providing your
service organisation an image which will fetch you your future customers.

8.3  CHAllENGES OF SERVICE PERSONNEl

Service personnel that interact with customers have some of the most stressful
jobs. Let us discuss some of the main issues facing service personnel that
make their jobs difficult.
8.3.1  Boundary Spanning Roles
Service employees are known as boundary spanners because they function
at  the  boundary  of  the  organisation.  They  serve  as  a  bridge  between  an
organization’s  internal  activities  and  its  external  customers,  stakeholders
and  environment.  They  play  a  vital  role  in  comprehending,  sorting,  and
evaluating data and materials entering and leaving the organisation. They
have a variety of duties to perform, some of which can be contradictory,
which  makes  their  job  challenging.  Some  examples  of  boundary  spanner
personnel include front desk employees or order takers in retail outlets or
restaurants.
8.3.2 Emotional labour
Emotional labour occurs when there is a mismatch between how a service
employee  feels  internally  and  the  emotions  that  management  expects
them  to  display  in  front  of  customers.  An  air  hostess,  for  example,  can
experience emotional labour while giving smiles, maintaining eye contact,
demonstrating genuine interest, and engaging in a discussion with passengers
who are essentially strangers and whom they might never see again.

121

Managing People8.3.3  Role Conflict
Service employees usually need to manage numerous tasks and consumers
at once, which frequently leads to workplace conflicts.

Role  conflicts  have  three  basic  sources,  including  person/role  conflicts,
organization/client conflicts, and interclient conflicts, as discussed below.

Person/Role Conflict.  Service employees may have conflicts between their
personalities, self-perceptions,  and beliefs and  the demands of  their jobs.
For example, the job can call for employees to smile and treat even rude
customers with kindness. Employees who are compelled to dress a certain
way  or  adjust  any  part  of  their  appearance  in  order  to  meet  employment
requirements may also experience person/role conflict.

Organization/Client Conflict. An employee may encounter conflict when
conflicting  expectations  are  placed  on  them  by  the  organisation  and  the
clients,  respectively.  For  example,  many  department  stores  assign  their
personnel  the  task  of  maximising  sales  per  customer  through  suggestion
and engagement. Customers, however, prefer to be left alone to make their
own decisions, and support should only be provided upon request.

Interclient  Conflict.  Employees  frequently  find  it  difficult  or  impossible
to satisfy the complete range of wants of a group of heterogeneous clients
when serving a large number of customers at once. Any college classroom
where the teacher must accommodate a wide range of student expectations
and varying preferences for formats and style will obviously show this type
of conflict.

Although the discussion on significance of employees in the business activity
started in mid-seventies the concept of internal marketing was introduced
only afterwards. One school of advocates of this concept initially suggested
that the employer should apply market research, market segmentation and
traditional marketing activities like advertising in order to attract employees
and  make  them  perform  in  the  desired  way.  Such  traditional  marketing

motivated  for  service-mindedness  and  customer-oriented  performance  by
an  active  marketing  like  approach,  where  a  variety  of  activities  are  used

According  to  the  service  profit  chain,  internal  service  quality,  employee
satisfaction, the value of the services offered to customers, and ultimately
customer  satisfaction,  retention,  and  profits  are  all  critically  linked,  as
shown in figure 8.3.

127

Managing PeopleFigure 8.3 The Service Profit Chain

Source:  Adapted  from  J.  L.  Heskett,  T.  O.  Jones,  G.  W.  Loveman,  W.  E.

The  service  profit  chain  hypothesizes  a  relationship  between  customer

the key drivers of a company’s profitability and revenue growth. Customers
are more likely to be satisfied when employees make their interactions with
the company and service experience happy and enjoyable. In other words,

are happy in their jobs, customer satisfaction will be difficult to achieve.
Employees that are satisfied with the company’s internal service culture will
be productive and help to increase profitability by ensuring that consumers
are satisfied and coming back for more, thus attracting repeat business.

Internal quality receives emphasis from the service profit chain. The level
of company policies and support services that enable employees to perform
their  jobs  determines  how  satisfied  they  are.  While  making  investment
decisions,  the  management  of  internal  service  quality  should  receive  the
same consideration as the management of external service quality.

Extended Marketing Mix for Services8.6.1 Hire the Right People
Making the appropriate hires is the first step to doing it correctly. Recruitment
and hiring of service personnel should receive a great deal of attention if
service quality is to be properly delivered. Hiring the right people includes
competing for the best people, hiring for service competencies and service
inclination, and being the preferred employer.

A service organisation must find the best candidates and compete with other
organisations for their hiring in order to secure them. Service competency
and  service  inclination are  two  complementing skills  that  are  required  of
service employees. The abilities and knowledge required to perform a job
are known as service competencies. The term “service inclination” refers to
a desire to work in service-related job, which is demonstrated by attitudes
toward service and orientation toward serving customers and co-workers.
Being  recognised  as  the  preferred  employer  in  a  specific  industry  is  one
approach to attracting the best candidates. An employee survey can be used
to measure it. For example, Google is the preferred employer in its field and
has been named by Fortune as the “Best Company to Work For” seven times
in the last ten years.

8.6.2 Develop People to Deliver Service Quality
Once a service organisation has selected the best employees, it must train
and  interact  with  them  to  ensure  service  quality.  Developing  people  to
deliver  service  quality  comprises  technical  and  interactive  skill  training,
employee empowerment, and teamwork promotion.

Employees  require  continual  training  in  the  required  technical  and
interpersonal abilities in order to deliver quality service. Technical abilities
include  things  like  knowing  how  a  retail  store’s  cash  registers  work  and
how  an  insurance  company  does  its  underwriting.  The  ability  to  interact
effectively with customers is made possible by training in interactive skills.
It allows the service personnel to provide courteous, caring, responsive, and
empathetic service.

Enabling employees with the freedom, knowledge, skills, resources, tools,
and motivation to choose how to best serve customers is what is meant by
employee empowerment.

An  organisation  can  improve  employee  performance  by  encouraging
teamwork,  and  the  support  and  togetherness  they  receive  increases  their
propensity to provide outstanding service.

8.6.3 Provide the Needed Support Systems
Service personnel need internal support mechanisms that are in line with
their  requirement  to  be  customer-focused  in  order  to  be  efficient  and
effective  in  their  job  performance.  For  example,  a  bank  employee  needs
internal support from management, co-workers, and other divisions as well
as technology facilitation for transaction accuracy and customer satisfaction.

Providing the needed support systems includes measuring internal service
quality,  providing  supportive  technology  and  equipment,  and  developing
service-oriented internal processes.

Managing PeopleMeasuring  and  rewarding  internal  service  is  one  strategy  to  promote
positive internal service relationships. Internal customer service audits help

Service employees require the proper tools and technology support in order

The  satisfaction  and  value  of  the  customer  should  be  considered  while

should also make it easier for the service staff to deliver high-quality service.

8.6.4 Retain the Best People
After  recruiting  and  professionally  developing  the  service  personnel  to
deliver desired quality services, service organizations must also try to retain
the  best  people.  Some  strategies  that  will  help  retain  the  best  employees
are  including  employees  in  the  company’s  vision,  treating  employees  as
customers, and measuring and rewarding strong service performers.

To keep employees motivated to work for the organisation and support its
goals, employees of service organisations need to be a part of the vision.

Employee retention rates will likely be better if they feel valued and their
needs are being met by the organization. Many service organizations now
view their employees as internal customers who can be pursued by basic
marketing strategies as targeted to external customers.

The best service performers must be rewarded and promoted for retaining
them. Systems of rewards may take into account factors like productivity,
sales, or another aspect of providing quality service.

8.6.5 Use Automation to reduce dependency on People
Service  industries  find  it  difficult  and  expensive  to  manage  their  service
personnel.  Moreover,  customers  can  notice  variations  in  product  quality
when  different  service  professionals  do  the  same  tasks.  Automation  can
be used to improve uniformity or cut costs. In some services, production
and  delivery  processes  can  be  completely  automated,  such  as  the  use  of
ATMs,  vending  machines,  automatic  car  washes,  etc.  In  other  instances,
equipment  can  help  service  professionals  complete  their  tasks  efficiently
and effectively. As an illustration, consider the employment of computers,
chatbots, tools, etc.

8.7  SUMMARY

People  constitute  an  important  dimension  in  the  management  of  services

9.2.1  Peripheral evidence
Peripheral evidence is actually possessed as a part of the purchase of service

9.3  EFFECTS OF SERVICESCAPE ON

BEHAVIOUR

In the context of services, servicescapes refer to the intentional manipulation
of  physical  aspects  that  creates  a  service  environment  or  atmosphere.
Service customers and employees are key to the success of services, and the
environment in which services are provided has a significant impact on how
they behave. The theoretical foundation for explaining how service settings
affect  customers  and  service  personnel  is  provided  by  environmental
psychology. The two main models will be briefly discussed in this section.

9.3.1  Stimulus–Organism–Response  (SOR)  model-  According  to  this
model, people’s affective states, or emotions and feelings, are influenced by
their environment, which in turn affects how they behave.

In  the  model,  the  multidimensional  environment  serves  as  the  stimulus,
while  customers  and  employees  are  the  organisms  that  respond  to  the
stimulus, and the responses are the behaviours aimed at the environment.
The underlying principle is that various aspects of the servicescape will have
an impact on customers and employees, and that these individuals will act
and react differently based on their internal responses to the servicescape.

According  to  environmental  psychologists,  people  often  respond  to
environments in one of two, opposing ways: approach or avoid. All desirable
activities that might be directed at a specific location, such as a desire to
stay, explore, work, and attach, are considered approach behaviours.  The
reverse, a desire not to stay, explore, work, or attach, is reflected in avoidance
behaviours.

9.3.2 The valence and intensity model of affect - According to this model,
affect  can  be  represented  by  the  two  interrelated  dimensions  of  pleasure
(valence) and arousal (intensity). Pleasure is a direct, subjective response

136

Extended Marketing Mix for Services
to the environment that depends on how much the individual likes or dislikes
the environment. Arousal is a less subjective concept than pleasure quality
and describes how stimulated the person feels. These together decide whether
people approach an environment, spend time and money there, or avoid it.

The  emotional  states  that  are  caused  by  the  service  environment  directly
affect  how  customers  and  employees  respond.  Through  the  formation  of
emotional states, the environment can influence behaviour in the form of
approach or avoidance. Approach behaviours can include the desire to remain
at  the  service  facility,  explore  and  engage  with  the  service  environment,
interact with others, and feel fulfilled. The avoidance behaviours, on the other
hand, manifest as a desire to avoid the service facility, neglecting service
personnel and other customers and causing a sense of disappointment and
dissatisfaction.

9.4  PHYSICAl ENVIRONMENT FACTORS

Physical  environments  are  designed  to  create  a  deliberate  impact  and
elicit a certain response from the target audience. It has influence on both
customer and employees. The physical environment is not the same in all
kinds of services. Depending on the nature and type of services, it varies.
For example, in the case of remote services (such as telephone, insurance,
utility,  etc.),  where  customers  use  the  services  from  a  distance  and  only
employees carry out actions in the service settings, consideration must be
given  to  the  needs,  effectiveness,  motivation,  and  satisfaction  of  service
personnel in the physical environment. When a customer uses a self-service
(such  as  an ATM  or  vending  machine)  to  get  a  service  on  their  own,  the
physical environment should be simple to use and emotionally satisfying.
Whereas,  in  the  case  of  interpersonal  services,  where  both  the  customer
and service personnel share the service facility (as in hospitals, restaurants,
airlines  etc.)  the  physical  environment  must  take  into  account  the  nature
and quality of social interaction that will occur between the customer and
service providers.

There  are  various  elements  that  make  up  the  physical  environment,  and
services  firms  have  a  varied  range  of  physical  environments. According
to Julie Baker, the physical environment is a composite representation of
ambiance, design, and social relationships. Figure 9.1 illustrates these three
physical environment aspects as ambient factors, design considerations, and
social factors.

Ambient Factors

Ambient factors relate to the background conditions below the level of a
customer’s  immediate  awareness.  It  refers  to  aspects  of  the  environment
or  atmospheric  conditions  present  in  a  place  that  affect  your  five  senses.
The  ambient  conditions  include  elements  such  as  temperature  (hot,  cool,
warm),  air  quality  (clean  and  fresh,  polluted/stale,  humidity,  circulation
etc.) background music or noise, and odor (scents and aroma that trigger
olfactory  senses). These  elements  can  all  have  an  impact  on  how  people
feel, think, and respond when they interact with a service provider.

Design Factors

The design factors are those visual stimuli that exist at the forefront of a
customer’s  awareness.  The  aesthetics  of  architecture,  colour  schemes,
materials  used,  shapes,  and  accessory  styles,  among  other  things,  have
significant meaning. Different elements of the physical environment act as
explicit or implicit signals for the users of the place.

Explicit communicators include signs that are placed both inside and outside
of a facility. They can be used as labels (firm names, department names, etc.),
for directional purposes (entrances, exits), and to communicate behavioural
norms (no smoking etc.). Other environmental symbols and artefacts may
convey information less explicitly than signs, offering users implicit hints
about the context of the environment as well as expectations for conduct. In
order to convey service concepts and build first impressions, signs, symbols,
and artefacts are essential. Customers search for environmental signals to
help them identify a certain service facility and set their expectations when
they are unfamiliar with it.

Functional dimensions of design in terms of layout, comfort refers to how
the service facility’s space is set up. Spatial layout refers to the arrangement
of  machines,  equipment,  and  furnishings.  The  size,  form,  and  spatial
relationships of these components are taken into consideration during layout
design. A  well-designed  layout  improves  the  effectiveness  and  efficiency
of service operations and makes customer interactions more comfortable.
Functionality is the capability of those items to make performing service
interactions easier.

The social factors relate to interactive environment comprising of people.
When  evaluating  the  quality-of-service  environment,  the  appearance  and

taken into account because they have the capability to improve or diminish
the  image  that  a  service  environment  creates.  The  concept  of  “aesthetic

9.6  GUIDElINES FOR DEVElOPING PHYSICAl

EVIDENCE STRATEGY

Managing physical evidence is essential for a service business to succeed.
It  significantly  influences  both  employee  and  customer  behaviour.  The
following are some guidelines for managing physical evidence effectively.

Recognize the Strategic Importance of Physical Evidence

As  we  have  already  discussed,  expectations  and  perceptions  of  service
quality  can  be  significantly  influenced  by  physical  evidence.  Therefore,
recognising the importance of physical evidence is a significant first step
for  service  organisations. After  that,  for  physical  evidence  strategy  to  be
successful, it must be clearly linked with the organization’s overall goals
and vision. The concept of service for both internal and external clients, as
well as the future vision, must be considered when designing it.

141

Managing Physical EvidenceBlueprint the Physical Evidence of Service

Service blueprinting should be done to ensure clarity regarding how service
processes  are  arranged,  where  people  and  other  environmental  elements,
as well as the equipment, furniture, and other support materials are placed.
Service blueprints can be used to visually indicate opportunities for physical
evidence,  among  other  things.  Physical  evidence  opportunities  could  be
identified at various steps of the service creation and delivery process. For
example, if a customer interacts with service personnel at a counter as the
first stage in the service creation process, the counter offers an opportunity
to manage physical evidence elements for expected outcomes.

Clarify Roles

 As we have discussed earlier in this unit, physical evidence has an influence
on both customers and employees, and the elements of physical evidence
vary depending on the nature and type of services. Clarifying the roles will
make it easier to find opportunities and decide who should be contacted in a
certain situation to make facility design decisions. Clarifying the roles also
acknowledges the significance of the physical evidence in creating positive
service experiences.

Assess and Identify Physical Evidence Opportunities

The ways in which physical evidence can be improved and modified must
be  investigated  by  service  providers.  The  effectiveness  of  the  physical
evidence  in  communicating  the  intended  messages  (high-quality  service,
exceeding customers’ expectations, and firm reputation) can be evaluated.
Based  on  customer  and  employee  assessments  of  the  physical  evidence,
missed opportunities or any flaws in the current physical evidence strategy
could be discovered for improvement. For example, the physical evidence
of a restaurant that promotes “family dining” to its targeted market segment
might  not  be  compatible  with  the  high-priced  menu  cues.  Depending  on
these revelations, the pricing or the layout of the restaurant would need to
change.

Update and Modernize Physical Evidence

Certain physical evidence elements require regular or recurring updating and
modernization. Service providers need to be ready to adapt when the market
changes. The orientation to change makes the service firm innovative and
significantly more effective than its competitors in influencing the opinion
of the customers.

Encourage Team Approach

The service user interacts with a system that exposes him or her to various

10.2  SERVICE PROCESS

Service processes outline the manner and order in which service systems
operate as well as how they interact to produce the value proposition that
customers are promised. Services processes are the architecture of services,
which  must  be  planned  and  managed  to  deliver  the  desired  customer
experience.  The  process  used  to  create  and  deliver  the  service  is  critical
to  the  performance  of  service  operations.     A  restaurant’s  product  is  the
process of preparing and serving foods, while an airline markets the process
of commuting. A haircut is a good example of a simple service operation.
Traveling overseas on an international flight may be a part of more complex
service process.

Shostack  gave  a  much-simplified  version  and  described  the  ‘process’  in
three stages. First, a process can be broken down in logical steps to facilitate
analysis and control. Secondly, there are more than one available options
of processes in which output may differ. Finally, each system includes the
concept of deviation or tolerance standards in recognition that the processes
are ‘real time’ phenomena that do not conform perfectly to any model or
description, but functions within a norm.

Shostack  further  observed  that  in  marketing  literature  no  description
on  process  is  found  although  concepts,  which  relate  to  process  like
‘standardization’  and  ‘customization’,  are  frequently  mentioned.  In  her
article Shostack described processes in two ways.

The  first  way  is  according  to  the  steps  and  sequences  that  constitute  the
process and she termed it as ‘complexity of process’. The second is according
to the exceptional latitude or variability of those steps and sequences, which
she  called  ‘divergence’.  Any  service  process  is  a  combination  of  both-
complexity and divergence. Analyzing the number can identify a service’s
complexity and intricacy of the steps required and the degree of freedom
inherent or allowed in a process step or sequence can be called its divergence.
In reality there could be services where process can be of high complexity
and  low  divergence.  Such  services  are  called  standardized  services.  For
example,  housing  loan  from  any  financial  institution.  On  the  other  hand,
there  could  be  services  with  low  complexity  and  high  divergence.  Such
services can be called customized services.

an understanding of above-described phenomenon is essential as complexity

adjusted  for  efficiency  in  the  process. A  change  in  overall  complexity  or

From an operational standpoint, service processes may be divided into four
major categories based on tangible actions on people’s bodies or physical
possessions  and  intangible  actions  on  people’s  minds  or  their  intangible
assets. These categories are referred to as “people processing,” “possession
processing,” “mental stimulus processing,” and “information processing.”

of  these  four  categories  have  important  implications  for  managers  of
operations, marketing, and human resources.

People Processing

Processes need users or customers to go through a series of actions during
service  delivery  in  order  to  receive  the  desired  benefits  of  such  services.
Passenger  transportation,  hairdressing,  manicures,  and  dental  work  are  a
few examples of people-processing services.

Possession Processing

In  possession  processing,  the  presence  of  the  customer  is  not  required;
the object that needs to be processed must be present. Unlike for people-
processing  services,  production  and  consumption  are  not  necessarily
simultaneous,  because  tangible  treatment  is  given  to  the  physical
possessions of the customer during possession processing. As a result, the
service provider has more flexibility to develop and deliver these services.
Examples of possession processing include air freight, cleaning, lawn care,
home repairs, elevator repairs, etc.

Mental Stimulus Processing

Mental stimulus processing refers to intangible activities that are directed at
people’s minds and have the ability to affect attitudes and influence behaviors.
Education,  news  and  information,  theatre  performances,  entertainment,
sporting  events,  expert  counsel,  and  various  religious  activities  are  all
examples  of  mental  stimulus-processing  services.  Customers  in  these
situations  must  be  mentally  present,  although  they  can  be  situated  either
nearby—for example, in the same lecture hall or sports stadium where the
services are being created—or far away yet connected via broadcast signals
or communications linkages.

Information Processing

Information  processing  refers  to  intangible  operations  carried  out  on  a
customer’s assets. Information and communications technology (ICT), as
well as experts who analyse and package information using their brains, are
two ways that information can be processed. Insurance, banking, financial
counselling,  legal,  market  research,  management consulting,  and  medical
diagnostics  are  a  few  examples  of  industries  that  provide  information
processing  services.  Once  the  service  request  has  been  initiated  in  this
category,  there  may  not  be  much  direct  interaction  with  the  consumer
necessary, and even that can be done over the phone, via the Internet, or by
mail.

information and those that process mental stimuli. For instance, it appears to
be information processing when a stockbroker analyses a client’s brokerage
transactions. It would appear to be mental stimulus processing, nevertheless,
when the analysis’s findings are utilised to suggest the best kind of investing
strategy for the future. In order to keep things simple, we may group together
our  coverage  of  mental  stimulation  and  information-processing  services
under the general heading of information-based services.

PROCESS

The role of customers in the service process has been redefined as a result
of the changing business dynamics brought on by the development of online
and  self-service  technology.  Participation  of  the  customer  in  the  service
delivery  process  is  required. As  a  result,  the  service  user  also  influences
the  service’s  quality.  At  the  same  time,  customers  can  also  influence  or
contribute to their personal satisfaction with the services they receive.

10.5.1 Service Encounters

A “service encounter” is when a customer interacts directly with a service.
In some circumstances, the entire customer service process can be shortened
into a single encounter that includes placing an order, making a payment,
and finishing the service delivery right away. In other cases, the customer’s
experience is made up of a number of interactions, a drawn-out process that
may  be  spread  out  over  time,  include  several  service  personnel,  or  even
take place in different locations. Although some studies exclusively refer to
encounters involving interactions between customers and service personnel,
in  fact  we  also  need  to  take  encounters  involving  interactions  between
customers and self-service technologies into account. The more involved a
customer is with the service operation, the longer and more frequent service
encounters are likely to become.

Some level of customer involvement in service delivery is required in many
services  that  involve  direct  interaction  between  customers  and  service
providers. However, the level of such participation can be broadly classified
as high-contact services, medium-contact services, or low-contact services.

Higher education, retail banking, and the retail shopping are a few examples
of  services  that  previously  required  a  lot  of  interaction  but  are  now

Medium- contact services

In the case of medium-contact services, customers’ inputs are required in
order to help the service providers in developing and delivering the service,
as  well  as  to  provide  some  level  of  customization.  It  includes  situations
where customers come to the service provider’s offices (or are visited by
that provider at their homes or another place) but leave before the service
is  completed.  The  purpose  of  these  interactions  is  commonly  limited  to
building  a  relationship  and  identifying  a  service  need,  as  in  the  case  of
management  consulting,  insurance,  or  personal  financial  advising,  where
clients  initially  visit  the  firm’s  office  but  only  interact  with  the  provider
minimally while the service is being produced. The same is true for many
digital services as well, which usually need users to download applications,
activate them, and provide information before using app-based services.

low-contact services

In the case of low-contact services, service personnel and systems do every
task.  If  necessary,  only  the  customer’s  physical  presence  is  required;  no
actual contact between clients and service providers is required. Examples
include going to the movie theatre or riding a bus.

In today’s technologically advanced society, both mental stimulus processing
(such as radio and television) and information processing services (such as
insurance) naturally fit into this category.

Possession-processing services are also included, in which the item in need
of repair can be shipped to the service facility or subjected to remote fixes
delivered electronically to the customers’ premises from a distant location.
Except for granting access to service providers and paying for them, users
can completely avoid involvement in possession-processing activities like
routine cleaning or maintenance.

Complaints  are  a  natural  consequence  of  any  service  activity.  Mistakes
are a critical part of every service. Since services are generally performed
in  the  presence  of  customers,  errors  are  bound  to  happen.  While  it  may
not be possible to prevent all errors, companies can learn to recover from
them. “Service recovery” refers to the action taken by the service provider
in  response  to  a  service  failure.  Service  recovery,  according  to  Tad  and
Brown,  is  “a  process  that  identifies  service  failures,  effectively  resolves
customer problems, classifies their root cause (s), and yields data that can
be integrated with other performance measures to assess and improve the
service system.” A good recovery can turn angry, frustrated customers into
loyal ones. Furthermore, customers who have been successfully recovered
not  only  remain  loyal  but  can  become  advocates  for  the  organisation
spreading a positive word-of-mouth.

When  an  error  or  service  failure  occurs,  the  customer  may  or  may  not
complain. A customer who doesn’t complain is less likely to come back to
the service provider. Therefore, the service provider has to make specific
efforts to encourage customers to voice their concerns. Complaints provide
feedback  on  how  the  service  provider  is  performing  in  the  market  place.
When  customers  complain,  two  potentially  positive  things  happen  for  a
service provider.

I.

The provider gets the chance to fix the problem and retain the customer
(service recovery).

II.  Complaints can point to areas of the business that need

A good complaint culture and a good complaint process may well lead a
service  provider  to  improved  financial  performance.  Research  has  shown
that  excellent  complaint  management  service  recovery  can  significantly
influence customer satisfaction and loyalty. The service provider, in order
to  improve  the  solicitation  of  complaints,  should  make  it  easy  for  the
customers to get in touch. This can be done by providing multiple means
of  contact,  like  toll-free  numbers,  websites,  social  media,  and  customer
contact  points  with  service  personnel.  Further,  these  should  be  published
on a continuous basis and the customers should be made to feel that their
feedback is invaluable and their opinions are wanted. The company should
treat a complaint as a gift and the one who complains as a friend.

suggestions might be implemented.

10.9  SUMMARY

The importance of process element in the extended service marketing mix
is  discussed  in  this  unit.  Service  processes  outline  the  manner  and  order
in  which  service  systems  operate  as  well  as  how  they  interact  to  deliver
the  value  proposition  that  customers  are  promised.  Consumer  interaction
with  the  system  is  important  for  the  production  of  services  due  to  the
inseparability characteristics of services. However, there are variations in
the  level  of  customer  involvement  in  high-contact,  medium-contact,  and
low-content businesses. From an operational standpoint, service processes
may  be  divided  into  four  major  categories  as  “people  processing,”
“possession  processing,”  “mental  stimulus  processing,”  and  “information
processing.”  The  fundamentally  distinct  types  of  processes  that  are
involved in each of these four categories have important implications for
managers of operations, marketing, and human resources. The importance
of blueprinting in the development of service processes is also covered in
this  unit,  along  with  challenges  relating  to  service  process  management.
Finally, the unit outlines a number of reasons why customers switch, and
most of these reasons are controllable by good complaint management and
effective service recovery.
