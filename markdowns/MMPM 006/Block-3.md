7.1  INTRODUCTION

A  decision  maker  may  have  a  probability  of  being  misled  while  making
a decision based on data. This can be  the  result  of faulty  assumptions or
observations, erroneous or inadequate data, or the existence of unidentified
variables. This is a risk since crucial strategic decisions based on incomplete
information  may  result  in  resource  waste,  missed  opportunities,  and
disastrous results. The true benefit of hypothesis testing in business is that
it  enables  experts  to  validate  their  hypotheses  before  acting  on  them. As
a  result,  a  business  can  essentially  confirm  that  its  analysis  is  accurate
before allocating resources to the implementation of a more comprehensive
strategy. We will discuss the concept, meaning, importance, and process of
hypothesis testing in this unit.

7.2  CONCEPT AND MEANING

In general, open-ended research questions provide a wider perspective on
the problem. In order to focus data gathering on a few key aspects of the
research  questions,  research  questions  are  transformed  into  hypotheses.

This means that the more general idea of a research question is stated in
terms of particular variables as a potential response to the research question.
In addition, a hypothesis can be a statement that links two or more variables
generated from one or more research questions. This relationship must also
be investigated to determine whether it is true or untrue. So, we can say that
hypotheses are expressed in terms of quantifiable variables. Let us use an
example to explain.

Research question: Identify the factors influencing the performance of a
salesperson.

  Hypothesis:  Marital  status  of  salesperson  has  significant  effect  on  sales
performance

The hypothesis has limited the scope of data collection, as opposed to the
research question, which would have required information on a number of
additional  variables,  including  gender,  age,  work  experience,  and  others,
in  order  to  determine  the  profile  of  a  salesperson.  The  aforementioned
hypothesis can be expressed as follows to show a relationship between two
or more research questions:

Hypothesis:  Sales  training  programs  would  influence  unmarried  sales
people to perform better.

Therefore,  the  hypothesis  helped  to  focus  the  effort  of  data  collection  in
order  to  test  the  relation  between  the  variables. A  hypothesis  is  actually
the assumption regarding the anticipated direction of the research findings.
For example, it can be expected that the research will focus on determining
whether there is a direct relationship between sales training and salespeople’s
performance. The more effectively sales training imparts selling skills, the
better the salesperson performs in turning prospective consumers into actual
purchasers of goods and services.

Kerlinger (1986) defines a hypothesis as “... a conjectural statement of the
relationship between two or more variables.” According to Grinnell (1993),
‘A hypotheses is written in such a way that it can be proven or disproven by
valid and reliable data—it is in order to obtain these data that we perform
our study’.

It  is  clear  from  the  aforementioned  definitions  that  a  hypothesis  is  any
assumption a researcher makes on the likely course of outcomes that could
be attained after the research process has been completed. The hypotheses
are always in declarative statement form, in contrast to the research problem,
which typically takes the form of a question. The statements constructed in
this way are subject to empirical investigation.

A type I error occurs when we incorrectly conclude that H0 is false when in
fact H0 is true. Similar to this, when we incorrectly accept H0 while H0 is
untrue, the error is referred to as a type II error.

Think  about  the  packaging  decision-making  dilemma  for  the  marketer.
Marketing  manager  would  commit  a  type  I  error  if  he  rejects  the  null
hypothesis  (H0)  and  chooses  a  plastic  container  while  consumers’  mean
preferences for glass jars are actually no better. A type II error might involve
not  selecting  a  plastic  container  when  consumer  preference  is  actually
higher.

Both these errors are bad and should be reduced to a minimum. However,
they  cannot  be  completely  eliminated  without  looking  at  the  entire
population, in which case the testing process would be useless.  These two
errors, however, cancel each other out for a given sample size. This suggests
that if the testing process is made to make type I errors less likely to occur,
type II errors are also more likely to occur and vice versa. A good balance
between these two errors is the best that can be done.

The Significance Level

Since type I error is expected to be more significant than type II error in
all  tests  of  hypotheses,  the  likelihood  of  type  I  error  must  be  carefully
controlled. This is accomplished by defining a significance level at which a
test will be performed. Thus, the probability of type I error is constrained by
the significance level, and test procedures are created to obtain the lowest
probability of type II error subject to the significance level.

The sign α (read as alpha) is typically used to denote the probability of a
type I error, while the symbol β (read as beta) denotes the chance of a type
II error.

Develop Hypotheses

Variables  are  investigated  in  this  step  to  determine  their  influence  or

Null  and  alternative  hypotheses  are  formulated.  As  we  have  discussed
earlier in this unit that a null hypothesis is a statement of the status quo,
one of no difference or no effect. The null hypothesis is tested. If the null
hypothesis  is  not  rejected,  no  changes  will  be  made.  The  expectation  of
a  difference  or  impact  constitutes  an  alternate  hypothesis.  Changes  will
result if the alternative theory is accepted. As a result, the null hypothesis
and the alternative hypothesis are the opposite. In marketing research, the
null  hypothesis  is  constructed  in  a  way  that  if  it  is  rejected,  the  desired
conclusion will also be accepted.

we will not be able to test our hypotheses, until the variables in the theoretical

both  be  taken  into  account  by  the  researcher.  The  test  statistic  quantifies
the sample’s degree of agreement with the null hypothesis. A well-known

Decide the Level of Significance

There is a chance that we will come to the wrong conclusion whenever we
infer something about a population based on a sample. As we have discussed,
there are two types (Type 1 and Type 2) of errors that might occur. The level
of significance affects how confidently a researcher rejects or accepts a null
hypothesis. Prior to taking any samples, the level of significance indicated
by the symbol α is decided. The probability of rejecting the null hypothesis
when it is true is indicated by the level of significance α. Although the value
of α varies from problem to problem, it is typically taken to be either 5% or
1%. A 5 % level of significance means that there are 5 chances out of 100
that  a  null  hypothesis  will  get  rejected  when  it  should  be  accepted.  This
means that the researcher is 95 per cent confident that a right decision has
been taken.

Interpretation of Data

The results of the data analysis are interpreted in order to determine whether
or not our hypotheses are validated. Depending on whether the result of the

difference between the sample statistic and the population parameter that
is hypothesised.  Alternatively, if the hypothesis is accepted, the difference
between the sample statistic and the hypothesised population parameter is

7.7  SUMMARY

In  this  unit,  we  have  discussed  the  concept,  meaning,  importance,  and
process  of  hypothesis  testing.  The  true  benefit  of  hypothesis  testing  in
business is that it enables experts to validate their hypotheses before acting
on them. We discussed the stages of the hypothesis-testing process. The null
and alternative hypotheses are set up such that one of them, and only one of
them, is always true. In the absence of strong evidence to the contrary, the
decision-maker would be willing to accept the null hypothesis. Of the two
errors that can occur in any hypothesis testing, type I error, i.e., incorrectly
rejecting  the  null  hypothesis,  is  considered  to  be  more  serious  and  thus
is  subject  to  explicit  control.  All  tests  are  performed  at  a  significance
level, which defines the highest probability of a type I error. The unit was
concluded by illustrating the use of SPSS for hypothesis testing.

7.8  KEY WORDS

Alternative Hypothesis: is contrary to null hypothesis, which establishes a
statistically important relationship between two variables.

Hypothesis: is a statement that links two or more variables generated from
one or more research questions.

Level  of  Significance:    is  the  probability  of  wrong  elimination  of  null
hypothesis when in fact, it is true. It defines whether the null hypothesis is
assumed to be accepted or rejected.

Null Hypothesis: is a type of hypothesis that states that there is no difference
between two situations, groups, outcomes, or occurrence.

Type I Error: occurs when we incorrectly conclude that null hypothesis is
false when in fact it is true.

Type II Error: occurs when we incorrectly accept null hypothesis while it
is untrue.

8.1  INTRODUCTION

We frequently come into situations where data appears as pairs of figures
related to two variables. A correlation problem considers the joint variation
of  two  measurements  and  the  degree  of  the  association  between  two  or
more sets of variables, neither of which is restricted by the experimenter.
Examples  of  correlation  problems  include  the  study  of  the  relationship
between  IQ  and  the  total  percentage  of  marks  a  student  scored  in  a

146

 Data Analysiscompetitive  examination,  the  relationship  between  blood  pressure  and
metabolism, or the relationship between an individual’s height and weight.
In these examples, both variables are observed as they naturally occur, since
neither variable is fixed at predetermined levels.

Regression, on the other hand, considers the frequency distributions of one
variable  (called  the  dependent  variable)  when  another  (the  independent
variable) is held fixed at each of several levels. A regression problem is used
to explain the variations in one variable (called the dependent variable) by a
set of independent variables. Examples of regression problems can be found
in the study of the yields of crops grown with different amounts of fertiliser,
the lifespan of specific animals subjected to various levels of radiation, the
hardness of plastics that are heat-treated for different periods of time, and
so on. In these problems, the variation in one measurement is studied for
particular levels of the other variable selected by the experimenter.

Correlation  and  regression  analysis  are  generally  performed  together.
Understanding the concept and applications of correlation and regression
statistical methods will help you better comprehend the nature, direction,
and importance of the bivariate relationships between the variables used in
marketing research studies, such as advertising expenditure and sales, sales
volume at various pricing levels, and so on. This is a matter for discussion
in this unit.

8.6  PRACTICAL APPLICATIONS OF

CORRELATION

The  purpose  of  correlation  analysis  is  to  assess  how  closely  two  sets  of
quantitative data are related. For instance, what relationship exists between
the sales of products A and B? Or how does the advertising expense relate
to other promotional expenses? Practically every dataset with two or more
variables can be used to apply correlation analysis. A different question is
whether it makes sense to investigate a variable’s association with another
variable  and  how  to  interpret  the  results.  For  instance,  there  is  evidence
to  suggest  that  spending  more  money  on  clothing  of  greater  quality  is
correlated with income levels. However, one must be cautious of fictitious or

152

 Data Analysisabsurd correlations that might be discovered between completely unrelated
variables by pure accident. It is the duty of the researcher to ensure proper
application of correlation analysis. Therefore, the main goal of correlation
is to identify a relationship between any two random variables.

Correlation  and  regression  are  generally  performed  together.  In  many
applications,  correlation  is  usually  followed  by  regression  analysis.
Correlation  analysis  is  used  as  a  starting  point  for  selecting  useful
independent variables for regression analysis. For instance, a construction
company could identify factors like population, construction employment,
and building permits issued last year that it feels would affect its sales for
the  current  year. These  and  other  factors  that  may  be  identified  could  be
checked for mutual correlation by computing the correlation coefficient of
each pair of variables from the given historical data (this kind of analysis
is  easily  done  by  using  an  appropriate  computer  routine).  Only  variables
having  a  high  correlation  with  the  yearly  sales  could  be  singled  out  for
inclusion in a regression model.

Correlation  is  also  used  in  factor  analysis,  where  attempts  are  made  to
resolve  a  large  set  of  measured  variables  in  terms  of  relatively  few  new
categories, known as factors. The results could be useful in the following
three ways: 1) to reveal the underlying or latent factors that determine the
relationship  between  the  observed  data,  2)  to  make  evident  relationships
between data that had been obscured before such analysis, and 3) to provide
a classification scheme when data scored on various rating scales have to be
grouped together. Another major application of correlation is in forecasting
with the help of time series models.

In  this  unit,  the  concepts  of  correlation  and  simple  linear  regression
between two variables have been discussed. A scatter plot of the variables
may suggest that the two variables are related, but the value of the Pearson
correlation  coefficient  r  quantifies  this  association.  The  correlation
coefficient r may have values between -1 and 1. The sign indicates whether
the association is direct (+ve) or inverse (-ve). A numerical value of r equal
to  unity  indicates  perfect  association,  while  a  value  of  zero  indicates  no
association.  Tests  for  the  significance  of  the  correlation  coefficient  have
been described. Spearman’s rank correlation for data with ranks is outlined.

relationships,  and  to  overcome  this,  regression  analysis  is  used. We  have
discussed the estimation and interpretation of regression results. The use of
SPSS and applications of correlation and regression have also been highly
highlighted.

8.11 KEY WORDS

Correlation: Degree of association between two variables.

Correlation  Coefficient:  A  number  lying  between  -1  (Perfect  negative
correlation) and + 1 (perfect positive correlation) to quantify the association
between two variables.

Linear  regression:  Fitting  of  any  chosen  mathematical  model,  linear  in
unknown parameters, to a given data.

Regression:  Relating  of  a  dependent  (or  response)  variable  to  a  number
independent variables, based on a given set of data.

Scatter Diagram: An ungrouped plot of two variables, on the X and Y axes.

9.1  INTRODUCTION

The main objective of regression analysis is to explain the variation in one
variable  (called  the  dependent  variable)  based  on  the  variation  in  one  or
more  other  variables  (called  the  independent  variables).  The  number  of
independent  variables  in  a  regression  analysis  could  be  one  or  more.  In
the case of one independent variable, we classify it as a simple regression,
whereas in the case of more than one independent variable, it is called a
multiple  regression  analysis.  We  have  discussed  simple  linear  regression
with one independent variable in unit 8. In this unit, we will discuss multiple
regression analysis.

9.2  MULTIPLE REGRESSION ANALYSIS

In  general,  a  multivariate  technique  of  analysis  is  required  rather  than  a
univariate  or  bivariate  technique  when  there  are  two  or  more  variables
involved  in,  or  simultaneously  affecting,  another  variable.  For  instance,
using a multivariate method known as multiple regression, we can assess

162

the  simultaneous  impact  of  pricing,  sales  promotion,  and  advertising  on
sales.

Regression analysis finds out the degree or relationship between a dependent
variable and a set of independent variables by fitting a statistical equation
through  the  method  of  least  square.  Whenever  we  are  interested  in  the
combined  influence  of  several  independent  variables  upon  a  dependent
variable our study is that of multiple regression. For example, demand may
be influenced not only by price but also by growth in industrial production,
the extent of import prices of other goods, consumer’s income, taste, and
preferences etc. Marketing researchers could use regression for explaining
per cent variation in dependent variables caused by a number of independent
variables and also problems involving prediction or forecasting.

Regression analysis is probably the most widely applied technique among
the analytical models of association used in marketing research. Regression
analysis  attempts  to  study  the  relationship  between  a  dependent  variable
and a set of independent variables (one or more). For example, in demand
analysis, demand is inversely related to the price of normal commodities.
We may write D = A - BP, where D is the demand, which is the dependent
variable, and P is the unit price of the commodity, an independent variable.
This  is  an  example  of  a  simple  linear  regression  equation.  The  multiple
linear  regression  model  is  the  prototype  of  the  single  criterion/multiple
predictor association models, where we would like to study the combined
influence of several independent variables upon one dependent variable. If
P is the consumer price index and Q is the industrial production index in the
preceding example, we may be able to study demand as a function of two
independent variables, P and Q, and write

D = A - BP + C Q as a multiple linear regression model.

9.2.1 Objectives

The objectives of the marketing researchers in using regression analysis are:

1)   To  study  a  general  underlying  pattern  connecting  the  dependent
variable  and  independent  variables  by  establishing  a  functional
relationship  between  the  two.  In  this  equation,  the  degree  of
relationship is derived, which is a matter of interest to the researcher
in his study.

2)   To use the well-established regression equation for problems involving

prediction and forecasting.

3)   To  study  how  much  of  the  variation  in  the  dependent  variable  is
explained by the set of independent variables. This would enable him
to remove certain unwanted variables from the system. For example,
if 95% of the variation in demand in a study could be explained by
price and consumer rating index, the researcher may drop other factors
like industrial production, the extent of imports, substitution effect,
etc.,  which  may  contribute  to  only  5%  of  the  variation  in  demand,
provided all the causal variables are linearly independent.

9.5  CONSIDERATIONS FOR MULTIPLE

REGRESSION ANALYSIS

1)   The equation should be validated statistically.

2)   For  forecasting  the  dependent  variable,  the  independent  variables
should be forecast first. For example, if demand is a function of the
price index and production index established by a multiple regression
model, then to forecast demand for the next period, it is imperative first
to forecast the price index and production index and then substitute
them in the model to get the forecast for demand. This is one of the
limitations of regression forecasting.

3)   When  the  variables  become  too  many  the  analysis  is  complex  and
very  often  the  market  researcher  does  not  know  which  variables  to
retain. This problem could be overcome by doing stepwise regression
on computer. For example, if demand is a function of 20 variables,
we  first  fit  the  demand  equation  with  3  important  variables  which
we  think  affect  demand.  Suppose  R2  =  0.85,  that  is,  85%  of  the
variation in demand is explained by these variables, we add another
two more variables of importance to make five independent variables.
Now  if  R2  =  0.95  we  can  as  well  stop  adding  further  variables  as
the contribution may not appreciably improve the situation. We can
thus visualise demand as a function of just 5 variables. The various
permutations of changing and adding variables are possible only with
the help of a computer. The important point to remember is that the
cut-off points for the number of variables to be added should be based
on the increase every time you get on R2. The moment the increase is
marginal, stop adding variables.

4)

If the independent variables among themselves are highly correlated,
then we are facing the problem of ‘multicollinearity’. Normally we
say that the partial regression coefficient with respect to X1 implies
change in Y per unit change in X1 provided X2, X3 .... are held constant.
This  poses  a  serious  problem  if  there  is  multicollinearity.  One  way
to  overcome  multicollinearity  is  to  drop  certain  variables  from  the
model if the corresponding standard error of the regression coefficient
is  unduly  large. Another  method  is  to  see  whether  the  original  set
could be transformed into another linear composite so that the new
variables are uncorrelated.

10.2 DISCRIMINANT ANALYSIS

Market researchers often need to understand how groups of customers or
items differ in terms of a set of explanatory or independent variables. For
this purpose, market researchers often use discriminant analysis to assign
an object (a customer or an item) to one group among the number of known
groups. Discriminant analysis is a useful tool for situations where the total
sample is to be divided into two or more mutually exclusive and collectively
exhaustive groups on the basis of a set of predictor variables. For example,
a problem involving classifying salespeople as “high performers” or “low
performers,” classifying customers as “loyal” or “not loyal,” and so on.

Objectives of two group discriminant analysis:

1)   Finding  linear  composites  of  the  predictor  variable  that  enable  the
analyst to separate the groups by maximising among groups relative
to within groups variation.

2)   Establishing procedures for assigning new individuals, whose profiles

but not group identity are known, to one of the two groups.

3)   Testing  whether  significant  differences  exist  between  the  mean

predictor variable profiles of the two groups.

4)   Determining which variables account most for intergroup differences

in mean profiles.

The  Omnibus  test  is  a  likelihood  ratio  chi-square  test  performed  to
determine the overall fit of the model relative to the null model. The
Omnibus tests of model coefficients are used to check that the new
model (with explanatory variables included) is an improvement over
the baseline model (without any explanatory variables, consisting of
the only constant term). It uses the chi-square test to see if there is
a significant difference between the log-likelihoods (specifically the
-2LLs) of the baseline model and the new model. If the p-value is less
than 0.05, then we have a new model that is significantly better than
the baseline model and should be further interpreted. The table below

11.1 INTRODUCTION

In the marketing domain, when collecting the data through survey methods,
the number of items is generally very large. Many of the items represent
the  same  concepts  or  constructs.  Analyzing  each  of  the  items  and  their

193

causal  relationships  is  extremely  time-consuming  and  may  not  give  the
desired  meaningful  insights  to  aid  in  the  decision-making  process. Thus,
before  doing  any  statistical  modelling,  there  is  a  need  to  reduce  the  data
into  meaningful  variables.  For  this,  marketers  use  factor  analysis.  Factor
analysis  provides  an  approach  that  reduces  a  set  of  variables  into  one  or
more underlying variables. The technique groups together those variables
that  seem  to  belong  together  and  simultaneously  supplies  the  weighing
scheme. For example, one may be interested in the identification of factors
that determine the company’s image.

Market  segmentation  is  another  very  important  decision-making  task  for
a marketing manager. One of the crucial tasks of a marketing manager is
to select a target market for a given product or service and formulate the
marketing  mix  strategy  specific  to  that  target  market.  Cluster  analysis  is
a very powerful technique for segmenting the market. It is an exploratory
technique used to group individuals or objects into clusters so that objects
in the same cluster are more similar to one another than they are to objects
in other clusters. The objective is to maximize the homogeneity of objects
within  the  clusters  while  also  maximizing  the  heterogeneity  between  the
clusters. In most of the analytical projects in marketing, cluster analysis is
often carried out before predictive or other statistical or analytical modelling.

In this unit, we will discuss the concept, methodology, applications, benefits,
and limitations of factor analysis and cluster analysis.

11.2  FACTOR ANALYSIS

Factor analysis is the generic name given to a class of techniques whose
purpose is data reduction and summarization. Very often, market researchers
are  overwhelmed  by  the  plethora  of  data.  Factor  analysis  comes  to  their
rescue by reducing the number of variables. Factor analysis does not entail
partitioning  the  data  matrix  into  criterion  and  predictor  subsets;  rather,
interest is centred on relationships involving the whole set of variables. In
factor analysis:

1)   The  analyst  is  interested  in  examining  the  “strength”  of  the  overall
association among variables in the sense that he would like to account
for this association in terms of a smaller set of linear composites of
the original variables that preserve most of the information in the full
data set. Often, his interest will emphasise the description of the data
rather than statistical inference.

2)   No attempt is made to divide the variables into criterion and prediction

sets.

3)   The models are primarily based on linear relationships.

Factor  analysis  is  a  “search”  technique.  Typically,  the  researcher-
decision maker lacks a clear priori structure for the number of factors
to be identified. Cut-off points with respect to stopping rules for the
analysis are often determined ad hoc as the output becomes available.
Even where the procedures and rules are stipulated in advance, the
results are more descriptive than inferential.

Factor  analysis  provides  the  tools  for  marketers  to  analyse  the  structure
of the interrelationships (correlations) among a large number of variables

of  variables  that  are  highly  interrelated,  known  as  factors.  These  groups

11.5  APPLICATIONS OF FACTOR ANALYSIS

In marketing, factor analysis could be used for any of the following purpose:

Explore the data patterns:

Factor analysis can be done to explore the data and reveal patterns among
the  interrelationships  of  the  items.  It  can  bring  out  the  hidden  or  latent
dimensions relevant to the study.

Data Reduction:

Factor  analysis  is  extremely  useful  to  reduce  a  large  number  of  items  or
variables to a smaller number of factors for statistical modelling purposes,
which  is  otherwise  difficult  to  model. As  a  result,  factor  analysis  can  be
combined  with  other  multivariate  analysis  techniques,  such  as  cluster
analysis, conjoint analysis, SEM (structure equation modeling), and so on,
to aid in the development of the latent variables model.

Confirms Hypothesis of Factor structure:

Factor  analysis  can  be  used  to  test  whether  a  set  of  items  designed  to
measure  certain  variables  in  fact  reveal  the  hypothesized  factor  structure
(i.e., whether the underlying latent factor truly causes the variance in the
observed variables and how certain we can be about it).

Theory Testing

Factor analysis can be used to test an a priori hypothesis about the relationship
among a set of observed variables; for this, generally confirmatory factor
analysis is used.

11.7  CLUSTER ANALYSIS

Cluster analysis is a technique that is used to segment a market. The objective
is to find a group of customers in the market that are homogeneous, i.e., they
share some characteristics so that they can be classified into one group. The
cluster or group so identified should be large enough so that the company can
develop it profitably, as the ultimate objective of a company is to serve the
customer and earn profits. The group of customers that the company hopes
to serve should be large enough for the company to make it a financially
viable proposition. This is also true for the customer, who will not pay more
than a certain price for a particular product (price, of course, is a function of
product positioning, cost of production, and so on).

As  an  example,  let  us  consider  the  watch  industry. There  could  be  many
ways in which the watch industry could be segmented, which are as follows:

a)  Gender (Male/Female)

b)

Technology (Digital/Analog)

c)  Design Features

d)  Occasion of Use (Formal/Casual/Party)

e)

Price (Low/Medium/High/Jewellery)

Some of the above segmentation factors are demographic (price, gender),
whereas others are psychographic (occasion of use).

This,  therefore,  presents  a  problem  to  the  market  researcher  or  company
as to how to identify combinations of factors that can be used to segment
the market. It is not always possible to segment a market on the basis of
one single factor. Thus, a combination of factors must be used to segment
the  market. And  this  is  where  the  Cluster Analysis  technique  specifically
deals with how objects (people, places, and products) should be assigned
to groups so that there should be similarity within the groups and as much
difference between the groups as possible.

11.9 DIFFERENT APPROACHES TO CLUSTER

ANALYSIS

There are various approaches adopted for cluster analysis.

One  extreme  approach  is  to  cluster  respondents  in  terms  of  all  available
information  about  the  respondent;  although  this  is  very  thorough,  it  may
lead to difficulty in interpretation and marked differences between groups,
i.e., the clusters or groups of respondents that are produced may be a hotch-
potch rather than a clearly marked description of the target population.

Another  extreme  approach  involves  grouping  respondents  only  based  on
data  that  directly  represents  the  specific  consumer  need. As  an  example,
consider the product’s quality and price. This has the advantage of clarity
and accuracy, but may be quite limited in describing the target population.

The way one approaches cluster analysis depends upon the objective of the
study. Suppose the objective of the company is the physical development of
an existing product, then the consumers’ needs regarding the product are the
most suitable variables for forming clusters.

Suppose an advertisement for a product is to be developed, then the attitude
of the consumer towards the product gains more importance, as there are
groups of people who have a positive, negative, or neutral attitude towards
the product or company. And, depending on the product or communication
need,  the  developed  advertisement  would  target  specific  clusters  of
consumers.

Suppose  a  company  wishes  to  develop  a  new  product  concept  or  make
product changes in the existing product offering; then, the attitude of the
consumer towards the product, company, or product category gains more
importance over other attributes when developing clusters.

11.10 FACTORS TO BE CONSIDERED WHILE
USING CLUSTER ANALYSIS TECHNIQUE

The  following  aspects  should  be  kept  in  mind  while  using  the  cluster
analysis method:

a).   A number of clusters may emerge after the analysis. However, there
is a limit to the number of clusters that a company can consider due
to- limitation of market potential within a cluster, and the distinction
between clusters is not clearly defined.

b).   Cluster  analysis  provides  a  way  of  segmenting  the  market,  but
these  segments  are  not  watertight  compartments.  Products  that  are
developed  for  a  particular  segment  may  attract  people  from  other
segments too.

c).   The characteristics of a cluster may change over time as the consumers’
economic status, education, lifestyle, etc. change. Then the company
has to take another look at the market.

d).   The  clusters  that  have  been  identified  are  used  to  develop  further
marketing strategies in areas like product developments, advertising
research, distribution strategies, pricing strategies, etc.

e).   The  most  important  assumption  in  cluster  analysis  is  that  the  basic
measure of similarity on which clustering is based is a valid measure
of  the  similarity  between  objects.  A  second  major  assumption  is
that  clustering  objects  is  theoretically  justified  and  supported.  As,
with other multivariate techniques, there should be theory and logic
underlying the cluster analysis.

f).   The major limitation of cluster analysis is the difficulty in evaluating
the quality of the clusters. It is very difficult to know exactly which
clusters are very similar and which objects are dissimilar, and it is also
difficult to select clustering criteria.

12.1  INTRODUCTION

In  marketing,  the  need  to  understand  consumer  behaviour  is  paramount.
Although consumer behaviour has many facets that have to be understood,
the need to discover the attributes that the consumer considers important is
quite common. Following an understanding of these attributes, marketers

attempt to comprehend consumers’ perceptions of the products or brands in
relation to these critical attributes. Many research and analytical techniques
are available and often employed for these purposes. In this unit, we will
discuss two multivariate techniques: conjoint analysis and multidimensional
scaling (MDS).

When the marketer is interested in getting answers to several “What if?”
kind of questions. What if I reduce one attribute? What if the competitor
changes its brand position? What if the salience of one attribute changed
through  some  consumer  education  efforts?  etc.  Most  of  these  questions
are  answered  by  the  conjoint  analysis  technique,  which  also  guides  the
marketer in optimising his decisions. It helps him understand the trade-offs
that consumers make between different attributes. In fact, it is with these
trade-offs that the marketer finds his greatest opportunities to optimise his
marketing efforts.

MDS is found to be superior to more traditional methods of attribute-wise
data collection because of its lesser demands on data scaling. It can work
with metrics as well as ranking data. This feature is also very consumer-
friendly, as consumers, in most cases, compare products in terms of “better”
or “worse” instead of assigning absolute values to products or their attributes.
With the help of MDS techniques, marketers can understand the significant
attributes of products or services and obtain the plots of competing products
or brands in the perceptual space of consumers. These details go a long way
toward helping marketers make product positioning decisions.

12.2 WHAT IS CONJOINT ANALYSIS

Conjoint analysis is essentially a data decomposition technique that attempts
to plot the output data in the joint space of each attribute’s importance. It
seeks data from the consumers in the form of their overall response to the
totality of products (or their descriptions), while the output is in terms of
the scores that the consumer has implicitly assigned to each of the attributes
and their levels.

The important thing to note is that the consumer is not asked to assign scores
to different attributes separately. In fact, the consumer is presented with the
stimulus in the form of the totality of the product, as in the case of MDS.
However, there is one difference between MDS and conjoint analysis. In
conjoint analysis, the stimuli are created by the researcher himself. while in
the case of MDS, already existing products or brands are used.

Conjoint analysis derives the importance weights (referred to as “part-worth
utilities”) assigned by each consumer to respective levels of attributes in a
way that is directly comparable. This feature of the technique allows for the
determination  of  the  trade-offs  that  consumers  make  in  their  minds.  The
relative  importance  of  the  attributes  can  also  be  derived  from  the  output
of  “part-worth  utilities.”  Thus,  starting  with  very  simple  input  data  (just
the  ranking  of  some  predesigned  product  alternatives),  conjoint  analysis
provides the part-worth utilities for each of the product attribute levels for
every consumer individually.

consideration. For example, a soap can technically be produced in all

attributes, such as price or product life, may have continuous values.
Conjoint analysis can treat them as well. Some range of values may

6.  Determination of part worth utilities

The rating or ranking data obtained from the consumers is analysed
next. Two methods are more popular for this purpose. In one method,
the  part  worth  utilities  for  each  of  the  levels  of  each  attribute  is
arbitrarily assigned. Based on these assumed values, the consumer’s
overall  rating  or  ranking  (as  the  case  may  be)  is  estimated.  These
estimated responses may, understandably, be quite different from the
actual data. After a few iterations, convergence is achieved, so that the
part-worth utilities found approximate the estimate responses to the
actual data the best.

In the alternative method, the part worth utilities are derived in one
step.  Here,  an  error  function  describing  the  difference  between  the
estimated and actual data is defined. This function is then minimised.

After using any of the available methods, the output is obtained for each
respondent separately. This is quite significant, as the disaggregated
data can be combined in any way desired. But if the output was only at
the aggregate level, then disaggregation might not have been possible.

7.  Conjoint Analysis applications

Calculating  the  part-worth  utilities  is  only  the  beginning  of  many
interesting  applications  of  conjoint  analysis.  In  marketing,  conjoint
analysis has wide application. Most often, a marketing manager needs
to  understand  how  a  customer  makes  a  purchase  decision  and  how
they  evaluate  various  tangible  and  intangible  attributes  associated
with  a  particular  product.  For  example,  while  making  a  purchase
decision for a smart phone, the customer makes judgments about the
product based on the combination of the brand name, price of different
models, internet connectivity, screen size, embedded memory, ability
to sync multiple email accounts, etc. The main objective of conjoint
analysis is to find the most preferred attribute of the product.

Let us explain some important marketing applications of conjoint analysis.

i.  Optimum Product Design

Since  all  possible  product  concepts  can  be  compared  after  adding
their respective attribute levels and part-worth utilities, it is possible
to determine the demand for different products out of any given set
of available products in the marketplace. The demand levels can be
converted into profit figures as the cost of producing and marketing can
also be calculated. These cost calculations are possible as the volume
of operations and the features of the products are now known. As a
result, the best product can be chosen based on profits (or any other
given management goal). Customers’ differential rates of purchase of
products are also duly considered at this stage.

Quite  often,  a  manager  will  want  to  know  the  effects  of  a  minor
change  in  any  of  his  or  her  company’s  or  a  competitor’s  attributes.
Conjoint Analysis facilitates this type of “What if” analysis with its
data base of part-worth utilities. In fact, different kinds of scenarios

12.6 ATTRIBUTE SPACE - OBJECTIVE SPACE

AND PERCEIVED SPACE

Any  product  or  service  can  be  visualized  as  composed  of  both  objective
and perceived (subjective) attributes, or “dimensions.” A company has an
objective description of its product in physical or chemical terms. Such an
objective attribute as “space,” in which various brands are viewed as points
positioned in space, will usually not agree with the buyer’s perception of
the products. The buyer’s perception of a product, service, or company is
a  sum  total  of  not  only  the  company  that  is  selling  the  product,  but  also
other factors in the market like competition and the products of competitors.
advertising, after-sales service, etc.

Also  note  that  the  dimensions  of  perceived  space  need  not  be  the  same
as  those  of  objective  space.  As  an  example,  for  many  consumer  goods
that are highly individualistic, like tea, coffee, toothpastes, perfumes, and
shampoos, where strong individual preferences play a key role in buying
decisions,  the  objective  space  of  the  company  and  the  perceived  space
of the consumer may be widely different. But in the case of an industrial
product,  the  perceived  configuration  of  brands  may  agree  rather  closely
with  an  objectively  constructed  configuration  in  which  measurements  of
such characteristics as speed, reliability, and efficiency can be made rather

Ultimately, the view of the consumer is the most relevant one, as this is what
would make the consumer go out and buy a particular product or brand.

12.10 SUMMARY

In this unit, we have discussed two of the techniques of multivariate analysis,
viz., conjoint analysis and Multidimensional Scaling (MDS).

Conjoint Analysis is essentially a data decomposition technique that attempts
to plot the output data on the joint space of each attribute’s importance. The
important point to note is that the consumer is not asked to assign scores to
different attributes separately. The main steps in using conjoint analysis are
determining the salient attributes for the given product from the consumer’s
perspective, assigning a set of discrete levels or a range of continuous values
to  each  of  the  attributes,  using  a  fraction  factorial  design  of  experiment
to design the stimuli for the experiment, physically designing the stimuli,
data collection, conjoint analysis, and determining part-worth utilities. The
possible  applications  of  conjoint  analysis  include  product  design,  market
segmentation, SWOT analysis, etc.

We also discussed the multidimensional scaling technique, also known as the
perceptual map or positioning map, which is a multi-variate technique that
is  particularly  suitable  for  measuring  human  perceptions  and  preferences
for  products  and  brands.  Its  applications  include  market  segmentation,
positioning studies, test marketing, etc.

Choice  set:  Set  of  profiles  constructed  through  experimental  design
principles and used in the choice-based conjoint approach.

Conjoint  analysis:    is  essentially  a  data  decomposition  technique  that
attempts  to  plot  the  output  data  in  the  joint  space  of  each  attribute’s
importance.

Level:  Specific  nonmetric  value  describing  a  factor.  Each  factor  must  be
represented by two or more levels, but the number of levels typically never
exceeds four or five.

Multidimensional Scaling (MDS): also known as the perceptual map or
positioning  map,  which  is  a  multi-variate  technique  that  is  particularly
suitable for measuring human perceptions and preferences for products and
brands

Part-Worth: Estimate from conjoint analysis of the overall preference or
utility associated with each level of each factor used to define the product
or service.

Trade-off analysis Method of presenting profiles to respondents in which
factors  (attributes)  are  depicted  two  at  a  time  and  respondents  rank  all
combinations of the levels in terms of preference.

Utility:   An  individual’s  subjective  preference  judgment  representing  the
holistic value or worth of a specific object.
