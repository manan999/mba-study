### UNIT 4: METHODS AND TECHNIQUES OF DATA COLLECTION

#### 1. Primary vs. Secondary Data
*   **Primary Data:** Data collected afresh and for the first time by the researcher, making it original in character.
*   **Secondary Data:** Data that has already been collected and processed by some other agency or organization for their own purposes.
*   **Context Dependency Metaphor:**
    *   *The Census Bureau* collects census figures; for them, it is **Primary Data**.
    *   *A Demographer* uses those published census figures to build a mortality table; for them, it is **Secondary Data**.

#### 2. Comparison of Primary Data Collection Methods
Different situations, budgets, and timelines demand different methods:

| Method | Description | Merits | Demerits | Preferred When |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Observation** | The investigator collects information personally through direct observation without questioning. | Objective, free from respondent bias, records actual behavior. | High investigator bias, limited scope, expensive, no insight into attitudes. | Natural behavior studies (e.g., traffic counts, shelf visits). |
| **Personal Interview** | Face-to-face questioning using a structured or unstructured questionnaire. | Most flexible, high response rate, permits deep probing and observation. | Most expensive, requires heavy supervision, prone to interviewer bias. | Complex topics requiring high accuracy and depth. |
| **Mailed Questionnaire** | Questionnaire sent via post/email to a list of literate respondents. | Inexpensive, wide geographic coverage, no interviewer bias, anonymous. | Highest non-response rate, slowest, no control over who answers, no probing. | Restricted funds, ample time, educated target population. |
| **Telephone Interview** | Questioning respondents over the phone. | Quickest, low cost, relatively high response rate, easy to supervise. | Short interview duration, excludes non-phone owners, no visual aids. | Quick, simple feedback on a few items under restricted time. |

#### 3. Secondary Data: Sources, Dangers, and Precautions
*   **Main Sources:** Government publications (e.g., RBI Bulletins, Census reports), trade journals, books, institutional records, and commercial databases.
*   **Dangers Involved in Using Secondary Data:**
    *   *Bias:* The original agency might have collected the data with a loaded objective.
    *   *Outdated:* The data might reflect a past scenario that is no longer valid.
    *   *Incompatibility:* The definitions, class intervals, or units of measurement might not match the current study.
    *   *Inaccuracy:* The original study might have suffered from high sampling/non-sampling errors.
*   **Precautions & Scrutiny (The Three Pillars):**
    1.  **Reliability:** Who collected the data? Was the methodology sound and unbiased?
    2.  **Suitability:** Does the data match the scope, definitions, and objectives of the current research?
    3.  **Adequacy:** Is the data detailed enough, or is it too highly aggregated to be useful?

### UNIT 5: ATTITUDE MEASUREMENT AND SCALES

#### 1. Concept of Attitude & Attitude Measurement
*   **Attitude:** A mental state of readiness, organized through experience, exerting an influence upon an individual’s response to objects and situations. It is multi-dimensional (cognitive, affective, behavioral).
*   **Attitude Measurement:** The process of assigning quantitative values to qualitative feelings, preferences, and opinions to make them analytically viable.

#### 2. The Four Levels of Measurement Scales
1.  **Nominal Scale:** Categorization or labeling without numerical value or order (e.g., Gender: Male/Female, Religion). Only mode can be calculated.
2.  **Ordinal Scale:** Categorization with a relative order or rank, but the intervals between ranks are unequal or unknown (e.g., Class ranks: 1st, 2nd, 3rd; Brand preferences). Median can be calculated.
3.  **Interval Scale:** Ranks with equal, known distances between points, but has an arbitrary zero point (e.g., Temperature in Celsius, IQ scores). Mean can be calculated.
4.  **Ratio Scale:** Ranks with equal intervals and a true, absolute zero point (e.g., Weight, Income, Age, Sales volume). All statistical analyses are permitted.

#### 3. Essential Scaling Techniques in Research
*   **Thurstone's Equal-Appearing Interval Scale:**
    *   *Method:* The researcher generates a large pool of attitude statements. A panel of "judges" is asked to sort these statements into 11 piles representing a continuum from *most unfavorable* to *most favorable*.
    *   *Score:* The scale value of each item is its median rating by judges. Ambiguous items (high standard deviation among judges) are discarded. Respondents check only the statements they agree with. Their score is the median/average scale value of endorsed items.
    *   *Drawbacks:* Time-consuming, judges' own attitudes can bias the scale positions, and it doesn't measure the intensity of agreement.
*   **Semantic Differential Scale:**
    *   *Method:* An attitude scale anchored by bipolar adjectives (e.g., Good vs. Bad, Fast vs. Slow) on a 7-point rating scale. Only the extremes are labeled.
    *   *Use:* Highly popular for brand image profiling. By plotting the average scores of respondents, researchers generate an "image profile" to compare competing brands.
*   **Likert Scale (Summative Model):**
    *   *Method:* Statements are rated on an intensity scale (typically 5-point: Strongly Agree to Strongly Disagree). Positive items are scored 5 to 1; negative items are reversed (1 to 5).
    *   *Item Analysis:* A critical step where the scale is pre-tested. Responses are sorted into high and low scorers. Statements that fail to statistically differentiate between the high and low scoring groups are dropped.
*   **Q-Sort Technique:**
    *   *Method:* Respondents sort a large number of cards (containing statements/brands) into specified piles representing a normal distribution (e.g., placing 2 cards in "most liked", 5 in "liked", etc.).
    *   *Use:* Faster and less tedious than paired comparisons. Used for intensive study of individual preferences and clustering individuals.
*   **Multidimensional Scaling (MDS):**
    *   *Method:* A group of mathematical techniques that map objects as points in a multi-dimensional space based on their similarities or preferences.
    *   *Use:* Product positioning and market segmentation (e.g., visual mapping of how consumers perceive competing car brands).

### UNIT 6: QUESTIONNAIRE DESIGNING

#### 1. The Core Considerations in Questionnaire Construction
A questionnaire is the most popular instrument of primary data collection, but its design is an art. It has three main guiding pillars:
1.  **The General Form:** Choosing the appropriate level of structure. Highly structured questionnaires are easy to analyze but rigid; unstructured questionnaires are excellent for deep qualitative probing but require skilled interviewers.
2.  **The Question Sequence:** Building a logical flow.
    *   *The Funnel Approach:* Start with interesting, easy, and broad questions to build rapport. Move to specific, substantive questions.
    *   *Relegating Embarrassing Questions:* Personal details (income, age, personal habits) must be placed at the very end to prevent premature termination of the interview.
3.  **The Question Wording:** Eliminating bias and ambiguity.
    *   Questions must be simple, concrete, and easily understood.
    *   Avoid double-barreled questions (asking two things in one question: *"Do you like the price and quality of Brand X?"*).
    *   Avoid leading questions (*"Don't you agree that our service is excellent?"*).

#### 3. The Role of a Pilot Study
*   **What is it?** A mini-run or trial administration of the questionnaire on a small, representative sample of the target population.
*   **Why is it used?** To detect structural flaws, ambiguous wordings, awkward question flows, and layout issues. It allows the researcher to edit the instrument before investing heavily in the main fieldwork.

### UNIT 7: SAMPLING AND SAMPLING DESIGNS

#### 1. Basic Concepts
*   **Universe/Population:** The aggregate of all elements under study.
*   **Sampling Frame:** The physical list or directory of all sampling units in the population (e.g., telephone directory, voter list).
*   **Sample:** A representative subset of the population selected for observation and analysis.
*   **Advantages of Sampling over Census:**
    *   *Lower Cost:* Enumerating a whole population is financially prohibitive.
    *   *Greater Speed:* Data collection and processing are completed faster.
    *   *Destructive Testing:* In cases like blood tests or checking product shelf-life, sampling is the only logical choice.
    *   *Higher Accuracy:* Smaller operations allow for better training, supervision, and less non-sampling error.

#### 2. Random (Probability) vs. Non-Random (Non-Probability) Sampling
*   **Random Sampling:** Every unit in the population has a known, non-zero chance of being selected. Enables the calculation of sampling error and statistical generalization.
*   **Non-Random Sampling:** Selection is based on convenience, judgment, or quotas. Generalization is limited, and sampling error cannot be measured mathematically.

#### 3. Probability Sampling Methods
1.  **Simple Random Sampling (SRS):** Every unit has an equal chance of selection (e.g., lottery method, random number table). Requires a comprehensive sampling frame.
2.  **Stratified Random Sampling:** The population is divided into homogenous subgroups (strata) based on a characteristic (e.g., Income: Low, Middle, High), and SRS is applied within each stratum. Proportional stratification maintains population ratios.
3.  **Systematic Sampling:** Selecting every $k$-th element from a listed population frame after starting at a random point.
4.  **Cluster & Multi-stage Sampling:** Dividing the population into heterogeneous groups (clusters, e.g., city blocks), selecting a sample of clusters, and then either surveying all units within selected clusters (Cluster) or subsampling within them in stages (Multi-stage).

#### 4. Non-Probability Sampling Methods
1.  **Quota Sampling:** The population is segmented (like stratified sampling), but the selection of units within each segment is left to the convenience or judgment of the interviewer.
2.  **Judgment (Purposive) Sampling:** The researcher uses their personal expertise to handpick units that they believe are highly representative of the population.
3.  **Convenience Sampling:** Selecting units that are easiest to reach (e.g., mall intercepts).
4.  **Snowball Sampling:** Asking initial respondents to identify others who fit the criteria; highly useful for rare or hard-to-reach populations.
*   **When is Non-Random Sampling Suitable?**
    *   In **exploratory studies** where high precision is not required.
    *   When **time and funds are severely restricted**.
    *   When a comprehensive **sampling frame is unavailable**.
    *   During **pilot-testing** of questionnaires.

#### 5. Case Application: Sampling Design for Domestic Airline Passengers
To design a survey investigating the problems of domestic airline passengers, the sampling design parameters would involve:
*   **The Target Population:** All passengers flying domestic routes within a country during a specified time frame (e.g., 3 months).
*   **The Sampling Unit:** An individual passenger.
*   **The Sampling Frame:** Since airline passenger manifests are highly confidential, a static frame is unavailable. Instead, a **time-space frame** is established (e.g., flight schedules, gates).
*   **Sampling Method Recommended:**
    *   *First Stage (Stratification):* Divide flights into strata based on airline, time of day (morning, afternoon, night), and route distance (short vs. long-haul).
    *   *Second Stage (Systematic Sampling):* Select passenger seats systematically (e.g., every 5th passenger boarding at selected gates) to eliminate interviewer selection bias.
*   **Sample Size:** Determined by balancing the desired level of precision (e.g., within 3% margin of error) with budget and gate access constraints.
