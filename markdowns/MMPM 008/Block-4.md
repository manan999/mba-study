12.2 DYNAMICS  OF  PHYSICAL  DISTRIBUTION

There are many different independent variables involved in the physical distribution
operation. Stock and Lambert (1987) mentioned the following:

Customer Service: The efficiency with which time and place utilities are created
translatesto customer service. The cost of distribution, market share, and profitability
are all directly influenced by the supplier’s level of customer service.

Order Processing: Order processing triggers the logistics process and directs activities
necessary  to  deliver  products  to  the  customer.  Speed  and  accuracy  of  order
processing affect costs and customer service levels.

Logistics Communication: Information is exchanged in the distribution process
in order to guide the activities of the system. It is a vital link between the firm’s
logistics system and its customers.

Transportation: The physical movement of products from the source of supply
and production to customers is the most significant cost area in logistics, and it involves
selecting modes and specific carriers as well as routing.

Warehousing: Providing storage space serves as a buffer between production and
use. Warehousing may be used to enhance service and lower transportation costs.

Inventory Control: Inventory is used to make products available to customers
and to ensure the correct mix of products at the proper location at the right time.

Packaging: The role of packaging is to provide protection to the products, maintain
product identity throughout the logistics process, and create effective product density.

Material Handling: Efficient material handling increases the speedand reduces the
cost of picking orders in the warehouse and moving products between storage and
transportation carriers. It is a cost-generating activity, and that must be controlled.

Production Planning: Used in conjunction with logistics planning, production planning
ensures that products are available for inventory in the correct assortment and quantity.

Warehouse Locations: Strategic placement of warehouses increases customer
service and reduces the cost of transportation.

The efficiency of an individual function, examined in isolation, may be quite different
from the effectiveness of the same function considered as part of the total physical
distribution process. Therefore, in order to improve the cost-efficiency balance of
the system as a whole, it is imperative that compromises be made among the functions.

Cutting costs while at the same time improving efficiency is the primary aim of
organising a physical distribution system. Costs have to be cut, but within the
constraints of uncontrollable variables (the rural environment). Cutting costs may
have an impact on delivery speed, reliability, and service. Costs will have to be
reduced, but the process should not result in these undesirable consequences. It is
necessary to evolve and implement a physical distribution system that is best suited
to the rural environment, with the object being minimization of distribution cost as
well as guaranteeing a minimum desired level of service.

12.5   RURAL  ROAD  DEVELOPMENT

A crucial pre-requisite for the development of rural markets is a network of roads
to connect the villages to towns and to transport products to rural households. India
has the second-largest road network in the world, which may not come as a surprise
given that it is the second-largest nation in the world by population alone. Our country’s
road network is similar to the veins and arteries because road transportation is so
common and essential for daily activities. Nearly 90% of Indians commute and travel
on the country’s road network, and 65% of all goods are moved and carried by
road, making it our main mode of transportation. This network consists of main
district roads, rural roads, state highways, and national highways. This also includes
local roads and state highways.

Indian government has been placing an increasing amount of emphasis on rural roads
as they have grown in importance over the years. This is due to the fact that rural
roads are just as significant as national highways in terms of the economic benefits
they provide. Actually, it’s common to think of rural roads as the villages’ lifelines.
They are crucial for reducing rural poverty, facilitating the movement of people,
commodities, and resources, encouraging the diversity of agricultural practises, and
fostering both rural and national economic growth.Rural roads are therefore essential
to the development of the nation. For the rural poor, improved earning opportunities
are specifically attributable to rural roads. Let’s highlight some of the most significant
benefits of developing rural roads:

1. Provides improved transportation services and access: By establishing
connections, rural roads considerably contribute to the accessibility of goods
and services in neighbouring townships, large cities, or markets. This means
that greater transportation services can enable better availability of inputs
and raw materials at reduced rates, which can ensure better access to market
centres for rural producers. This can significantly affect the economic potential
of the rural poor. Simply said, rural farmers’ ability to go to markets to
sell their goods or purchase raw materials to improve production will allow
them to significantly boost their income.

2.

Increases productivity and agricultural activities: Sustaining agricultural
development also depends on rural roads. By ensuring that the farmer receives

202

the water, seeds, and other agricultural inputs they require on schedule, a
robust network of rural roads helps improve agricultural activity. If the quality
of agricultural products is high, farmers will be able to sell more of these
high-quality products at a higher price. Profits may rise as a result of this.
By enhancing the income of impoverished households, increased agricultural
output can not only reduce rural poverty directly, but it can also do so
indirectly by increasing agricultural incomes and lowering food prices.

3. Ensures diversification of agricultural operations: The development of cash
crops and the commercialization of agricultural activities can both benefit
from the rural road network. In order to improve their yield for market
sale, people in rural areas can now employ cutting-edge farming methods.

4.

Increases labour and material mobility and reduces travel time: The presence
of roads in rural areas expands the range of rural economic activity beyond
the limits of rural production. Rural roads typically allow for quicker travel
times, and the time saved can enable the rural poor be more productive
and earn more money. Farmers might use the time saved to work other
non-farm jobs to supplement their income. As a result, roads in rural areas
make it easier for non-farm economic activity to coexist with farm activities,
which improves the quality of life for rural residents.

5. Opens up additional opportunities for livelihood: As more non-agricultural
revenue options are created by rural roads; this can also result in changes
to the sources of income. Simply said, greater road connectivity in rural
areas can expand off-farm employment options by allowing people to move
freely between locations. The increased mobility will especially help women,
laborers, and small contractors leave the villages for the neighbouring
economic hubs in search of higher paying jobs.

6. Provides access to education:  Access to education services for the rural
populace can be made easier with improved road connectivity. They can
travel to the nearby towns and cities to pursue greater education, which
may result in more favourable work prospects for them. In this way, rural
poor people can provide a better life for their families.

7. Provides socioeconomic advantages: The socioeconomic advantage of rural
road building and upgrading is that they can raise rural residents’ asset values,
facilitating trade and commercial opportunities.

Thus, rural road connectivity and sustained availability are key components of rural
development, as they assure continuing access to economic and social services and
thereby generate a sustained increase in agricultural incomes and productive
employment opportunities. It is also, as a result, a vital ingredient in ensuring sustainable
poverty reduction, which demands permanent rural connectivity. Rural roads influence
the process of growth by facilitating the dispersal of knowledge and the reduction
of inequalities. They act as infrastructure multipliers, reduce poverty, and have a
close synergy with rural livelihood outcomes such as increased incomes (e.g., tradable
agricultural surplus, material goods, and cash).

12.9 SEASONAL  DEMAND AND  DISTRIBUTION

IMPLICATIONS

The distribution of any product in rural areas, whether agricultural inputs, consumables,
or durables, necessarily follows a seasonal pattern. Since 75 percent of rural income

207

Accessing  Rural Markets

is generated through agricultural operations, which are seasonal, the demand pattern
is also seasonal. A typical example is that of fertilizers. The demand for fertiliser is
always high during the start of the Kharif and Rabi seasons. The fertiliser manufacturers
have evolved a distribution pattern so that the seasonal demand can be met. Likewise,
the demand for consumables and durables will be high during the peak crop harvesting
and marketing seasons. This is the time at which rural people have substantial cash
inflows. Hence, the distribution should be fairly intensive during peak crop harvesting
and marketing seasons.A tendency of rural savings being used in nearby urban markets,
for buying of consumer durablesnear major festivals like Deepawali or Puja is also
observed. If marketers in their rural distribution planning can take into accountthis
aspect of seasonality in buying, possibility of extending such distribution could be
dove tailed with the festival melas in rural setting andthis arrangement would result
in adequate sales realizations. During the summer months, in places that lack irrigation
facilities, the demand will be very minimal. Thus, the distribution system has to adapt
itself to the seasonal pattern of demand. In addition, festival seasons like Sankaranthi
or Pongal in the southern regions as opposed to Baisaki or Deepavali in the north
are also demand seasons. The festivals also coincide with the harvest seasons, like
Sankarnathi during the Kharif harvest and Baisaki during the Rabi harvest. So, the
distribution for rural areas should be more intensive during harvest and festival seasons
as opposed to a fairly uniform demand pattern in urban areas.

You would appreciate that such seasonality would have important implications for
inventory and transportation decisions. As seasonality causes demand to fluctuate
around predictable time lines, transient market structures like haats and melas have
typically been found to characterise the rural market situation. Melas or fairs often
coincide with important harvest or festival seasons and enable the marketer to time
his distribution patterns with surges in the purchase behaviour of the rural customer.
These structures are important participants in rural markets and will be discussed
when you study retailing and distribution strategy in Unit 14.

13.4 RURAL CHANNEL MEMBERS

A marketing channel handles the task of getting commodities from manufacturers
and sellers to consumers. It bridges the gaps in time, space, and possession that
keep goods and services from people who want or desire them. Carrying and
forwarding agents (CFAs), redistribution stockists, wholesalers, and retailers are
the four main actors in the conventional distribution route that services rural markets.

CFAs: CFAs, who are often hired by FMCG and consumer goods businesses to
augment their depots, is the first point of contact with the corporate depot for
distributing products to channel members. Depending on the stock handled, they
typically serve a single state or area. There are two different kinds of CFAs: one
with investment, investing in stocks and moving the firm forward, and the other
without investment (also known as the forwarding agency), moving the business

213

Accessing  Rural Markets

forward. The bulk of CFAs fit into type two; they offer godown space for stock-
keeping and stock redistribution stockists in bigger cities and district centers that
servesurrounding  urban and rural markets. Company personnel register the orders
and deliver them to the CFA for execution.

Redistribution stockists: Redistribution stockists, who deliver inventory to the
rural and urban retail networks, are the vital link between a business and its rural
channels. They are often found in district offices and cover roughly 30% of rural
merchants. They are the company’s first clients, unlike CFAs, investing in stock
and hiring a sales team to reach merchants in rural and metropolitan areas in
accordance with the permanent journey plan (PJP). Some businesses with extensive
product lines, like HUL, additionally employ separate stockists—known as rural
distributors—to serve rural areas.

Wholesalers: One of the most significant  channel participants to serve rural markets,
wholesalers continue to have a monopoly on half of the supplies and consumption
in rural areas. In the lack of direct company access to rural retailers, wholesalers—
one of the oldest channel members serving rural markets—control about 50% of
rural supply and consumption.Direct distribution still cannot reach more than 70%
of the rural market. There is not often a definite line between wholesalers and
merchants in rural locations. Because small retailers from nearby villages buy from
these merchants, especially in the feeder markets, all retailers double as wholesalers.

A typical Indian wholesaler is a GallaKirana (food-grain ) trader. As a trader rather
than a distributor, he favors supporting a brand or commodity  during times of growth
and withdrawing that support during times of decline. Rural markets were overlooked
due to the sparse availability of rural retail outlets. Businesses were compelled to
rely significantly on wholesalers, who took advantage of them by delivering goods
to even the tiniest rural stores. This led to commercial malpractices, including
sellingspurious goods. Companies need  toanalyse and expand wholesalers in nearby
markets (small towns and big villages) as suppliers of goods to rural retail stores
to prevent such practices and inefficiencies in the system..

Retailers: They serve as the first point of contact for consumers in rural areas and
the final link in the supply chain. They often buy their supplies from wholesalers in
adjacent feeder towns, and occasionally they receive their supplies from stockists
by van shipments. Another kind of merchant consists of mobile traders and typical
retailers with permanent stores. These sellers knock on doors rather than having a
fixed storefront or other location. This group of retailers also includes traders who

13.5.1    UtilisationOf  Cooperative  Societies

A large number of cooperative societies operate in rural areas for different purposes,
like marketing cooperatives, dairy cooperatives, credit cooperatives, farmer service
cooperative societies, consumer cooperatives, and other multipurpose cooperatives.
Given the number of such societies, there is at least one cooperative society of
one form or another for every two to three villages. These societies are linked with
higher-level societies at the taluk, district, or state level. Thus, these cooperatives
have an arrangement for centralised procurement and distribution through their
respective state-level federations. Such state-level federations can be motivated to
procure and distribute consumable items and low-value durable items to the member
societies for selling to rural consumers. Many of the societies extend credit to their
members for purchases. This not only strengthens societies in terms of sales and
turnover, but also in terms of profit generation for long-term viability. Farmers Service
Cooperative Societies (FSCS), for example, provide their members with a passbook
indicating their credit limits based on land holding. The members are allowed to
purchase their requirements from the society on the strength of the pre-fixed credit
limits, which are adjusted at the time of crop sales. The products stocked and sold
by FSCS are wide-ranging, e.g., controlled cloth, soaps, detergents, fertilizers,
pesticides, seeds, and other items. Where organised well, these FSCS function like
mini-supermarkets for rural consumers since they sell a variety of goods at reasonable
prices. It is an option worth considering for marketers in their bid to access rural
markets. This suggestion of involving the cooperatives is made since they have the
necessary infrastructure for bulk purchases and distribution to their member societies
in even remote rural areas.

13.5.2  UtilisationOf Public Distribution System

The Public Distribution System (PDS) in the country is fairly well organized. The
revamped PDS places more emphasis on reaching remote rural areas like hills and
tribals. The purpose of the PDS is to make essential commodities like food grains,
sugar, edible oils, kerosene, and others available to consumers at a reasonable price.
The shops that distribute these commodities are called Fair Price Shops. These
fair-priced shops are run by state civil supplies corporations, cooperatives, and private
entrepreneurs. Here again, there is an arrangement for centralized procurement and
distribution. Because PDS outlets are located throughout the country, both urban
and rural, they can be used to market consumables and low-value durable items in
rural areas. Effective utilization of the PDS system should be explored by the
manufacturing and marketing men since they already have a distribution set-up.

13.5.3  Self Help Groups (SHGs)

Companies have begun to use self-help groups, which have traditionally run
microcredit operations in rural areas, as important direct-selling institutions, providing
direct access and feedback to rural consumers. One successful example of using
the SHG is the Hindustan Unilever model. HUL initiated project Shakti, which aims

216

to involve the self-help groups (SHGs) that ran microcredit operations in the sale
of FMCG products. The objectives of the SHGs are to encourage members to
save from their daily wages or crop sales and to find ways to make their collective
savings grow. Adopting the direct marketing model of Amway and Avon, HUL’s
Shakti aims to involve SHGs in buying the company’s products at cost, which they
can then sell to their friends and to other community groups and earn a profit.

13.5.4   Periodic  Markets

Periodic markets are an important characteristic of rural marketing in India. Two
institutions, viz., fairs or Melas and shandies (Haats), perform the periodic marketing
function and are of major significance for distribution.

Fairs or Melas

Melas are an important institution that caters to rural markets. Several fairs (Melas)
owe their origin to religious backgrounds, while some fairs are organised primarily
for economic reasons (Sonepur cattle fair). People travel long distances to attend
fairs and melas. Melas are generally organised after harvest season, so the villager
has enough money, which he will be ready to spend.

Haats

Haats provide a very useful mechanism for reaching the interior rural markets. The
importance of haats is greater in the interior, less prosperous villages. In the more
fertile tracts, permanently located shops are more important as outlets for goods.

Haats and Shandies are different names used to designate periodical markets held
once a week. A periodic market is a public gathering of buyers and sellers meeting
at a customary location at regular intervals. Most of the haats serve an area within
a radius of 12–15 km. Haats can feed about 4000 people spread over 10–15 villages.
The importance of the haat is based on the number of stalls selling “urban” products.
Depending on the season, their transaction intensity may vary.Higher transactions
take place after the harvest season.

The significance of these haats and melas can be understood by the fact that they
are part of the regular purchase behaviour of the rural customers.

Hawkers

Hawkers play an important role in satisfying the needs of several rural consumers
who do not have access to retail outlets. Hawkers trade in goods like vegetables,
fruits, clothes, utensils, cosmetics, etc. and move from door to door at a defined
periodicity from village to village.

Once the channel has been established, it is imperative that the management
periodically assess them to track their success. Two main categories of distributor
assessments exist, namely:

(a) Evaluations aimed at assisting management in maintaining ongoing operational

control of distributors’ efforts in selling the company’s products.

(b) The overall performance review, conducted at regular intervals, is to provide
management with a comprehensive picture of the distributor’s activities.

The first stage of the evaluation or appraisal process is a recurrent, day-to-day, or
in specific sectors, hour-to-hour monitoring of the performance of the channel members
based only on how they have been doing in sales. The stock often shows the quantity
of material invoiced to channel partners billed to channel members and retailers,
which is a component of sales analysis reports that are fed data from multiple SAP
& ERP reports. These statistics may also be used to analyze how much more the
store might be charged depending on the sales at that location. The other aspect
of evaluation is primarily focused on conformity to specified standards for outlets
representing manufacturers and specific matrices set out by the principal firm.

(a) Channel Partner Performance Audit: A channel partner performance audit
is a comprehensive evaluation carried out regularly to look at numerous
distribution partner metrics. These audits can be conducted at the wholesale
and retail levels for all or a portion of the channel partners. Typically, the
audit is conducted once a year. These audits are completed in three certain
stages.

(1) A set format and criteria for evaluating channel partner performance

(2) The  performance  of  channel  partners  must  be  reviewed  at

14.1 INTRODUCTION

The process of retailing and distribution entails the physical movement of goods
from the point of production to the point of consumption. It involves decisions on
transportation choices, warehousing, inventory maintenance and control, materials
handling, and selling standards of customer service. The rural markets, with their
infrastructural characteristics, present unique challenges to retailing and distribution
planners. We have already discussed the physical infrastructure and dynamics of
distribution in unit 12 and participants in the rural distribution process in unit
13. Against that backdrop, let us look at the place of purchase variations and the
marketing implications emanating from the same. A situation common in village retailing
and distribution is that a significant amount of purchases are made not by goods
moving to consumption locations, but by aspiring consumers moving to nearby urban
markets to buy them, and this behaviour is motivated by more than a simple lack
of availability. A good percentage of rural consumers show a preference for shopping
at urban locations on account of the wider assortments available, the perception of
quality, or just for the shopping experience. Rural markets show significant point
of purchase variation. Taking the supply chain network further than these villages
is something that corporations would like to consider in terms of incremental costs

14.2 PLACE  OF  PURCHASE  VARIATIONS  AND

RURAL  RETAILING

As urban consumers, we all have the perception that rural customers rely almost
entirely on the village retailer for the majority of their purchase needs. The reality,
however, is changing. Not all rural consumers buy from the same location and not
all their needs are sought to be consummated at their rural retailer It is also true
that the same consumer purchases from various locations depending on the product
and need.

Distribution is clearly the key to rural marketing. But it’s wrong to imagine that it’s
all that matters. Consumers in rural areas are not cut off from the urban world. As
urban areas spread, rural consumers are increasingly getting at least occasional access
to markets in towns, either directly or through friends and relatives. Rural shopkeepers
are also increasing the range of products they buy from urban wholesalers or even
other small retailers. There are transitory retail formats, like weekly haats and mandis,
or travelling retailers. Whatever the means, rural consumers are eager to consume,
and if marketers won’t help them, they’ll find other ways. We must also be mindful
of the fact that as reach of media, both audio visual and mobile based social media
become increasingly deeply penetrated, the awareness level of product and service
options is rising and the buying aspiration may be satisfied either at rural or nearby
urban locations.

There are a few categories where rural distribution is still comparatively low – or,
more interestingly, where the range in villages may be too limited for the consumers
– but which is compensated for by consumption sourced from urban areas.

A study on haats indicates that, despite the same product being available in the
village shop, 58 percent of the rural consumers visiting the haats preferred to buy
these from the haat because of better prices, quality, and variety.

Rural consumers do not rely on the local outlets and haats alone, as some of the
purchases are made in urban areas. This is because there are a few product categories
specially in the durables category, where rural distribution is still comparatively low,
so the consumer buys from towns. In certain cases, the consumer seeks variety. In
the case of toilet soaps and washing powders, the range in villages may be perceived
as limited by consumers.

Rural retailers enhance the distribution system by offering the following benefits when
addressing customer needs.

Accessibility: Consumers can only benefit from products and services if they buy
and use them. In order to meet customer demands and make it easier for customers
to access them, retailers collect products and services from various sources and
place them in one location.

Convenience of timing: Retailers keep track of their stock to make sure it is there

225

Accessing  Rural Markets

for customers when they need it. Kirana (Corner) shops give their customers more
freedom and options because of their extended weekday hours.

Convenient size: When customers want specific quantities and sizes, retailers reduce
the quantities and deliver the goods in those specifications. As an illustration, shampoo
is offered in little sachets to encourage a healthy lifestyle. Consumption of goods
and services has an impact on consumers’ way of life and contributes to the formation
of social identities. The store helps customers by providing the right products in
the right size, services, and advice.

Rural Retailers’ Challenges

Many people might be hesitant to start their own shops in small villages for a variety
of reasons. It is challenging to maintain appropriate stock-keeping units during the
procurement process due to a lack of effective coordination with the feeder town
where the retailer purchases his products. Let us see some of the factors that pose
challenges to rural retailers:

Inventory: The retailer has a lot of stock-keeping units to manage. As a result,
there is a large inventory and a high cost of maintaining an inventory.

Irregular sales: Another challenge is that because people in rural areas make such
modest wages, it is difficult to constantly push sales.

Challenges of the pull strategy: A distinct challenge is attracting customers from
nearby areas. Without a regular promotion strategy, people from nearby villages
cannot be attracted.

Thin margins: The retailer is unable to charge more since the customers cannot
afford to pay higher prices. Retailers must frequently travel to the feeder town to
collect products. As was previously said, retailers obtain their merchandise from
wholesalers or the closest firm stock point. It is not possible to fully pass on travel
costs to the clients. The margins will be small in these cases.

Credit sales: Credit is routinely used by rural customers to finance large purchases.
The outcome is that the store faces working capital problems.

Credit Facilities: Depending on the location and the products, different credit
facilities are offered to customers. In general, it has been noted that consumer credit
extensions are relatively rare in towns, although they are frequent in rural areas.
Variations according to the product were also observed in a different study. Rice,

were not.

Pricing: In remote villages, some retailers charge prices above the maximum retail
price. They justify their overcharging by pointing out that it costs them time and
money to procure the goods from wholesalers. It follows from this that channel
participants in rural areas may be looking for higher margins.

Reason for Stocking a Product or Brand: Rural retailers typically stock a specific
product or brand because customers ask for it or the profit margins are attractive.
Less frequently, they do so because wholesalers push them to or because their
competitors do so.

Information source and influence: The significance of the wholesaler was revealed
by a study conducted by Kilaru (1994) in the Deoria district of Uttar Pradesh that
included 25 retailers in seven villages. The study found that the wholesaler is the
retailer’s most important information source. The wholesaler is also the most important
influence on the retailer due to any trade credit policies that may be followed.
Additionally, the retailer shapes rural customers’ perceptions and has a significant
impact on their choice of brand as an opinion builder.

Channel Conflict: An increase in the number of small retail stores in a neighbourhood
increases competition among existing small retailers. Long-standing retailers view
new retailers as threats, which could result in conflict over suppliers and customers
between the retailers themselves.

Channel Promotion: Retailers in interior villages do not avail themselves of discount
schemes because they prefer not to stock more, but this may not be the case for
retailers in feeder markets. As a result, the rural retailers are unable to pass on the
benefits of buying in bulk to their customers.

Frequency of purchasing: It was revealed in a study that in order to maintain
regular stocks, 30% of retailers visit the market (feeder center) daily, 40% visit
the market weekly for replenishing the stock, 20% visit biweekly, and the rest as
needed.  If it is not necessary for the retailer to make the purchase himself; he may
seek the assistance of a fellow retailer, relatives, or even neighbours. This not only
saves him time but also turns out to be economical.

Stocking: The average stock value for each product category in the interior villages
has been found to be around one-third that of the feeder village. In interior villages,
the average stock value of all packaged goods was around one-fourth of that in
feeder villages. When compared to interior villages, the monthly off-take for packaged
goods was only marginally higher in feeder villages. In the feeder villages, toiletries
had a greater off-take than other product categories, whereas packaged foodstuffs
and tobacco had a larger off-take than other product categories in the interior villages.
In comparison to feeder villages and even shops in town, the stock turnover ratio
(stock level to monthly off-take) for toiletries in interior villages was significantly
lower.

The earlier finding that there are few goods and items stocked in the interior villages
is supported by the low off-take, low stocks, and low stock turnover ratio. As a
result, there is no room on the retail shelves for new items and brands because the
present products and brands have essentially taken over the retail space of the rural
retailers. This, coupled with the retailer’s fear of the unknown, makes it necessary
to make extra effort to promote the introduction of new product or brand offers in
rural markets. A simple mass media-directed push approach alone is simply ineffective.

Purchase Source: Generally, agents of distributors do not visit retailers in the interior;
instead, they make one or two trips each month to towns or feeder villages to collect
stock. Retailers in feeder villages buy goods like cosmetics, toiletries, detergents,
and packaged foods from distributors’ agents, who supply these goods on a regular
basis. Retailers prefer big retailers in trade centres (feeder markets). It was observed
that 60% of retailers prioritise fair prices over other factors like product variety,
financing availability, and so on. Only a small percentage of retailers seem to want
credit facilities, which is odd because most people think they pay more when they
use credit.

Store Patronage: The rural areas have a high rate of store patronage. While over
60% of the consumers have been found to be patronizing the same shop,about 15%
of customers have been coming to the same store for more than 10 years. This is
evident even if a customer has a wide range of retail options.

A normal customer may readily identify the markets where to purchase various goods

From the company’s perspective, a reduction in transportation costs would be critical,

transport, and handling in smaller villages further complicates the situation. As a

In this situation, the company should look at the possibilities of utilizing the “natural

 Many FMCG companies that market in rural markets benefit from the natural flow

c) Competition:

The extent of reach provided by competitors does not have to be the only guiding
factor in determining the value of a location. Whether or not to follow the competition
would depend on the degree of differentiation between the competing brands. In a
highly differentiated market, it is not necessary to imitate each competitor’s strategy
because the customer may prefer to visit a distant market to shop in a different
location. On the other hand, if the products are not differentiated, then the extent
of reach to be attained would depend on the competitors’ level of reach.

and corporate media such as vans is responsible for information-rich consumption
in rural areas.

IT revolution

tour of oneself by avoiding loud TV advertisements. In addition to the Internet,
kiosks of enterprises such as ITC e-choupals, HUL I-shakti, Kisan Kendras, etc.
have opened new doors for information-seeking villagers.

15.1 INTRODUCTION

Historically, fast-moving consumer products companies like Colgate Palmolive (India)
and Hindustan Unilever Limited (HUL) have been quite proud of their distribution
capabilities. For instance, HUL products are sold in more than 8 million retail locations,
and the business has a distributor network in India that is more than 4,500.However,
due to a lack of direct access to end users, traditional FMCG companies, who
were formerly thought of as masters of marketing, faced significant challenges in
the digital age. Direct to customer (D2C) start-ups can potentially surpass well-
established FMCG companies due to their aggressive subscription business strategies.
As more people shop online, it is expectedthat FMCG companies will sever more
ties to their established distribution networks in an effort to become closer to their
end users.

The growth of organised retail outlets and B2B distributors like Jiomart, Walmart,
Metro Cash & Carry, etc. raised a common concern about conflict between FMCG
companies and their distributors.Distributors claim that FMCG companies are offering
their products at reduced prices to wholesalers and B2B channels, which usually
leads to dispute between the two parties. As a result, B2B merchants are selling
FMCG goods to small stores and retailers for less money than the traditional distribution
route does, which is now “adversely damaging” their brand and goodwill.

As the conventional distribution channel still plays a significant role in the rural market
by delivering products to dispersed and inaccessible villages, FMCG companies
will have to manage the balance between distributors, B2B, and online channels.

15.3 HUL’S RURAL DISTRIBUTION STRATEGIES

15.3.1  Indirect Coverage Approach

HUL started its efforts to expand into rural areas by using its urban network of
stockiests and distributors to provide indirect coverage of a reachable rural market
with significant business potential. According to this strategy, company vans were
replaced with vans operated by Redistribution Stockiests, which catered to a small
number of nearby marketplaces. Retail stockiests were given the task of visiting all
villages every two weeks in rural locations with low business potential but easy
access.

15.3.2  Operation Harvest

Traditional media had a limited reach, which made it difficult for rural markets to
become aware of various products. Additionally, due to the significant costs involved,
it wasnot always practical for the distribution Stockiest to service all of these areas.
However, these markets were crucial because there were many chances for expansion.
The company made the decision to launch specialized mobile van operations in order
to raise awareness and increase point-of-purchase accessibility.

Operation Harvest sought to augment the function of traditional media in rural India
and, in the process, develop relationships and customer loyalty with rural consumers.
Conducting product awareness campaigns on vans was another component of
Operation Harvest. Vans from HUL and its distributors visited villages as part of
this effort to distribute promotional materials, display product advertisements, and
identify important retail and distribution hubs.

15.3.3  Cinema Van Operations

These are usually supported by the Redistribution Stockiest. Films and audio cassettes
containing song and dance scenes from well-known movies were available at Cinema

entering into agreements with end users.

With the help of these subsequent “operations,” the company was able to penetrate
markets much more deeply than other companies. HUL recognized early on in its
rural distribution programme that market share would only be developed once demand
was cultivated through awareness-raising campaigns and continuous supply. The
company had to “build up” the market village by village as part of its rural push.

15.3.4  Operation Streamline

One of the main projects HUL undertook to enter the rural markets was Operation
Streamline. In the case of Operation Streamline, the products are dispersed from
the C&F agents to the re-distributors, who then give them to the star sellers. The
Star Seller, a cross-functional initiative, sells anything from detergents to personal

Through Project Streamline, HUL established a hub and spoke network and hired

These sub-stockiests are essentially stock points because the distributor cannot

distributes the product to stores in villages nearby and collects payment from them.
In order to get over the obstacle of motorable roads, HUL had initiated the project

15.3.5  Operation Bharat

HUL’s ambition to boost Fair & Lovely’s sub-10 percent rural penetration resulted
in the launch of Operation Bharat. HUL’s Rural Distribution Effort had the aim to
invest Rs 20 crore and reach out to 35,000 villages and 16 million households.

Trials had to be conducted for that to be accomplished. However, a one-product
charge would have been prohibitively expensive. The Personal Products Division
(PPD), worth Rs. 1,526 crore, added a few more extras to distribute costs. Products
for skin care (Fair & Lovely), dental health (Pepsodent toothpaste), hair care (Clinic
shampoo), and body care (Pond’s Dreamflower talc) were also introduced. And
so, the Bharat Pack was created. The four brands were all urban hits but misses in
the rural market, which was something they all had in common.

HUL considered creating regionally customized packs. For instance, in packs intended
for the north, a cold cream might be substituted for Fair & Lovely. Eight languages
were available for Bharat Packs (those spoken in the states covered). The four
goods were produced in Silvassa and Chennai. It would have been more logistically
challenging to add products customized to a specific region.

Another driver for such a massive push was the need to impact the topline. This
exercise was conducted in eight states, which accounted for 65 percent of sales
growth.

Three challenges these brands encountered in rural areas were identified by internal
market research: lack of brand awareness, lack of product benefits knowledge,
and total lack of any first-hand usage experience. The final one seemed to be the
most daunting challenge, along with a dislike of spending money, even on trials.

be large enough to make the number of repeat users meaningful. Two, the price-
volume ratio should be as close to the break-even point as possible. The 80 percent
productivity at Rs. 15 achieved both objectives. So HUL settled for that price.

geographic regions and had differing levels of prosperity. Between the months of
December 1997 and June 1998, as well as again between September 1998 and
March 1999, these sample operations were carried out.

15.3.6    Project  Shakti

Project Shakti was launched by HUL in 2001 with the intention of enhancing product
visibility and demand for its goods in rural parts of India. It gives employment prospects
for rural women who participate in this programme as Shakti Ammas (SA). The
project was first launched in 50 villages in the Andhra Pradesh state’s Nalgonda
district, and it was later expanded to other states. Approximately 45,000 female
entrepreneurs have enrolled for Project Shakti, which has reached over 3 million
homes and more than 100,000 villages across 18 states. Using the Shakti Ammas
as brand ambassadors, Project Shakti is HUL’s direct-to-consumer retail distribution
programme for rural areas.

HUL appoints Shakti Ammas by identifying underprivileged women with the help
of village panchayats or by collaborating with rural women’s self-help groups (SHGs).
HUL has allocated resources to giving these Shakti Ammas on-the-job training and
support. HUL sales trainers prepare Shakti Ammas to become saleswomen by
teaching them how to sell products to consumers and persuade them to make
purchases.After training, she makes a small investment to buy HUL products to
sell. Products are delivered by the HUL’s rural distributor to the Shakti Ammas,
who then sells them directly to village consumers and shopkeepers. Shakti Ammas
get HUL products on a “cash and carry” basis and, as needed, can also apply for
microcredit from SHGs or regional banks. This channel is used to sell a wide variety
of goods that are useful to rural consumers, including Lifebuoy, Lux, Ponds, Wheel,
Clinic Plus, Nihar, Pepsodent, Annapurna salt, and 3 Roses tea. Typically, 6–10
villages are serviced by each Shakti Amma.

Shakti Amma eventually gains the trust of the people by visiting their houses on a
regular basis with her products. As a result, she becomes a recognizable face in
the village. Her selling enterprise is made simple by this. She also has the benefit
of persuading people in a language that they are familiar with.

A typical Shakti entrepreneur makes roughly Rs. 15000 per month in revenue, giving
her a sustainable monthly income of at least Rs. 1000. Because most of these women

15.3.7  Khushiyon Ki Doli

The main objective of the “Khushiyon Ki Doli” campaign was to involve the general
public and raise awareness via the use of technology. Four sets of dolis, or palkis,
are moved all around the village. Each of these dolis or palkis is equipped with a
small generator, an LCD TV, and a DVD player. The primary target group is village
housewives. The LCDs display a variety of HUL’s product advertisements. It has
been found that the rural masses consistently look up to the urban masses as is a
common human habit.

Rural women and kids can quickly become interested in HUL’s new advertising
campaign. The rural crowd stands in wonder as soon as the LCDs turn on, keeping
a close view of the video with full attention because it is something new to them.
HUL also organized a few activities towards the campaign’s conclusion with prizes
including discounts on product bundles and a few sachets to make it more entertaining
and participative.

The module goes through a three-step process: customer awareness comes first,
then engagement, and finally retail interaction. A group of promoters goes to each

village and invites the villagers to a location known as a “Mohalla,” which is the
first stage in raising awareness.  The company uses these gatherings to inform the
“Mohallas” about the business, the products it sells, their advantages, and cleanliness
and  healthful  practices. To  encourage  increased  consumer  participation  and
involvement, about 4-5 teams organize these events in each village for small, targeted
groups in the local language or dialect. TV commercials and quick AVs that play
continually serve as a means of introducing brands throughout this activity. By utilizing
“real” examples, the marketers illustrate how using these products can improve
consumers’ daily lives by promoting improved cleanliness.

To add to the “fun” factor and improve participation, promoters also hold easy quizzes
and activities centred on the brands and everyday hygiene practices. In order to
generate consumer trial as part of this activation, The team give incentives to both
participating consumers and local retailers. This helped to increase availability at
village retail points. Following the mohalla activity, the promoters make consumer
home visits and go from house to house to create trial, offering customers attractive
promotions and payment plans. Another team similarly visits every shop in the village
to ensure that HUL brands are more widely available and noticeable.

15.4 SUMMARY

HUL has been using a consistent strategy to capitalize on the enormous potential
of India’s rural market. Since beginning its initial rural project in the 1960s, HUL’s
rural marketing strategies have evolved from an indirect approach to rural areas to
door-to-door marketing through Shakti Amma. Many developments are anticipated
that will have an effect on HUL’s strategies for rural distribution. HUL’s rural
distribution efforts are likely to bear more fruit in the future due to enhanced
Information Communication Technology (ICT) and road infrastructure, higher
penetration and usage of mobile phones in rural areas, and the rising purchasing
power of rural consumers. Furthermore, as rural customers’ aspirations to use the
same products as city people rises, so does their buying behavior.
