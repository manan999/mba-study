## Unit 4: Product Decisions

### 1. Core Service Product Concept (Kotler's 5 Levels)
* **Core Benefit**: The fundamental benefit or utility the customer is buying (e.g., Hotel: rest/sleep).
* **Basic Product**: The fundamental, functional attributes (e.g., Hotel: room, bed, bath).
* **Expected Product**: Set of attributes/conditions the buyer normally expects (e.g., Hotel: clean sheets, quietness).
* **Augmented Product**: Features that exceed customer expectations and differentiate from competitors (e.g., Hotel: prompt room service, complimentary breakfast).
* **Potential Product**: The possible future evolution of the service to further distinguish the offering (e.g., Hotel: all-suite concept, personalized smart-room preferences).

### 2. Gronroos's Three Levels of Service Product
1. **Basic Service Package**: Core service + Facilitating services (mandatory for service delivery, e.g., check-in desk) + Supporting services (value-adding, e.g., health club).
2. **Augmented Service Offering**: Expands the package to include *accessibility* (working hours, location), *interaction* with physical/technical resources, and *customer participation*.
3. **Market Communication**: Managing the service brand image through promotions so customers recognize and value the augmentation.

### 3. Stages in New Service Development (NSD)
To bring a new service to market, managers navigate five conceptual stages:
1. **Customer Benefit Concept**: Translating customer needs and desired utilities into expected benefits.
2. **Service Concept**: Defining the specific benefit path and answering, "What business are we in?" (e.g., entertainment vs. fine arts).
3. **Service Offer**: Fleshing out the details of both intangible elements (performances, scripts) and tangible infrastructure (facilities, seating).
4. **Service Forms**: Selecting delivery options and packages (e.g., yearly membership fees vs. single tickets).
5. **Service Delivery System**: Structuring the people (competencies) and physical evidence (facilitating goods) that perform the service.

### 4. Service Positioning & Positioning Strategies
* **Service Positioning**: Designing the company's offering and image to occupy a distinctive place in the target market's mind using the **3 Cs** (Customer, Competitor, Company) and **STP** (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning).
* **Ries and Trout's Positioning Options**:
  1. *Strengthening current position* against competitors (improving own offerings without direct clash).
  2. *Identifying an unoccupied market position* (filling market gaps).
  3. *Repositioning the competition* (outperforming them through active promotion/innovation).
* **Positioning Strategies / Themes**:
  - *Positioning by Attributes*: Differentiating based on a unique feature (e.g., theme-based resorts).
  - *Positioning by Benefits*: Focusing on specific customer rewards (e.g., online platforms offering bank-associated discounts).
  - *Positioning by Competition*: Directly comparing against competitors.
  - *Price-Based Positioning*: Emphasizing value/prestige relative to price.
  - *Positioning as a Leader*: Capitalizing on being #1 in the sector (e.g., LIC in life insurance).
  - *User Positioning*: Tailored to specific target groups (e.g., IRCTC's special senior citizen pilgrimage packages).
  - *Use/Application Positioning*: Positioning for a specific application (e.g., a bank positioning itself as the "best home loan provider").

## Unit 5: Pricing Decisions

### 1. Pricing Goods vs. Services
Pricing services is highly complex compared to physical goods due to:
* **Costing Difficulties**: Services lack physical raw material units. Costs are calculated based on input units (e.g., time, personnel hours).
* **Intangibility**: High intangibility content leads to negotiated, non-uniform pricing (e.g., consultancies, legal fees).
* **Perishability**: Fluctuations in demand cannot be buffered by inventory, prompting dynamic price adjustments.
* **Price Regulations**: Many services (electricity, rail fares, bank base charges) are government-controlled.
* **Pricing Terminology**: Terminology varies widely by industry:
  - *Premium* (Insurance), *Interest* (Use of money), *Fare* (Transport), *Fee* (Education, Health), *Rent* (Property usage), *Commission* (Brokerage), *Tariff* (Utilities), *Toll* (Road usage).

### 2. Role of Non-Monetary Costs
Customers evaluate purchases based on the total sacrifice required, which includes:
* **Time Costs**: The time spent traveling to, waiting for, and interacting with the service.
* **Search Costs**: The effort expended to research, locate, and compare intangible service options.
* **Psychic Costs**: Psychological friction such as fear of rejection (e.g., loan approvals) or anxiety over using complex systems (e.g., online trading).
* *Strategic Implication*: Marketers can increase the monetary price if they successfully reduce these non-monetary friction costs.

### 3. The Pricing Tripod
A robust pricing strategy rests on three analytical legs:
1. **Cost to the Firm**: Establishes the *price floor* (minimum acceptable price). Includes activity-based costing (ABC) to allocate overheads.
2. **Value to the Customer**: Establishes the *price ceiling* (maximum willingness to pay based on perceived utility).
3. **Competitors' Pricing**: Guides where to position the price between the floor and the ceiling.

### 4. Key Service Pricing Strategies
* **Discount Pricing**: Lowering prices during low-demand seasons to shift demand.
* **Psychological Pricing**: Setting prices at odd numbers (e.g., INR 499) to imply a bargain.
* **Penetration Pricing**: Setting a low initial price to capture price-sensitive segments (e.g., Jio's launch).
* **Prestige/Skimming Pricing**: Setting high initial prices to exploit premium segments and signify status.
* **Price Bundling**: Combining interrelated services as a single package (e.g., Amazon Prime bundling shipping with video streaming). Types include *pure bundling* (sold only as a package), *mixed bundling* (sold together or separate), and *add-on bundling* (base + extras).
* **Diversionary / Offset Pricing**: Attracting customers with a low-priced basic service, then charging high rates for peripheral/post-delivery services.
* **Guaranteed Pricing**: Paying only after results are delivered (e.g., headhunters, real estate brokers).
* **Captive / Two-Part Pricing**: Low entry price for basic service/equipment, with ongoing fees for usage/refills (e.g., DTH setup box + monthly subscription).

* **Differential/Dynamic Pricing Case (IRCTC Tatkal vs. Premium Tatkal)**:
  - *Tatkal*: Charges a fixed additional percentage of the basic fare (10% to 30%) subject to defined floors/ceilings.
  - *Premium Tatkal*: Employs dynamic/surge pricing where fares increase automatically as seat availability drops and booking rate accelerates.
  - *Pros of Dynamic Pricing*: Maximizes revenue from high-urgency travelers; manages peak capacity.
  - *Cons*: Can lead to customer dissatisfaction and perceived unfairness if not transparent.

## Unit 6: Place Decisions

### 1. Modes of Service Distribution
1. **Customers Visit the Service Site**: Requires convenient physical outlets (e.g., hospitals, cafes, universities).
2. **Service Providers Go to Customers**: Necessary for site-specific services (e.g., pest control, catering, home painting).
3. **Transactions Conducted Remotely**: Delivery occurs via digital channels at arm's length (e.g., online banking, stock trading, SaaS).

### 2. Channels of Service Distribution
* **Direct Sales**: Straight from provider to customer (e.g., online courses). Direct sales allow maximum quality control and customer feedback.
  - *Electronic Channels*: 24/7 convenience, consistent delivery, and low cost (e.g., online flight ticketing).
* **Sales via Intermediaries**: Used when direct outreach is geographically restricted:
  - **Franchising**: Granting rights to exploit a trademark, business format, or method in exchange for royalties (e.g., fast food chains). Promotes rapid expansion with lower capital risk for the franchiser.
  - **Quasi-Retailing**: Outlets that sell intangible services rather than physical inventory (e.g., travel agencies, hair salons, bank branches).
    - *Strategic tips*: Locate near complementary facilities, and centralize back-end production while decentralizing customer contact sites (e.g., diagnostic labs).
  - **Agents & Brokers**: 
    - *Agents*: Represent the principal under a long-term commission-based contract (e.g., insurance agents).
    - *Brokers*: Bring buyers and sellers together for a specific transaction (e.g., real estate brokers).

## Unit 7: Promotion Decisions

### 1. Challenges in Service Communications (Mittal's 5 Problems of Intangibility)
* **Incorporeal Existence**: Services are not physical matter, making them hard to display.
  - *Strategy*: Use **Physical Representation** (show unique physical elements like premium aircraft seats).
* **Abstractness**: Concepts like financial safety or health are hard to visualize.
  - *Strategy*: Show a **Service Consumption Episode** (customers enjoying the service benefits).
* **Generality**: Vague promises ("great quality") fail to differentiate.
  - *Strategy*: Use **System Documentation** (show facts, figures, and capacity) or **Performance Documentation** (cite historical success rates).
* **Non-Searchability**: Services cannot be inspected before purchase.
  - *Strategy*: Use **Independent Performance Documentation** (audits) and **Consumption Documentation** (customer testimonials).
* **Mental Impalpability**: Complex services are hard for newcomers to comprehend.
  - *Strategy*: Show a **Service Process Episode** (step-by-step documentary of the delivery) or a **Case History Episode** (actual case study of a client).

### 2. Guidelines for Advertising Campaigns (PRO Perspectives)
When creating an ad campaign for a prestigious educational/management institution, follow these parameters:
* **Positive Staff Impact**: Ensure the ad motivates internal faculty and staff to deliver on the promises.
* **Provide Tangible Clues**: Show physical evidence (campus infrastructure, digital library, class interactions).
* **Make Service Understandable**: Clearly define the learning process and curriculum flexibility.
* **Capitalize on Word-of-Mouth**: Embed authentic testimonials from successful alumni and corporate recruiters.
* **Continuity**: Use consistent symbols, themes, and institutional colors.
* **Promise Only the Possible**: Avoid exaggerating placement records to maintain credibility.

### 3. Methods of Service Promotion (Promotion Mix)
* **Advertising**: Focuses on tangibilizing services and building brand familiarity.
* **Sales Promotion**: Limited compared to goods (sampling is expensive). Focuses on *premiums* (physical gifts to add tangibility) and *price/quantity deals* (group travel discounts).
* **Personal Selling**: High interaction; requires building personal relationships, displaying professional competency, and customizing the service packages.
* **Public Relations & Publicity**: Community outreach and media stories (e.g., doctors conducting free rural health camps). Highly credible and cost-effective.
* **Social Media & Online Channels**: YouTube, blogs, and review platforms are vital. Services are experience-heavy, so peer reviews, viral videos, and influencer endorsements ("word-of-mouse") act as powerful triggers for alternative evaluation.
* **Integrated Service Marketing Communications (ISMC)**: Coordinates all departments (marketing, HR, operations, PR) to ensure that every touchpoint (ad copy, website, frontline employee interaction) delivers a single, consistent brand message.
