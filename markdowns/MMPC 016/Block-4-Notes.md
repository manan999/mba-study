### UNIT 10: INTERNATIONAL MARKETING

#### 1. Domestic vs. International Marketing
*   **Concept:** While marketing principles (satisfying needs profitably) are universal, the markets, consumer behaviors, and environmental constraints differ significantly across national borders.
*   **Key Differences:**
    | Feature | Domestic Marketing | International Marketing |
    | :--- | :--- | :--- |
    | **Markets Served** | Single market with relatively familiar, homogeneous segments. | Multiple markets with diverse cultures, languages, and behaviors. |
    | **Control & Research** | Easier to monitor and control; high default market awareness. | Highly complex; systematic, cross-cultural market research is mandatory. |
    | **Product & Brand** | Standardized product mix is common; life-cycle stage is uniform. | Product adaptation is often required; product life cycle (PLC) stages vary by country. |
    | **Media & Advertising** | Known media availability; single message with localized sub-segments. | Complex media regulations; message must be customized or globally standardized. |
*   **International Marketing Mix:** Deciding between **Standardization** (using the same 4 Ps globally to save costs and maintain brand consistency) and **Adaptation** (modifying product, price, place, and promotion to suit local cultural, legal, and economic needs).

#### 2. Key Drivers and Challenges
*   **Drivers:** Liberalization of economies, tax advantages in specific countries (e.g., zero-tax regimes), free trade agreements within trade blocs, domestic market saturation, R&D cost amortization, and logistics advancements.
*   **Challenges:** Rapid technological changes (Industry 4.0), intense competition from local/global store brands, consumer empowerment ( awakened consumers demanding customized, value-driven products), and cultural diversity.

#### 3. Market Selection, Channels, and Exporting Steps
*   **Selecting International Markets:** Evaluated using a filtering process:
    1.  *Macro-Screening:* Examining country-level indicators (GDP, population, political risk).
    2.  *Micro-Screening:* Analyzing industry-specific demand, competitor intensity, and distribution access.
    3.  *Final Selection:* Scoring markets based on strategic fit and resource match.
*   **Channels of Distribution:**
    *   *Direct Channels:* Selling directly to host-country retailers or consumers (e.g., e-commerce, sales offices). Higher control, higher cost.
    *   *Indirect Channels:* Utilizing intermediaries (distributors, agents, trading houses). Lower cost, but limits market feedback.
*   **Steps for Successful Exporting:**
    1.  *Market Research & Product Adaptation* (aligning with local standards).
    2.  *Acquiring Export Licenses & Registration* (e.g., IEC in India).
    3.  *Identifying Buyers & Securing Orders* (via trade fairs or agents).
    4.  *Securing Finance* (pre-shipment and post-shipment credit).
    5.  *Manufacturing, Packaging, and Quality Inspection*.
    6.  *Customs Clearance, Shipping, and Submitting Documents for Payment*.

#### 4. Market Research & Consumer Behaviour
*   **Significance of Market Research:** Minimizes the high risk of cross-border operations. Essential to understand local demand patterns, competitor moves, and compliance requirements.
*   **International Consumer Behaviour:** Shaped by non-economic factors (culture, social status, religion, local aesthetics) in addition to disposable income. Standardized global products must often be tweaked to match local cultural associations (e.g., Barbie doll variants matching local ethnicities).

### UNIT 11: INTERNATIONAL FINANCE

#### 1. The Global Financial System & Foreign Exchange Market
*   **Global Financial System:** The framework of institutions, regulations, and financial markets (like IMF, World Bank, and forex markets) that facilitates cross-border capital flows. Volatility in this system (e.g., currency depreciation, interest rate shocks) directly impacts MNE transaction costs and profitability.
*   **Foreign Exchange (Forex) Market:** The global, decentralized market for trading currencies.
    *   *Participants:* Commercial banks, central banks, MNCs, speculators, and brokers.
    *   *Exposures:*
        1.  *Transaction Exposure:* Risk that currency fluctuations will affect the value of contracted cash flows before settlement.
        2.  *Translation (Accounting) Exposure:* Risk that translating foreign subsidiary financial statements into parent currency will show accounting losses.
        3.  *Economic (Operating) Exposure:* Risk that long-term exchange rate changes will impact the firm's competitive position and future cash flows.

#### 2. Balance of Payments (BoP)
An accounting statement summarizing all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world over a specific period:
1.  **Trade Account:** Records exports and imports of physical (visible) goods.
2.  **Current Account:** Records trade in services (invisibles), unilateral transfers (remittances, gifts), and factor income (interest, dividends).
3.  **Capital Account:** Records capital transfers and acquisitions of non-financial assets (e.g., patents).
4.  **Financial/Official Reserves Account:** Records public/private investment flows (FDI, FPI) and changes in gold/SDR holdings by the central bank.

#### 3. Terms of Payment in International Trade
To balance risk between exporters and importers, four main payment terms are used:
*   **Cash in Advance:** Importer pays before shipment. Zero risk for exporter; maximum risk for importer.
*   **Documentary Collection:** Shipping documents are sent through banks. Importer obtains documents to clear goods only after payment:
    *   *Documents Against Payment (D/P) / Sight Draft:* Importer pays immediately on presentation.
    *   *Documents Against Acceptance (D/A) / Usance Draft:* Importer signs a promise to pay at a future date (credit period).
*   **Open Account:** Goods are shipped directly to the importer, who pays after a credit period (e.g., 30/60 days). Maximum risk for exporter.
*   **Letter of Credit (L/C):** Importer’s bank (issuing bank) guarantees payment to the exporter upon submission of matching shipping documents. Transfers credit risk from the importer to the bank. Governed by **UCPDC 600** rules.

#### 4. Foreign Investment & Risk Mitigation
*   **FDI vs. FPI:**
    *   *Foreign Direct Investment (FDI):* Physical investment in assets (Greenfield, JVs, Acquisitions) with long-term management interest and control.
    *   *Foreign Portfolio Investment (FPI):* Investment in financial assets (stocks, bonds, ADRs/GDRs, FII flows) for financial returns, without control. Highly liquid.
*   **Risk Mitigation & Hedging Strategies:**
    *   *Hedging:* Taking an opposite position in a related asset (usually using derivatives) to offset exchange rate losses.
    *   *Derivatives:* **Forward Contracts** (locking in an exchange rate for a future date), **Futures Contracts** (standardized forwards traded on exchanges), **Currency Options** (right, but not obligation, to buy/sell currency), and **Currency Swaps**.
    *   *Approaches:* **Full Hedging** (covering all exposures), **Selective Hedging** (hedging high-risk exposures), or **Speculation** (leaving exposures unhedged to profit from currency movements).

### UNIT 12: INTERNATIONAL OPERATIONS & LOGISTICS MANAGEMENT

#### 1. Strategic Issues in International Operations Management
Operations management in an MNE involves coordinating dispersed manufacturing facilities. Key strategic issues include:
*   **Plant Location Decisions:** Choosing where to manufacture based on factor costs (labor, land, energy), proximity to markets, infrastructure quality, and host-government incentives.
*   **Capacity Planning:** Deciding whether to build centralized global factories (scale economies) or decentralized regional plants (local customization and lower transport costs).
*   **Sourcing & Make-or-Buy Decisions:** Determining which components to manufacture in-house and which to outsource globally. Driven by cost, quality, and IP protection.

#### 2. Logistics & Marketing Interface
*   **International Logistics Management:** The physical movement and storage of raw materials and finished goods across national borders.
*   **Interface with Marketing Management:** Logistics is the execution engine of marketing. A marketing promise of "rapid delivery" or "high product availability" fails without tight logistics integration.
*   *Key Coordination Areas:*
    1.  *Customer Service Standards:* Balancing delivery speed with transport costs.
    2.  *Pricing and Packaging:* Designing packaging that survives long transit while remaining attractive on retail shelves.
    3.  *Inventory Management:* Optimizing stock levels to prevent stock-outs in foreign markets without incurring high holding costs.

#### 3. Supply Chain Management (SCM) & Modelling
*   **SCM Parts:**
    *   *Upstream:* Sourcing and procurement of raw materials from global suppliers.
    *   *Horizontal:* Internal materials handling, warehousing, and quality control.
    *   *Downstream:* Distribution and delivery of finished goods to final consumers.
*   **Supply Chain Models:**
    *   **SCOR (Supply Chain Operations Reference) Model:** Focuses on measuring total supply chain performance (reliability, responsiveness, flexibility, cost, asset management) from the supplier's supplier to the customer's customer.
    *   **GSCF (Global Supply Chain Forum) Model:** Focuses on managing cross-functional, cross-organizational business processes through dedicated teams (linking logistics, R&D, finance, and marketing).

### UNIT 13: INTERNATIONAL HUMAN RESOURCE MANAGEMENT (IHRM)

#### 1. Scope, Staffing Approaches, and Cross-Cultural Issues
*   **IHRM Scope:** Managing employees (Parent Country Nationals - PCNs, Host Country Nationals - HCNs, and Third Country Nationals - TCNs) across global subsidiaries.
*   **Staffing Orientations (Perlmutter's EPRG Framework):**
    1.  *Ethnocentric:* Key subsidiary positions are filled by parent-country nationals (PCNs/expatriates). Assumes home practices are superior.
    2.  *Polycentric:* Subsidiaries are managed by host-country nationals (HCNs). Assumes local managers understand local markets best.
    3.  *Regiocentric:* Staff are transferred within regional boundaries (e.g., European managers transferred within Europe).
    4.  *Geocentric:* The best people are selected for key positions globally, regardless of nationality.
*   **Cross-Cultural Issues:** Cultural differences (language barriers, variations in work ethics, communication styles) can lead to conflict. IHRM must manage diversity to ensure cooperation.

#### 2. Expatriate Selection, Training, and Performance Appraisal
*   **Expatriate Selection Factors:** Technical competence, cultural adaptability, emotional resilience, family adjustment capability, and language skills.
*   **Expatriate Training:** Focuses on minimizing **culture shock**:
    *   *Pre-Departure Training:* Cultural awareness, area studies, language training, sensitivity training, and preliminary site visits.
    *   *HCN / On-the-Job Training:* Continuous development after joining the foreign subsidiary.
*   **Expatriate Performance Appraisal Challenges:**
    *   *Geographical Distance:* Appraisal data is hard to verify.
    *   *Evaluator Bias:* Conflict between Home-Country Evaluators (who know corporate strategy but are far away) and Host-Country Evaluators (who see daily behavior but might evaluate based on local norms).
    *   *Solution:* Use multiple raters, customize performance metrics, and account for local operating constraints.

#### 3. International Compensation
*   **Objectives:** Attract and retain qualified international executives, facilitate transfers between locations, and maintain consistency and equity across subsidiaries.
*   **Two Main Compensation Approaches:**
    1.  **The Going-Rate (Market Rate) Approach:** Links expatriate base pay to the local salary structure of the host country.
        *   *Pros:* Simple to administer; maintains equity with local host-country peers.
        *   *Cons:* Can result in a pay cut if transferred to a lower-pay country; hard to repatriate.
    2.  **The Balance Sheet Approach:** Focuses on maintaining the expatriate’s home-country living standard, adding financial inducements.
        *   *Pros:* Most common approach; ensures equity between expatriates from the same home country; simplifies repatriation.
        *   *Cons:* Can create wide disparities between expatriates of different nationalities working in the same host country office.
