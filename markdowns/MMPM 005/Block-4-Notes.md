## Unit 11: Managing Service Quality

### 1. Concept of Service Quality
* **Definition**: The degree of discrepancy between customer expectations or desires and their actual experience/perceptions of the service performance.
  $$\text{Perceived Service Quality} = \text{Perceived Service} - \text{Expected Service}$$
* **Process vs. Outcome**: Customers evaluate service quality based on *technical/outcome quality* (what they get) and *functional/process quality* (how they get it).

### 2. The SERVQUAL Gaps Model (PZB) Applied to an e-Commerce Portal
The model identifies five key discrepancies that lead to service quality failures:
* **Gap 1: The Listening Gap (Not knowing what customers expect)**:
  - *e-Commerce context*: Management designs website categories without researching how modern customers search for products.
  - *Solution*: Proactive customer research, analyzing search logs, and conducting usability tests.
* **Gap 2: The Service Design & Standards Gap (Not selecting the right standards)**:
  - *e-Commerce context*: Management knows customers expect instant support but fails to set standard response times (SLA) for query tickets or fails to establish page-load speed targets.
  - *Solution*: Set customer-driven standards, design clean user flows, and implement automated chatbot fallback options.
* **Gap 3: The Service Performance Gap (Not delivering to service standards)**:
  - *e-Commerce context*: The website experiences server downtime during a flash sale, or warehouse staff make packaging/billing errors, failing to meet the target shipping speed.
  - *Solution*: Invest in robust cloud hosting, automate inventory synchronization, and properly train and motivate warehouse operations staff.
* **Gap 4: The Communication Gap (Not matching performance to promises)**:
  - *e-Commerce context*: Advertising promises "Next-Day Delivery across India" but checkout systems fail to exclude remote pin codes, leading to late deliveries.
  - *Solution*: Align marketing campaigns with operational capabilities and clearly state shipping terms.
* **Gap 5: The Customer Gap (Difference between expected and perceived service)**:
  - *e-Commerce context*: The net result of Gaps 1 to 4. The shopper experiences frustration because their expectations of product quality and delivery speed do not match the reality.
  - *Solution*: Close Gaps 1 to 4 to ensure expected service matches or exceeds perceived service.

### 3. Five Dimensions of Service Quality (SERVQUAL) with Hospital Examples
1. **Reliability** (promised dependability and accuracy):
   - *Hospital Example*: Diagnoses are correct, billing is accurate, and surgeries are scheduled and performed exactly on time.
2. **Responsiveness** (willingness to help and provide prompt service):
   - *Hospital Example*: Nurses respond instantly to call buttons, and emergency room wait times are kept to a minimum.
3. **Assurance** (knowledge, courtesy, and ability to inspire trust/confidence):
   - *Hospital Example*: Doctors display clear professional credentials, speak reassuringly, and display deep expertise.
4. **Empathy** (caring, individualized attention):
   - *Hospital Example*: Doctors remember patients by name, recognize their fears, and design treatment plans around their specific routines.
5. **Tangibles** (appearance of physical facilities, equipment, and staff):
   - *Hospital Example*: Clean, sterile rooms, state-of-the-art diagnostic machines, and clean uniforms for the medical staff.

### 4. Causes of Delivery Gaps (Gap 3) in Hospitality & Solutions
* **Causes**:
  * *Role Ambiguity/Conflict*: Frontdesk staff are unsure whether to focus on speedy check-in (efficiency) or warm conversation (satisfaction).
  * *Poor Employee-Job Fit*: Hiring staff with high technical skills but low service inclination (poor interactive skills).
  * *Inadequate Technology/Systems*: Staff lack real-time access to room cleaning statuses, leading to delayed allocations.
  * *Lack of Teamwork & Empowerment*: Frontline employees are not authorized to resolve issues (e.g., offering a free meal for a dirty room) without manager approval.
* **Solutions**:
  * Provide clear job definitions and service scripts.
  * Train staff in both technical and interactive/interpersonal skills.
  * Empower frontline employees to make on-the-spot recovery decisions.
  * Foster teamwork and align rewards with customer satisfaction metrics.

### 5. Advocacy (Positive vs. Negative Word-of-Mouth)
* **Advocacy**: Loyal customers who actively recommend a service to others, acting as a highly credible, unpaid sales force.
* **Net Promoter Score (NPS)**:
  $$\text{NPS} = \% \text{ Promoters (Score 9-10)} - \% \text{ Detractors (Score 0-6)}$$
* **Dynamics of Advocacy**:
  - *Positive Advocacy*: Delighted customers become brand advocates, reducing perceived risk for prospective buyers.
  - *Negative Advocacy*: Detractors spread negative reviews. Connective technologies amplify negative reviews ("word-of-mouse"), making complaint recovery critical.

## Unit 12: International Trade in Services

### 1. General Agreement on Trade in Services (GATS) — 4 Modes of Supply
* **Mode 1: Cross-Border Supply**: The service crosses the border, while both provider and consumer remain in their home countries.
  - *Example*: An Indian software developer email code to a client in Germany, or teleconsultation by an Indian doctor for a US patient.
* **Mode 2: Consumption Abroad**: The consumer travels to the provider's country to consume the service.
  - *Example*: A US citizen visiting India for heart surgery (medical tourism) or a foreign student studying at an Indian university.
* **Mode 3: Commercial Presence**: A service firm establishes a physical branch or subsidiary in a foreign country.
  - *Example*: State Bank of India opening a branch in London, or an Indian hospital group setting up a clinic in Mauritius.
* **Mode 4: Presence of Natural Persons**: An individual travels temporarily to another country to deliver a service.
  - *Example*: An Indian IT consultant traveling to New York on an H-1B visa to deploy a software system at a client's office.

### 2. India's Opportunities & Constraints in International Services
* **Opportunities**:
  * *Software & IT Hub*: India controls ~55% of the global sourcing market and holds 75% of global digital talent, with massive exports via Mode 1 and Mode 4.
  * *Telemedicine & Medical Tourism*: High-quality healthcare at a fraction of Western costs, driving growth in Mode 2.
  * *Skilled Manpower Pool*: Abundance of English-speaking professionals (engineers, accountants, doctors) gives a cost-quality advantage.
* **Constraints**:
  * *Market Access Barriers*: Visa restrictions, quota limits, and cumbersome visa procedures in Western countries (Mode 4 constraints).
  * *Lack of Mutual Recognition Agreements (MRAs)*: Indian qualifications in fields like law, accounting, and medicine are often not recognized abroad, restricting practice.
  * *Domestic Regulations*: India has a high Services Trade Restrictiveness Index (STRI) in accounting and legal sectors, limiting foreign reciprocal integration.
  * *GSP Benefits Withdrawal*: Loss of preferential tariff access in key Western markets.

## Unit 13: Managing Demand and Capacity

### 1. Demand-Capacity Mismatch & Implications
Because services are perishable and cannot be inventoried, matching demand and capacity is a major challenge:
* **Implications of Mismatch**:
  * *Excess Demand (Demand > Maximum Capacity)*: Customers are turned away, resources are overstretched, and service quality deteriorates.
  * *Demand > Optimum Capacity*: No one is turned away, but facilities are overcrowded, staff are overworked, and customer satisfaction drops.
  * *Excess Capacity (Demand < Optimum Capacity)*: Fixed assets and staff are underutilized, leading to high unit costs and a silent/uninviting atmosphere.

### 2. Strategies for Matching Capacity and Demand
* **Shifting Demand to Fit Capacity (Demand Management)**:
  * *Pricing*: Lower prices during off-peak hours (e.g., late-night flight discounts, morning movie show discounts, restaurant "Happy Hours").
  * *Product*: Modify offerings seasonally (e.g., business hotels offering family packages on weekends).
  * *Place*: Change timing or create remote/satellite distribution outlets.
  * *Promotion*: Advertise peak hours to encourage off-peak utilization (e.g., "visit us during mid-week to avoid crowds").
* **Adjusting Capacity to Fit Demand (Capacity Management)**:
  * *Part-Time Staff*: Hire temporary staff during peak seasons.
  * *Overtime*: Ask regular employees to work extra hours.
  * *Cross-Training*: Train staff to perform multiple tasks (e.g., ticketing staff helping at the boarding gate).
  * *Scheduling*: Perform maintenance and conduct staff training during off-peak seasons.
  * *Customer Participation*: Encourage self-service (e.g., ATMs, self-checkout).

### 3. Yield Management (Revenue Management)
* **Definition**: A method of maximizing revenue from a fixed, perishable capacity by dynamically adjusting prices based on real-time demand forecasting.
* **Ideal Conditions for Yield Management**:
  1. Relatively fixed capacity (e.g., airline seats, hotel rooms).
  2. Segmented market (different price sensitivities, e.g., business vs. leisure travelers).
  3. Perishable inventory (empty seat/room is lost forever).
  4. Product sold in advance (allows bookings over time).
  5. Fluctuating demand patterns.
  6. Low marginal sales costs but high capacity-change costs.
* **Managerial Risks**:
  * *Customer Alienation*: Perceived unfairness if customers discover they paid higher prices than neighbors.
  * *Loss of Competitive Focus*: Over-focusing on revenue maximization might degrade service quality.
  * *Employee Morale*: Staff might feel automated systems override their professional judgment.

### 4. Service Guarantees
* **Definition**: A structured promise to refund, credit, or compensate a customer if the service fails to meet specific quality standards.
* **Four Features of an Effective Guarantee (Hart)**:
  1. *Unconditional*: No hidden exclusions or "buts."
  2. *Easy to Understand & Communicate*: Simple, short, and memorable guidelines.
  3. *Meaningful*: Must cover elements customers care about and offer a significant payout.
  4. *Easy to Invoke & Collect*: Quick resolution without administrative hassle.
* **Marketing Impact**: Reduces perceived risk, boosts purchase intent, encourages customer feedback, and aligns staff toward quality goals.
* **Preventing Customer Abuse**: Maintain database logs of payouts, verify customer claims, set clear fair-use parameters, and train staff to identify suspicious patterns.

## Unit 14: Emerging Issues in Services Marketing

### 1. Customer Relationship Management (CRM) & Retention
* **CRM**: A decision support system that collects customer data from multiple touchpoints (websites, apps, reviews) to offer a unified, personalized customer interface.
* **Benefits**: Enables customization, automates repetitive tasks, tracks purchase history, and provides analytics for targeted promotion.
* **Customer Retention Logic**:
  - *Zero Defections*: Retaining 5% more customers can boost profits by up to 100% (Reichheld & Sasser) because:
    * No initial acquisition/promotional costs.
    * Loyal customers buy more, require lower operating costs as they learn the system, pay premium prices for personalization, and provide free, highly credible word-of-mouth referrals.

### 2. Self-Service Technologies (SSTs)
* **Concept**: Technologies that allow customers to produce and consume services independently without direct employee involvement (e.g., ATMs, online banking, self-checkout kiosks).
* **SST Evaluation**:
  | Benefits | Drawbacks |
  | :--- | :--- |
  | **Convenience**: 24/7 availability. | **Equipment Failures**: Downtime creates severe frustration. |
  | **Speed**: Eliminates waiting queues. | **Lack of Human Contact**: Cannot handle split payments or custom issues. |
  | **Accuracy**: Customers input their own preferences. | **High Setup Costs**: Expensive initial hardware/software installations. |
  | **Personalization**: Data-driven, customized UI. | **Complexity**: Difficult for technologically challenged segments. |

### 3. Key Emerging Trends in Connected Services Marketing
* **Horizontal Innovation & Co-Creation**: Companies collaborate with customers to design services (co-creation) and with competitors (co-opetition) rather than relying on vertical, internal R&D (e.g., P&G's Connect + Develop).
* **The F-Factor (Friends, Families, Fans, Followers)**: Connected customers trust their social communities and online reviews (TripAdvisor, Zomato) far more than paid advertisements or corporate messaging.
* **Social Conformity**: Connectivity turns private buying decisions into social decisions, where users adapt their preferences based on the "wisdom of the crowd."
* **Customer Path Transition (4 A's to 5 A's)**:
  - *Traditional 4 A's*: Aware $\rightarrow$ Attitude $\rightarrow$ Act $\rightarrow$ Act Again. (Funnel model driven by company touchpoints).
  - *Connected 5 A's (Kotler)*: **Aware** (exposures) $\rightarrow$ **Appeal** (attraction to short list) $\rightarrow$ **Ask** (social research/asking reviews) $\rightarrow$ **Act** (buying & deep usage) $\rightarrow$ **Advocate** (spontaneous recommendation).
* **The O Zone (O3 Influence Framework)**:
  - Marketers must leverage three sources of influence across the customer path:
    1. **Outer Influence**: Company-driven marketing, advertising, and PR.
    2. **Others' Influence**: Word-of-mouth from friends, family, and online communities (highly critical in the *Ask* phase).
    3. **Own Influence**: Personal experience, preference, and post-purchase usage (critical in the *Act* and *Advocate* phases).
