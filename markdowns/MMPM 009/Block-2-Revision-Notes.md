# Block 2 Revision Notes: Retail Planning and Formats

## Unit 4: Strategic Retail Planning Process

### 1. Strategic Planning: Definition and Four Approaches
* **Definition**: Strategic planning is the process by which a retail organization envisions its future and develops the necessary operational procedures, structures, and systems to achieve its long-term objectives.
* **The Four Strategic Planning Approaches**:
  * **Reactive (Past-oriented)**: An attempt to resist change and return to the "good old days." Driven by a fear of the future and a desire to undo current shifts.
  * **Inactive (Present-oriented)**: A passive attempt to preserve the status quo. The organization believes the current state is acceptable and that future change will only make things worse, thus avoiding action.
  * **Pre-active (Envisioning the future)**: Envisioning future trends (mainly technological and demographic) and planning in advance to position the organization to exploit them.
  * **Pro-active (Creating the future)**: Designing a desired future state and actively taking measures to shape it. Rather than just anticipating trends, pro-active planners control and create outcomes.

### 2. Strategy Formulation: Deliberate vs. Emergent
* **Deliberate Strategy**: Structured and planned. It begins with SWOT analysis (matching internal strengths/weaknesses with external opportunities/threats), formulates competitive and functional strategies, and sets up control systems to execute them.
* **Emergent Strategy**: Organic and incremental. It develops over time through a series of consistent daily decisions and problem-solving actions that push the organization in a strategic direction, often influenced by internal organizational politics.

### 3. Corporate and Business Generic Strategies
* **Corporate Level Strategies (Portfolio Scope)**:
  * **Forward Integration**: Gaining control/ownership over distribution channels or retail outlets (e.g., a manufacturer opening its own stores).
  * **Backward Integration**: Gaining control/ownership over supply sources (e.g., apparel retailers owning manufacturing mills).
  * **Horizontal Integration**: Acquiring or merging with competitors to consolidate market share.
  * **Diversification**:
    * *Concentric*: Adding new, related product lines or businesses.
    * *Conglomerate*: Adding completely unrelated product lines or businesses.
  * **Market Penetration**: Growing market share for existing products in existing markets.
  * **Market Development**: Taking existing products into new geographical areas or customer segments.
  * **Product Development**: Improving existing products to boost sales.
* **Business Level Strategies (Basis of Competition - Porter’s Generic)**:
  * **Cost Leadership**: Competing on price via cost reductions, cheap fixtures, low rentals, and high inventory turnover (e.g., Walmart, McDonald's).
  * **Differentiation**: Competing on unique features, high customer service, flexibility, and brand value to charge premium prices (e.g., Apple Store).
  * **Focus/Niche**: Targeting a highly specific, narrow market segment with specialized cost-efficiency or unique offerings.

### 4. Performance Audit and Frameworks
* **Performance Audit**: The process of analyzing the health of a retail business, assessing both **"hard" systems** (strategy, operations, IP) and **"soft" systems** (culture, values, rewards) to check if the plan is on course.
* **Key Frameworks**:
  * **Gap Analysis**: Compares actual performance against the desired performance, identifying the "gaps" and prioritizing resources to bridge them.
  * **Balanced Scorecard**: Translates strategy into action across **four balanced drivers** (financial, customer orientation, internal business processes, and learning & growth). Emphasizes leading indicators alongside historical financial reports.
  * **Value-Chain Analysis**: Traces cause-and-effect linkages that drive performance by looking at key steps (understanding customer, managing the offering, channel management, location, operations, and exit strategy).

## Unit 5: Models of Retailing

### 1. The Wheel of Retailing Theory
* **Concept**: Proposed by McNair (1931), it states that retail institutions pass through a continuous evolutionary cycle where new formats enter as low-cost/low-price discounters and gradually trade up to higher-cost/premium operations, leaving them vulnerable to new low-end entrants.
* **The Three Phases**:
  1. **Entry Phase**: Retailer enters the market with low status, low rentals, low prices, minimal service, and low profit margin requirements (attracting price-sensitive, non-loyal shoppers).
  2. **Trade-Up Phase**: As the retailer succeeds, it seeks to expand its target market by upgrading store locations, enhancing atmospherics, adding services (credit, delivery), and increasing prices.
  3. **Vulnerability Phase**: The retailer becomes characterized by high costs, rigid operations, and premium prices. This makes it vulnerable to a new wave of low-price innovators, restarting the wheel.
* *Example*: Walmart and McDonald's started as basic low-end concepts and gradually expanded menus/atmospherics. In India, Big Bazaar entered as a discount bargain store.

### 2. Dialectic Process Theory ("Melting Pot" Theory)
* **Concept**: Proposes that new retail formats emerge from the clash and fusion of two opposing formats.
  * **Thesis** (Traditional format) + **Antithesis** (Opposite format) = **Synthesis** (New hybrid format).
* *Example*: Traditional physical stores (Thesis) + Direct home-based mail marketing (Antithesis) converged to create **Electronic Direct Marketing / E-commerce** (Synthesis).
* *Example*: Physical retail's touch-and-feel (Thesis) + Online retail's borderless reach (Antithesis) fused to create the **Brick-and-Click / Omnichannel Model** (Synthesis).

### 3. Retail Life Cycle Stages
* **Introduction (Early Growth)**: Format is brand new. Low sales, high risk, high launch costs, and negative/low profits. Success is uncertain (e.g., early e-commerce entry).
* **Growth (Rapid Development)**: Sales and profits expand rapidly. Geographical expansion occurs. Competitors copy the format, and cost pressures start rising towards the end of the phase.
* **Maturity**: Sales growth slows down due to market saturation. High competition requires margin cuts to maintain volume. The strategic goal is to delay decline.
* **Decline**: Falling sales, prices, and profitability. Retailers must reposition, abandon the format, or target niche loyalists (e.g., traditional bookstore formats losing ground to e-readers).

### 4. Theory of Natural Selection (Dreesman)
* **Concept**: Adapts Darwinian evolution to retail. Retail formats do not evolve in a vacuum; they must continuously adapt to environmental changes (demographic, economic, technological) to survive. Those that fail to adapt go extinct (e.g., specialty toy stores failing due to category killers, traditional bookstores declining without an online presence).

### 5. Scrambled Merchandising
* **Definition**: A strategy where a retailer adds product lines or services that are completely unrelated to its core competencies (e.g., a shoe store selling umbrellas, sweaters, and handbags; Starbucks selling sandwiches and salads).
* **Drivers**: Increasing average transaction value, capturing impulse buys, providing "one-stop shop" convenience, and balancing seasonal sales dips.
* **Risks**: Lack of retail buying/selling expertise in unrelated areas, high inventory costs, and potential dilution of brand reputation if the lines fail.

## Unit 6: Based on Ownership

### 1. Independent, Single-Store Ownership Structure
* **Features**: Owned and run by an individual or family (e.g., Kirana stores, mom-and-pop shops). Centralized, low-layer management.
* **Advantages**: High operational flexibility, low startup capital, fewer licensing hurdles, entrepreneurial zeal, and strong personal ties with local customers.
* **Limitations**: Low volume purchasing means weak bargaining power with suppliers; manual operations (limited IT adoption); high failure rate; overdependence on the owner, leading to lack of strategic planning.

### 2. Corporate Chain Stores
* **Features**: Multiple outlets commonly owned, centrally managed, and standardized in terms of layout, merchandise, and operations (e.g., Trent Westside, Shoppers Stop, Reliance Fresh).
* **Advantages**: High bargaining power via bulk purchasing; economies of scale in marketing/warehousing; access to TV/mass media; ability to afford advanced technology (ERP, VMI).
* **Limitations**: High capital investment (leases, inventory); rigidity in adapting to local micro-markets; administrative complexity across geographies; limited branch employee autonomy.

### 3. Franchising Structures
* **Definition**: A contractual agreement where a brand owner (**franchisor**) licenses its brand, products, and operating systems to an investor (**franchisee**) in exchange for upfront fees and ongoing royalties.
* **Forms of Franchising**:
  * **COCO**: Company Owned & Company Operated. Full capital and operation by the brand.
  * **FOCO**: Franchise Owned Company Operated. Franchisee funds setup; brand runs operations.
  * **FICO**: Franchise Invested & Company Operated. Pure investor funding; operations by the brand.
  * **COFO**: Company Owned & Franchisee Operated. Brand owns/invests; franchisee manages. (Rare).
  * **FOFO**: Franchise Owned & Franchisee Operated. Franchisee invests, owns, and manages under brand guidelines, paying royalties. (Most common).
* **Advantages**:
  * *Franchisee*: Access to established brand, shorter learning curve, training, and marketing support.
  * *Franchisor*: Rapid geographical expansion with minimal capital outlay; motivated owner-operators.
* **Limitations**:
  * *Franchisee*: Contractual rigidity, royalty fees on gross sales, risk of store oversaturation.
  * *Franchisor*: Brand reputation risk if franchisee violates quality standards; high coordination costs.

### 4. Leased Stores / Departments ("Store within a Store")
* **Concept**: Renting out specific departments inside a host store or mall to independent operators (e.g., cosmetics counters in Shoppers Stop, food courts in malls).
* **Advantages**:
  * *Host*: One-stop shopping for customers, shared overheads, steady rental income, experts manage complex categories.
  * *Lessee*: Access to host store's foot traffic, instant credibility, lower security/overhead expenses.
* **Limitations**: Mismatched operational hours, and host store gets blamed by customers for lessee mistakes.

### 5. Consumer Cooperatives
* **Concept**: Retail shops owned and run by cooperative societies or consumer groups to eliminate middlemen and keep prices low (e.g., Kendriya Bhandar, Sahakar Bhandar).
* **Advantages**: Lower prices, reliable quality, and avoidance of measurement fraud.
* **Limitations**: Limited marketing/publicity; lack of skilled management; member disinterest.

### 6. Vertical Marketing System (VMS)
* **Concept**: Unlike conventional channels where manufacturers, wholesalers, and retailers operate independently, a VMS coordinates or integrates channel members to maximize efficiency.
  * **Independent VMS**: Separate entities coordinate loosely. Standard for stationary, grocery trades.
  * **Partially Integrated VMS**: Two levels are owned by one member (e.g., manufacturer-owned retail outlets).
  * **Fully Integrated VMS**: Sourcing, manufacturing, wholesaling, and retailing are controlled under a single corporate umbrella (e.g., HUL, ITC, Reliance).

### 7. Dealership and Network Marketing
* **Dealership**: A licensing model where a dealer obtains rights to sell a manufacturer's products. The dealer behaves as an independent retailer, receives guaranteed supply/support, and pays no royalty (e.g., Maruti Suzuki or GM car dealers).
* **Network Marketing**: A direct-selling model where independent agents earn commissions by selling products to their personal network (family, friends) and recruiting sub-agents (e.g., Amway, Tupperware).

## Unit 7: Store and Non-Store Based Retail Formats

### 1. Classification of Store Formats
* **Specialty Store**: Narrow product line, deep assortment. Emphasizes expert staff and customer service (e.g., Zara, Titan EyePlus).
* **Department Store**: Broad variety of product lines (apparel, home, furniture) organized into separate departments managed by specialist buyers (e.g., Shoppers Stop, Lifestyle).
* **Supermarket**: Self-service food and grocery store with low margins and high volume (~4K-5K sq. ft., e.g., Reliance Fresh).
* **Convenience Store**: Small neighborhood shop with extended hours, carrying high-turnover daily necessities at higher prices (e.g., 7-Eleven, Spencer’s Daily).
* **Superstore/Hypermarket**: Mass merchandisers combining supermarkets and discount stores (~50K-100K sq. ft.). High volume, low margins, check-out lanes (e.g., DMart, Spencer's Hyper).
* **Off-Price Retailer**: Sells surplus, irregular, or overstocked branded items at deep discounts. Includes:
  * **Factory Outlets**: Manufacturer-owned stores selling clearance/excess stock (e.g., Nike Factory Outlet).
  * **Warehouse Clubs**: Member-only discount warehouses (e.g., Costco, Sam's Club).
* **Catalogue Showroom**: Retail showroom next to a warehouse. Customers browse catalogs/displays, select items, and receive them directly from the warehouse hatch (e.g., Urban Ladder, FabIndia).

### 2. Classification of Non-Store Formats
* **Direct Marketing**: Reaching out to customers via phone, mail, TV, or social media to trigger a direct order response (without face-to-face contact).
* **Direct Selling**: Face-to-face personal selling to consumers in their homes or offices (e.g., door-to-door, party plans).
* **Automatic Vending**: Impersonal vending machines selling high-turnover, low-cost convenience goods (soft drinks, snacks) 24/7. High equipment costs drive up retail prices.
* **E-tailing (Electronic Retailing)**: Sells products online. Segmented into **B2C** (retail to consumer, e.g., Amazon, Nykaa) and **B2B** (wholesale trade between businesses, e.g., software licensing, raw material supply).
