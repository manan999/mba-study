## Unit 8: Retail Location Strategy

### 1. Importance of Store Location Decisions
* **Strategic & Financial Weight**: Location decisions involve massive capital investments (land/building purchase or lease rents) and are highly **inflexible** once finalized. A poor location can quickly lead to business failure.
* **Cost Bearings**: Affects transportation/logistics costs (proximity to suppliers) and human resource costs (remote locations require employee transport/higher wages).
* **Traffic & Footfall**: Directly influences pedestrian and vehicular shopper volumes. Pedestrian traffic is analyzed by counting age/gender, flow by time of day, and conducting intercept interviews.

### 2. Delineating Retail Markets
* **Isolated Stores**: Standalone outlets with no retailers nearby.
  * *Pros*: No direct competition, low rentals, high visibility, flexibility.
  * *Cons*: Hard to pull customers over long distances, high advertising costs to build initial traffic, no shared costs.
* **Unplanned Markets**: Traditional business areas that grow organically without structured municipal planning (e.g., Central Business Districts/old city centers like Chandni Chowk).
  * *Pros*: High natural public transport access, low rents, high variety attracts bargain hunters.
  * *Cons*: Parking scarcity, severe congestion, no cost-sharing for security or promotions, limited expansion space.
* **Planned Markets**: Malls and structured shopping complexes.
  * *Pros*: One-stop family shopping, presence of large **anchor stores** to pull crowds, high footfalls, shared maintenance and promotional costs (organized by mall management).
  * *Cons*: High rentals, strict operating hours/guidelines, intense competition, dominance of anchor tenants.

### 3. Location Assessment Techniques & Delineation
* **Judgmental**: Reliance on gut feel, location history, or mimicking competitors' locations.
* **Systematic Screening**: Evaluating drives times, radius boundaries, and published demographic data (household income, population growth).
* **Analog Approach**: Comparing proposed sites with existing successful stores.
  * *Differential Analysis*: Differentiating features between most and least successful current stores.
  * *Regression Analysis*: Using math models to predict sales/profit as a function of site variables (size, population density, manager experience).
* **Market-Area Analysis**: Profiling customer locations using:
  * *Geo-demographic data* (demographic characteristics of local tracts).
  * *Lifestyle data* (buyer habits).
  * *Customer Spotting*: Mapping buyers via POS queries (phone/zip code), in-store surveys, or coupon redemptions.
* **Retail Saturation Coefficient (RSI)**: Measures potential sales per sq. ft. of space.
  $$\text{RSI} = \frac{P \times E}{R}$$
  Where: $P$ = Population size, $E$ = Per capita expenditure on the category, $R$ = Existing retail space in the area. High RSI suggests under-stored markets (high opportunity).

### 4. Locational Theories
* **Quality and Distance Theory**: Footfalls are directly proportional to the quality/size of the retail store and inversely proportional to the distance customers must travel.
* **Land Value Theory**: Competition among businesses for prime spaces determines urban land prices, driving higher-margin retailers to central high-traffic spots to ensure "highest and best use" of the land.
* **Spatial Interaction Model (Reilly’s Gravitational Law)**: Systematically estimates how much demand a given store will draw from various distances, balancing store attractiveness against distance friction and transport costs.

## Unit 9: Retail Product Mix and Merchandise Strategy

### 1. Merchandising and Cross-Merchandising
* **Merchandising**: The central function of planning, buying, stocking, and selling goods to target markets. It directly triggers customer **impulse buying** (unplanned purchases caused by attractive merchandise displays).
* **Cross-Merchandising**: Displaying complementary items from different categories together (e.g., displaying socks next to shoes, or tea bags next to mugs) to prompt impulse buying and increase average order value.

### 2. Merchandise Planning
* **Staple vs. Fashion Merchandise**:
  * *Staple (Basic)*: Daily needs, stable year-round demand. Easy to forecast, low inventory risk, low profit margins.
  * *Fashion*: High-style, status, or seasonal items. Hard to forecast, high margin potential, high obsolescence risk.
* **Assortment Trade-offs**: Retailers must balance:
  * *Assortment Breadth (Variety)*: Number of different product categories carried.
  * *Assortment Depth*: Number of brands, colors, sizes, and SKUs within a category.
  * *Backup Stock*: Safety stock kept in the warehouse to prevent stockouts (investing in backup stock limits variety/depth budgets).

### 3. Key Concepts
* **Category**: A distinct, manageable group of substitutable items (e.g., Men's Deodorants).
* **Category Captain**: A major supplier/vendor entrusted by the retailer to manage a specific category’s assortment, pricing, and display due to their category expertise.
* **Private vs. National Brands**:
  * *National Brands*: Manufacturer-owned (e.g., Colgate). High customer pull, backed by national ads, but lower profit margins and strict returns policy.
  * *Private Labels (Store Brands)*: Retailer-owned. Higher margins (20-30%), direct procurement control, but require store-level push and carry higher inventory risk.
* **Slotting Allowances**: Fees charged by physical retailers to manufacturers to display products on prime shelf spaces (does not exist in e-tailing).

### 4. Merchandise Manager Roles & Inventory Metrics
* **Duties**: **Planning** (sales forecasting/budgeting), **Organizing** (allocating categories), **Directing** (training buyers), **Coordinating** (aligning vendors/logistics), and **Controlling** (markdown tracking).
* **Inventory metrics**:
  * *Gross Margin Return on Investment (GMROI)*: Measures inventory productivity.
    $$\text{GMROI} = \frac{\text{Gross Margin}}{\text{Average Inventory Cost}}$$

## Unit 10: Retail Pricing Strategy

### 1. Organized Retail Pricing Strategies
* **Everyday Low Pricing (EDLP)**: Maintaining stable retail prices below MRP without frequent discount events (e.g., Walmart, Big Bazaar). Reduces ad costs and stabilizes inventory flow.
* **High-Low Pricing**: Charging higher regular prices but running frequent discount/sales promotions. Creates store excitement and targets both price-sensitive and quality-sensitive segments.
* **Leader Pricing (Loss Leaders)**: Pricing fast-moving items at or below cost to drive store traffic. Profits are recovered on other complementary, higher-margin goods.
* **Price Skimming**: Setting a high initial price for new/innovative products to recover sunk costs before competitors enter, then lowering it.
* **Penetration Pricing**: Setting a low initial price to capture high market share, break competitor brand loyalty, and build sales volume.
* **Price Lining**: Offering merchandise at a limited number of predetermined price points (e.g., Rs. 99, Rs. 199, Rs. 299).
* **Psychological Pricing**:
  * *Prestige Pricing*: High prices to denote exclusivity and quality (e.g., Rolex, Taj Hotels).
  * *Odd-Even*: Odd ending prices (Rs. 99.95) imply a discount/deal; even ending prices (Rs. 100) imply high quality.
  * *Reference Pricing*: Positioning a product next to an expensive alternative to set a favorable price anchor.
* **Other strategies**: *Pre-emptive* (low price to deter entry), *Extinction* (short-term low price to eliminate competitors, followed by price hikes), *Perceived-value* (pricing based on what customer values), *Fixed vs. Variable* (fixed pricing vs. individual bargaining).

### 2. Retail Calculations
* **Markup vs. Margin**:
  * **Markup**: Profit added to cost, expressed as a percentage of cost:
    $$\text{Markup \%} = \frac{\text{Selling Price} - \text{Cost}}{\text{Cost}} \times 100$$
  * **Margin**: Profit expressed as a percentage of the selling price:
    $$\text{Margin \%} = \frac{\text{Selling Price} - \text{Cost}}{\text{Selling Price}} \times 100$$
* **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**: Direct costs of acquiring inventory.
  $$\text{COGS} = \text{Opening Stock (at cost)} + \text{Purchases \& Freight Inwards} - \text{Closing Stock}$$
* **Markdown**: Reduction on the normal selling price to clear slow-moving, end-of-season, or damaged stock.
* **Fine-Tuning Tactics**: *Coupons* (vouchers for discounts), *Rebates* (post-purchase cash refunds on proof of purchase).

## Unit 11: Retail Communication Mix Strategy

### 1. Integrated Marketing Communication (IMC)
* **Definition**: The process of coordinating and unifying all communication channels (advertising, sales promotions, PR, personal selling, digital media) to deliver a clear, consistent, and persuasive brand message.
* **Flow Types**: One-to-One (targeted SMS/emails), One-to-Many (mass flyers/billboards), Many-to-One (customer feedback), Many-to-Many (social media chats/forums).

### 2. Traditional Elements
* **Advertising**: Paid, non-personal sponsor-identified mass communication. Divided into **National** (centralized MNC branding) and **Local** (regional/store-level promotion).
* **Sales Promotion**: Time-bound incentives to boost sales.
  * *Customer-Oriented*: BOGO, flat discounts, loyalty points.
  * *Intermediary-Oriented*: Trade allowances, dealer contests, training.
* **Personal Selling**: Face-to-face salesperson-buyer interaction. Highly critical for high-end luxury products.
* **Publicity**: Unpaid, organic media/news coverage. Highly credible but less controllable.
* **Public Relations (PR)**: Strategic actions to build a positive brand reputation and manage stakeholder relationships.

### 3. The Personal Selling Process
1. **Prospecting**: Researching and qualifying potential buyers.
2. **Preparation/Pre-approach**: Gathering product data, competitor rates, and planning the pitch.
3. **Approach**: The first contact with the prospect; making a positive impression.
4. **Presentation**: Explaining the product and showing how it solves the customer's problem.
5. **Handling Objections**: Patiently addressing buyer concerns regarding price or quality.
6. **Closing**: Guiding the customer to make the final purchase decision and complete the transaction.
7. **Follow-up**: Post-purchase contact to ensure satisfaction, build loyalty, and get referrals.

### 4. Modern/Digital Elements
* **SEO (Search Engine Optimization)**: Optimizing keywords to rank organically on search engines.
* **PPC (Pay Per Click)**: Paid keyword advertising.
* **Social Media & Mobile Ads**: Targeted ads on Instagram/WhatsApp/Twitter.
* **Innovative Tactics**: *Omnichannel message coordination*, *Product customization*, *Virtual-visual mirrors (AR/VR)*, *Social Shopping (Live streaming sales)*, *Loyalty programs*.

## Unit 12: Physical Evidence

### 1. Atmospherics: Definition and Stimuli
* **Definition**: The design of a store's physical environment (interior, exterior, layout, visual displays) to create a specific emotional response and shape customer behavior.
* **Environmental Stimuli Effects**:
  * **Pleasure-Displeasure**: Whether the shopper finds the environment enjoyable (e.g., lighting, background music).
  * **Arousal**: The degree of stimulation (e.g., fast music increases activity/arousal, slow music subdues it).
  * **Dominance**: Whether the shopper feels in control (large ceilings and open spaces make customers feel submissive; HSL colors influence mood: red triggers excitement/assertiveness, blue triggers tranquility).

### 2. Components of Atmospherics
* **External**: Storefront, marquee (signboard), display windows (visual attraction), and parking availability.
* **Internal**: Interior design, flooring, temperature, scents, and lighting (e.g., bright lights for activity at McDonald's, dim lights for relaxation at sit-down restaurants).
* **Store Layouts**:
  * **Grid**: Parallel shelves and aisles. Highly space-efficient, easy for planned, quick shopping (utilitarian - e.g., groceries/pharmacies).
  * **Free-Form (Boutique)**: Asymmetric arrangements. Relaxed browsing, luxury feel, but poor space utilization (hedonic).
  * **Racetrack (Loop)**: A single loop guides customers through all departments, maximizing impulse buying.
  * **Storied Layout**: Multi-floor layout; saves real estate costs and segregates product categories.

### 3. Visual Merchandising (VM)
* **VM definition**: Presentation of products in a way that highlights their unique selling points and educates/entertains customers (e.g., themed window displays).
* **VM Fixtures**: Slatwall panels (walls), garment racks/gondolas (floors), dump displays (bins for discount items), mannequins, tracking spot lights, planograms, and shelf tags.

### 4. Store Space Management & Performance Measures
* **Objectives**: Maximize return on investment by optimizing space between departments, product displays, customer aisles, and checkout zones.
* **Space Performance Metrics**:
  * **Sales/Profit per Square Meter**: Used when products are displayed in a single layer on floor space (common in fashion retail).
  * **Sales/Profit per Linear Meter**: Measures productivity of multi-shelved fixtures (racks/gondolas) by counting shelf length.
  * **Sales/Profit per Cubic Meter**: Measures 3D space productivity; used for freezer cabinets, dump bins, and high-stack warehouse clubs.
* **Web Atmospherics (E-tailing)**: Site navigability, server response speed, search functionality, clear product pages, and checkout cart design to minimize cart abandonment.
