7.1 INTRODUCTION

An organization has to make a gamut of decisions before it enters the global market. It
has to resolve three basic questions which may be related to each other (Hill & Jain,
2006):

1) Which foreign markets to enter, when to enter and to what extent i.e. level of

involvement?

2) What would be the choice of the entry mode? and

3) What would be the role of strategic alliances, if any?

In this unit we will discuss the first two parts. The issues related with strategic alliances
will be taken up in unit 9. Here, we will discuss various entry modes that an organization
can choose from in the wake of its decision to “go international,” starting with the
simplest, i.e., exporting (Keegan, 2002). It is important for an organization to first
tackle the question related to: which foreign markets to enter as also the timing and
scale of entry. The decision should be taken in the context of long term growth and the
profit objectives of the firm. We will first discuss the impact of the general business
environment of the target country on the modal choice of market entry.

7.2 VOLATILITY  OF  ENVIRONMENT

An organization planning to ‘go-global’ has to decide its entry mode and strategy.
Accordingly, it has to evaluate the prevailing business environment that might affect its
decision to go global.The scanning of global business environment which is also referred
as PESTEL analysis helps us probe the issues and challenges involved in deciding the
appropriate entry for an international market. The PESTEL analysis provides perspective
to an organization on selecting the particular market and also the entry mode to get into
business. The nature of the market also helps in determining what would be the possible
factors to consider before one actually get into business. If a woollen manufacturer
from India intends to go global, he/she will, obviously, first tap the markets which falls
in Northern Hemisphere having extreme cold and people needs woollens to protect
themselves and can subsequently explore the fragmented business opportunities even
in the countries having plenty of Sun-shine. Similarly, a low capacity (80cc to 300 cc)
bike manufacturer from India should scan the business environment of the countries in
Africa, Asia and Latin America where customers need bike for utility, instead of luxury-
buying of bikes of high capacity by rich and affluent customers in markets of developed
countries, i.e. Europe and North America.

There  are  certain  factors  which  are  controllable  and  certain  factors  that  are,
uncontrollable. For example, when KFC (Kentucky Fried Chicken) came to India as
a part of its global expansion programme, it faced stiff resistance from the Hindus who
felt that KFC was selling beef under the disguise of chicken and that it’s vegetarian and
non-vegetarian items were cooked in the same oil. Wherever KFC went, from Bangalore
to New Delhi, the company had to face stiff resistance and protests, including several
of its stores being destroyed by the protestors. The company did not or could not have
anticipated such kind of trouble. However, once it was able to overcome this problem,
the company experienced another problem and it was that its customers were unable
to relish the taste of the product; it received thumbs down from them. This case narrates
how companies have to face the unpredictability of the environment and in this case it
was the socio-cultural factors that troubled the company.

In the context of the unit, it is important to understand how the environmental factors
affect the market entry, particularly, the uncontrollable factors which are more important
to consider. A company may have developed its own objectives and strategies to
achieve them, but the environmental factors have to be considered carefully. It is the
business environment analysis especially of political, economic, social, technological,
environmental and legal which have its important bearing on entry mode of firm. Scanning
of business environment must be an ongoing process as the nature and scope of
international business operations change, the company will be able to adapt to these
evolving changes.

Environmental Scanning

The MNEs normally, before the market entry, try to obtain and scan all the needed
information about the market potential and competition in different countries. Once an
MNE has established its operations, the information required would broaden. The
process of information acquisition is referred as scanning. Keegan (1982) has identified
four major categories of information. These are:

 Market information



Prescriptive information,

 Resource information, and

 General information.

The market information deals with international marketing mix factors, the features
of products, pricing, distribution and promotional strategies. It further also helps
understand the competitive products in the markets, nature of competition, marketing
offers and investments required by a firm to deal with it. The prescriptive information
is about rules and regulations affecting business, both in the home and the host country.
It may include factors such as foreign exchange, taxes, incentives and any other
regulations affecting the company. The resource information is mostly about various
resources, other regulations affecting the company e.g., manpower, money, raw
materials, potential acquisitions and mergers, joint ventures, and any regulations in
this respect. General information relates to macro-level information. This would cover
economic, social, political, scientific environment and the management practices in
different countries.

7.3 THEORIES  OF  INTERNATIONALIZATION

While studying international entry strategies, why is it important to study the theories of
business internationalization? The answer lies in the fact that modes of entry into
international market are predicted on the basis of internationalization theories. In this
section; we will discuss some conceptual approaches to internationalization so that we
get a fair idea about different theories of internationalization. How they govern conduct
of internationalization of a firm including the planning of internationalization in a given
scenario. We shall discuss six basic conceptual approaches, which are as follows:

 Uppsala model

 Transaction cost analysis

 Eclectic theory or contingency approach



Industrial network approach

 Business strategy approach

 Bargaining power approach

1) Uppsala Model: The key proposition of this model is that how the firms proceed in
an incremental manner in the international markets and this is largely based on the
knowledge which they have gained over a period of time (Whitelock, 2002). This
assertion is based on the fact that firms first expand into markets which are physically
and culturally close (geographic closeness) and slowly gain experience and knowledge
(experiential knowledge) which they use for distant, diverse and variegated markets.
An Indian Ayurveda product manufacturing company will first focus on markets
which are in close geography of India and have similar cultural affinity, for instance,
India Ayurveda products manufacturers are popular in South Asia and are increasing
their reach to markets of South-East Asia (Indo-China region) and Middle-Eastern
Countries. Similarly, a North American company, Kellogg’s first expanded in the
regions which are geographically closer and are somewhat similar to the home
markets, Mexico or Canada and after having gained experience of internationalization,
it expanded to markets of Europe, Asia, Africa and Latin America.

2) Transaction Cost Analysis (TCA): This model is based on the assumption that
markets are competitive in nature. According to Whitelock (2002), TCA predicts
that firm integrate when asset specificity is high, in the other world, it has become

3) Eclectic Theory or Contingency Approach: Dunning (1988) explained the
importance of locational variables in the foreign investment decision of a firm in his
‘Eclectic or Contingency Approach’ which is also known as the ‘Ownership-
Location-Internalization (OLI)  framework’. Dunning propounded  that the
propensity of a firm to engage itself in international production increases is dependent
on the following three variables:

a. Ownership advantages: Any internationally engaged firm has bigger
ownership advantages when compared with the domestic one as it owns
foreign production facilities and assets. Ownership advantages may be tangible
assets like owning mine or rare mineral and intangible assets like technical
know-how or managerial talent.

b. Locational advantages: The locations offer the business gains and profits
from the assets it owns, i.e. factor endowments (labour, energy, materials,
components, transport and communication channels) in the foreign markets.
If locational advantages do not exist, such a market will be served by exports.

c.

Internalization advantages: It serves firms interests to gain from ownership
as well as locational advantages through the process of internationalization.
There is no reason to sell these assets or offer them on lease to some other
firm.

4)

Industrial Network Approach: The Industrial Network Approach is based on
the basic assumption that the international firm cannot be analysed as an isolated
actor/enabler. In fact, it has to be viewed with respect to other actors/enablers in
the international business environment. A firm is dependent on the resources
controlled by others. For instance, a radar manufacturer for an aeroplane industry
is  completely  dependent  on  the  export  orders  received  by  the  aeroplane
manufacturer. Similarly, a cryogenic engine manufacturer is dependent on the orders
received from space agencies of respective countries. There is no stand-alone use
of a radar and a cryogenic engine. Further, the supplier to such radar and cryogenic
engine manufactures are also dependent on wider industrial connection around the
globe. Johanson and Mattson (1988) rightly explain it as the relationships of a firm
within a domestic network which can be leveraged in industrial network connections
to other networks in other countries.

5) Business Strategy Approach: This approach is based on the idea of ‘pragmatism’
whereby the firm makes trade-offs between a number of variables in its decision
to internationalize.  It can therefore be said that according to this approach, the
process  of  internationalization  takes  into  account  market  attractiveness,
psychological distance, accessibility and informal barriers.

6) Bargaining Power Approach: According to this approach the choice of entry
mode is the outcome of negotiations between the firm and the government of the

host country.For instance, Russian defence equipment manufacturers are able to
bargain better terms in India’s defence sector due to their closeness for a long
number of years.

Country risk is the second factor that needs to be assessed before entering into any

currency risks, cultural distances and institutional norms.

The third factor influencing the market entry decision in international markets is that of
government policies. Government policies vis-a-vis the foreign investments and
companies including the kind of regulatory support offered have its important bearing
on the market entry decision. Government policies reflect the long-term orientation for
the industrial polices and role of MNEs. The growing interest of foreign companies in
the production-linked incentives scheme for promoting domestic manufacturing is a
result of government policies. Similarly, the government of India is offering almost US$12
billion as subsidies to the chip-manufactures to set-up their plants in India. This will
improve the eco-system of domestic manufacturing and promote local employment,
economic opportunities and exports. Government policies have another dimension of
political risks, for instance, a foreign company is always concerned about the
nationalization of assets, expropriation, import restrictions, economic blockage, etc.
Due to Russia-Ukraine stand-off in 2021, many companies have moved to safer places
for business.

Timing also influences the decision of international market entry. When an economy
opened-up, it makes sense to enter rather than when many other firms have established
themselves to give tough competition for market entry (Shad Morris, James Oldroyd
& Ram Singh, 2021). This is also known as the first-mover advantage.

The internal capabilities of the firm refer to entering and developing local resources,
assets and competencies in order to gain and sustain competitive advantage. The last
aspect on the cost benefit analysis refers to the expected return on investment.

7.5 MARKET ENTRY  MODES

International  Entry
Strategies

There are different ways in which an organization enters any market. In fact, the choice
is dependent on the factors discussed in the earlier section. We will now explain some
of the key modes in the ensuing paragraphs. The usual choice is to start the international
business with the ‘exporting’ and especially the ‘direct exporting’. It offers a firm to
gain insights of the market along with experience which helps it to plan bigger decision.
The exporting is also a least cost model as the production is being done home, and if
the product fails even after making several adaptations and leveraging other elements
of marketing mix, it can be easily withdrawn. Indirect export also helps some knowledge
of international business and help to create a network of prospective buyers along with
understanding of various stakeholders in the export supply chain.

The third major mode is licensing/franchising in which the licensor or franchisor (usually
the owner of the title) sells the rights of intellectual property such as‘patents’ or ‘technical
know-how’ to the licensee/franchisee for a royalty. The licensor/franchisor does not
have to bear the full costs and risks associated with foreign market expansion. The
fourth most preferred option is joint venture, in which a new entity takes birth, by
joining of hands of two parties. The last and the most significant mode of international
market entry is that of foreign direct investment (FDI). FDI can be in the form of a
Wholly-owned Subsidiary or an acquisition of an existing company, in part or as whole.
In both cases the company decides to build or acquire offices, factories and other
infrastructure in the host country in order to operate. Usually subsidiaries come into
existence through green or brown field projects.

7.7 SUMMARY

Market entry strategies are about ways and means to enter any given market when
firms go global. The strategy is however dependent on the market entry modes chosen
by the firms.

Understanding the process of internationalization is important to the modes of entry.
Internationalization refers to the process of a firm’s efforts to go global and entry mode
it chooses. There are several theories/models of internationalization that have been
proposed, viz., Uppsala model, Transactional Cost Analysis, Eclectic Theory, Industrial
Network, Business Strategy and Bargaining Power.

The factors that affect market entry mode are: Market (Country) attractiveness; Country
risks (political and operational); Government policies; Timing, Internal capabilities, Cost
benefit analysis. Having assessed the factors that affect market entry, the firm has to
choose a particular market entry mode.

Though there could be several perspectives on entering foreign markets, the basic
entry strategies relate to: direct export, indirect export, joint ventures, licensing and
Greenfield projects. The mode of entry would depend upon the firm’s level of
involvement that it wants to have. Direct export represents the basic entry mode which
capitalizes on economies of scale in production concentrated in the home country and
it can have a better control over distribution.The strategy of indirect export is achieved
by exporting through export intermediaries. In Licensing/franchising the licensor or
franchisor (usually the owner of the title) sells the right of intellectual property; such as
patents or technical know-how to the licensee/franchisee for a royalty. The licensor/
franchisor does not have to bear the full costs and risks associated with foreign market
expansion. Another most preferred option is joint venture, in which a new entity is
created through the joint efforts of two parties. Foreign investment is an also a preferred
entry mode when level of commitment in a market is high and can be in the form of
direct investment and portfolio investment, i.e. buying shares of an existing company.
FDI in turn can be in the form of owning a wholly owned subsidiary or through
acquisitions. Another underlying reason for foreign investment is that of entering into
strategic alliances in the host country.

8.1 INTRODUCTION

The concept of organizational structures has become extremely relevant in the context
of global organizations. It is indeed important to understand the organization structures
especially the underlying linkages among four key areas; which include global strategies,
global management, strategy implementation and global competitiveness. The essence
of global strategy lies in coordination and integration of geographically dispersed
operations for achieving global competitive advantage (Stone house et.al, 2001). In
this context, the desired organizational structure and their different types become an
important subject. The globally engaged firms usually choose an organizational structure
which is aligned to their strategic objectives. In this unit; we are going to discuss various
types of organizational structures and their advantages and limitations. The cost-cutting
of business operations in the time turbulent economic times further compel us to think
and innovate on organizational designs and structures. Organizational designs are
primarily based on the four interlinked fundamentals viz. organizational structure,

134

organizational process (management process), people and organizational culture. In
the subsequent section; we are going to discuss these key elements and then move
further to study different types of structures.

8.2 ORGANIZATION  FACTORS  AND  THEIR

ELEMENTS

While planning the organizational structure for any internationally engaged business
firm; the balance of autonomy and integration are two issues that are critical. Subsidiaries
need autonomy in order to adapt to their local environment and parent company,
simultaneously needs integration with all subsidiaries to implement global strategy. Global
organizations need to achieve both integration and autonomy while fostering the
increasingly important tasks of learning and knowledge transfer. Being able to transfer
knowledge from one country to another provides a key source of advantage for
multinational companies.

Four factors and their individual elements determine the crucial organization forces that
affect a company’s ability to formulate and implement global strategy.

 Organizational structure comprises the reporting relationships in business.

 Management processes comprise the activities such as planning and budgeting

that make the business run.



People comprise the human resources of an internationally engaged business
firm and include its managers and all other employees.

 Culture comprises the values and unwritten rules that guide and shapes the

behaviour of employees of an organization.

Each of these factors directly affects the others in formulating and implementing global
strategy. Each organizational factor and its elements have a distinct effect on the use of
each of the five global strategy levers, which are as follows:

 Market participation which involves the choice of country-markets in which to
conduct business and the level of activity, particularly in terms of market share.



Products/services involve the extent of product and market depth of a globally
engaged company.

 Location of value adding activities refers to locational advantages, the firm
enjoys for entire range of business activities in the global value chain including
where to locate each such activity.

 Marketing involves the extent to which a worldwide business uses the same
brand names, advertising and other marketing elements in different countries.

 Competitive moves involves the extent to which a global business makes
competitive moves in specific countries as part of a global competitive strategy.

Functional organization structure

A functional organization structure is one in which each type of work is performed in a
different department. For example, the company’s finances are looked after by the
finance department. Each division performs a set of work. For example, marketing
department looks after the marketing issues of the organization and so on. Each product
line or geographic region then makes use of these centralized resources treating each
department as if it is dealing with a separate external entity. (http://www.ehow.com,
2009). This helps the organization to have advantage of very standardized processes
for each of its functions, and have economies of scale. These organizations focus on
specialization of job skills, and are more centralized in nature. This type of organizational
structure works well for smaller and less complex organizations dealing especially with
the organizations that have only one or a few products or services. Furthermore, these
are operating in relatively stable environment and have consistent business strategies.

8.10    TRANSNATIONAL  STRUCTURE

This structure focuses on the management processes and culture. It follows the N-
form (network) in contrast to the M-form (multi-divisional). The reason for this is
that it stresses on issues like integration, combination, multiplication of resources
and capabilities. It also focuses on managing assets and core competence as a
network of alliances in contrast to geographical and functional division (Aboy, 2009).
A transnational model is basically a compromise between the local autonomy and
centralised decision-making whereby the organizations try to maintain a balance
between the pressures of global integration and the pressures of local responsiveness
(Raisinghani, 2009).

According to the transnational model there are four types of differentiated managerial
roles (Lasserre, 2003).

a) Global business managers

b) Country managers

c) Functional managers

d) Corporate managers

Since the model follows a hybrid structure, each managerial level has its own role.

The key features of transnational designs are:

1) Business units are part of a network which follows a principle of reciprocal

dependency.

2) All roles are important for competitive success and therefore no dimension

viz. global, functional or geographical dominates.

It follows a clearly defined and tightly controlled set of operating systems.

An alliance is the sharing of capabilities between two or more firms with the intention of
enhancing their competitive advantages or creating new business entity to achieve a
mutually beneficial business objective. Global alliance(s) are aimed either to develop a
global market presence or to enhance the global competitive capabilities of the firm.
Alliances exist both locally and globally depending on the expansion and diversification
plans of an organization. There exist mixed stories of success of such alliances both in
domestic and global businesses. Traditionally, such alliances have existed in the industrial
sectors like auto-components, textiles and food processing. Through the 60’s and
70’s India has been trying to forge joint ventures (JVs) between the local and foreign
counter parts. The Maruti (Government of India) and Suzuki Motor Company or the
Kwality (Delhi based ice-cream company) and Walls (Hindustan Unilever) tie-ups are
some of them. In most cases; where the business objectives are clear and coherent;
such alliances witnessed a success. The development of BrahMos Missile system, one
among the best in the world, is a result of pooling of technical expertise, capabilities
and financial resources of India and Russia. The export order for sale of BrahMos
Missile to Philippines valuing around 374 Million USD in 2021, is one among the largest
export order for any defence sector company in India. The missile also offers a strategic
deterrence to India for its own defence. Indian government has been vying to supply it to
other countries in Indo-Pacific region to achieve both strategic and business gains from
this collaboration of India and Russia (Shad Morris, Oldroyd & Ram Singh, 2021).

which have been forged. Similarly, some firms joined hands to produce cost
effectively in China and export it to the world markets.

the cross-functional teams which are formed and this helps it to scale new
heights.

9.2 CONCEPT OF STRATEGIC ALLIANCES

Strategic alliances are “voluntary agreements between firms for exchanging, sharing,
or co-developing of products, technologies or services. Strategic alliances are business-
oriented collaborations aimed for gaining competitive advantages and avail extended
ownership solutions for business.

Alliances are of two kinds, contractual and equity based. The former includes co-
marketing, research and development contracts, turnkey projects, strategic suppliers,
strategic distributors and licensing/franchising. They require lower commitments and
their scope is limited. Alternatively, equity based alliances require higher commitments,
and includes, strategic investment, cross shareholding and joint venture. Another important
aspect of strategic alliances are strategic networks, which are a kind of joint ventures
for gaining competitive grounds and gain business advantage from each other’s strengths.
The recent case of business alliance of Louis Vuitton and BMW is much in news as
both the industries are quite different but have something in common, the customers for
luxury product. They enter into a strategic alliance as both offer exclusive luxury brands
focused on craftsmanship. The logic is simple, one who can afford a BMW vehicle,
can probably also afford a Louis Vuitton bag. Hence; it is their shared audience and
values that has facilitated both the partners to join hands. Their recent ad-promotional
reminds of win-win when a model shows a Louis Vuitton bags with the BMW i8
sports car.

defining key parameters which will be important for organizations in future to decide
the potential partners.

9.3 TYPES  OF  STRATEGIC  ALLIANCES

Global alliances are more complex process for any internationally engaged firm. The
success or failure involves in global alliances entails far-larger stakes for any firm
economically and strategically. According to Doz and Hamel (1998) there are three
broad types of strategic alliances:

a) Coalition: This is an alliance of the competitors, distributors, and suppliers in
the same industry; pooling their resources and capabilities together in such a
manner that they are able to gain better hold of the global markets. For example,
STAR alliance network in the airlines industry is an example of coalition.

b) Co-specialisation: In this alliance, the unique capabilities are pooled together
for developing new products or technologies. In this case each partner
contributes to a unique asset, resource or competence. For instance, India
and  Japan  has  decided  in  2019  to  co-develop  5th  and  6th  generation
communication technologies, pooling strengths of each other wherein Japan
has specialization in IT Hardware and India’s core strengths lie in the

development of software. Similarly; Rolls-Royce and Hindustan Aeronautics
Limited (HAL) has signed an agreement in 2021 to manufacture parts for
Rolls-Royce’s Adour engines under ‘Make-in-India’ program of government
of India. Airbus and GE SNECMA are examples of such alliances.

c) Learning alliance: These alliances are formed for know-how transfers,
particularly, knowledge transfers. For example, Honda is spending $2.75 billion
to join General Motors’ autonomous vehicle unit Cruise Automation in 2017.
The endeavour is aimed at learning the futuristic ideas. Similarly, Mazda and
Toyota has entered into a business and capital alliance in 2017 to learn from
each other experience in manufacturing and create a new type of value for
future mobility. This includes projects for electrification, alternative eco-friendly
technologies and enhanced connectivity and commuting option for customers.

Alliances begin with the classic equity alliance or joint venture. It is formed when two
or more distinct firms pool a portion of their resources in a separate but jointly owned
organization. In such a joint venture or alliance, the equity stakes may be equal or one
of the partners may hold more than majority. These could be Greenfield Projects
(completely new investments) or a divisional merger whereby two separate divisions
of two partnering firms merge themselves to form an alliance. Alliances may take several
forms, namely, technology licensing, joint research and development, joint product
development and joint distribution and marketing. Unlike equity alliance, divisional
mergers are easy to form and dissolve, since partnering firms have minimum or no
stakes. The third kind of alliance, minority equity alliance, involves one parent company
making a minority investment in another. Minority equity alliances are used frequently
in industries which rely on high-end technology  and one of the companies is
technologically outdated or has obsolete technology and is intending to offer a minority
stake to a company for sharing their technology, technical or managerial know-how.
Industries like telecommunication, software and biotechnology are some of the places
where minority-stake investment strategies are commonly deployed or leveraged. The
minority equity alliance can be a hedge, in which the larger firms may have an option to
later purchase a majority equity stake in its smaller partner. Minority equity alliances
are also used when there are governmental restrictions on full acquisitions.

The type of alliance to be forged is also dependent on the scope (global or local) and
the objective (market access or capabilities enhancing) of the organization. Local alliance
is the one in which the object is for a foreign company to penetrate a local market or to
have access to a set of resource available in a particular country. A Global alliance is
the one in which the object would be either to develop a global market presence or to
enhance the worldwide competitive capabilities of the firms. There are three broad
types of global alliance. In nutshell, it can be concluded that coalition is an alliance of
competitors, distributors and suppliers in the same industry pooling their capabilities to
reduce cost or establish standards with the ultimate goal of developing market reach.
Co-specialisation is alliance of firms that join their respective unique capabilities to
complement each other in order to create a business or to develop new products/
technology. Lastly; for partners to transfer technical and managerial know-how or to
develop new competencies, a learning alliance is formed.

The first stage is the ‘Partner Assessment’ stage which involves analysing the strengths
and weaknesses of the potential partner and thereby creating strategies to accommodate
the management styles of different partners. The second stage is of ‘Contract
Negotiation’ which involves determining whether all the partners involved have realistic
objectives or not. It is further aimed at understanding and defining the contribution of
each partner. The third stage is that of ‘Alliance Operation’ which involves addressing
the commitment of senior management and finding the strengths of the resources devoted
to the alliance. It further entails the linking of budgets and resources having strategic
priorities. The fourth stage is the ‘Alliance Termination’ which involves winding-up the
alliance when either the objectives have been met or the objectives could not be met.
The final stage is the ‘Implementation and Management’ of Alliance which focuses on
the issues defining the success of the alliance. This involves a planned document whereby
who, how, what, when etc. are answered. The success and the failure of alliance depend
on how a particular venture is structured. A very good example of strategic alliance is

Partner Analysis

One of the strategic aspects of strategic alliances is partner analysis. Partner analysis
consists of strategic fit, capabilities fit, organizational fit and cultural fit. Strategic fit is
an analysis of compatibility amongst partners with respect to their explicit or implicit
strategic objectives. Strategic fit, therefore includes assessment of the criticality of the
alliance for the partners, the relative competitive position of partners and their
compatibility in strategic agendas.

The second aspect about capability fit implies competitive capabilities of the business.
It is a three step approach, which includes the determination of the organizational
resources, contribution in the value-chain of each partner and finally identifying the
gaps that might exist after the contribution is made. Cultural fit implies corporate, industry
and national culture. Out of the three any two, especially the industry and national
differences exist in most cases and partners must take into account such differences,
purely for operational reasons. The last, organizational fit refers to structures, systems
and procedures of an organization. Elements like decision making, documentation,
accounting, reporting, formalization and incentives are part of the process.

9.6 SUMMARY

The unit focuses on strategic alliance, which you should not confuse with mergers and
acquisitions. Buy-outs and takeovers are where one firm buys-out the stake in the
other firm, thereby gaining controlling authority. On the contrary, in alliances firms come
together to work jointly either on a ‘turnkey-basis’ or on a ‘rollout-basis’. Most often
when firms go-global and test waters in host country, alliances are one of the most
favoured options. It helps MNE’s gain knowledge and experience about the host
country environment along with learning on how to deal with market-specific nuances
of doing business.

A strategic alliance is a link between two or more firms that enhances the effectiveness
of the firm’s competitive strategies. An alliance is a voluntary arrangement between
two or more firms that involves the exchange, sharing or co-development of products,
technologies, or services. An alliance is, therefore, sharing of capabilities between two
or several firms, with the view of enhancing their competitive advantage or creating a
new business venture and without losing their respective strategic autonomy.

Alliance can be local or global in nature depending how firms decide to merge and do
business together. A local alliance is one where the object of the foreign company is to
penetrate a local market or to have access to a set of resources available in a particular
country. Global alliance is one where the objective is to develop market presence or to
enhance the global competitive capabilities of the firm. The three types of global strategic
