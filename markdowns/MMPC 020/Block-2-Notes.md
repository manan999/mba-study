### UNIT 5: CSR: AN OVERVIEW

#### 1. Concept of Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)
*   **Definition:** The commitment of business to contribute to sustainable economic development, working with employees, their families, the local community, and society at large to improve their quality of life.
*   **Evolution of Focus:** CSR has transitioned from voluntary corporate philanthropy (charities unconnected to business strategy) to an integrated, core corporate agenda.

#### 2. Shareholder Primacy vs. Stakeholder Primacy
*   **Shareholder Primacy (Milton Friedman):** Managers are agents of shareholders and have a fiduciary duty to maximize profits. Spending company money on social causes is seen as taxation without representation.
*   **Stakeholder Primacy (R. Edward Freeman):** A firm is a social institution. Stakeholders are any group or individual who can affect or is affected by the achievement of the organization's objectives.
*   **Classification of Stakeholders:**
    *   *Primary Stakeholders:* Crucial for the firm's survival (e.g., shareholders, employees, customers, suppliers).
    *   *Secondary Stakeholders:* Influence or are influenced by the firm but are not essential for its survival (e.g., media, CSOs, trade associations).

#### 3. Stakeholders as Drivers of CSR
*   Stakeholders have emerged as strong drivers of CSR through:
    1.  **Consumers:** Demand social consciousness and audit companies’ ethics before buying.
    2.  **Employees:** Prefer working for socially responsible organizations, boosting retention.
    3.  **Government:** Introduces regulations (e.g., Section 135 in India) to enforce CSR.
    4.  **Civil Society (CSOs):** Act as watchdogs, calling out environmental or social negligence.
    5.  **Investors (SRI/ESG):** Allocate capital to firms that demonstrate strong environmental, social, and governance standards.

#### 4. Corporate Citizenship
*   **Concept:** Corporate citizenship views the corporation as a member of society with associated rights and duties. It implies that businesses must behave responsibly, respect human rights, protect the environment, and contribute to the community.
*   It goes beyond philanthropy to position the corporation as a partner in solving public issues.

### UNIT 6: BUSINESS STRATEGY IN CSR

#### 1. Integrating CSR into Management Practices
*   **Peripheral CSR:** Separating CSR from the core business (e.g., outsourcing charity). This satisfies immediate stakeholder pressure but does not create long-term social or financial value.
*   **Integrated CSR:** Embedding CSR into core business activities, day-to-day operations, core competencies, and strategic decisions (integrating business and society).

#### 2. Four Strategic Approaches to CSR
1.  **Cost and Risk Reduction:** Mitigating external threats and reducing costs.
    *   *Trade-off Hypothesis (Friedman):* View that CSR incurs unnecessary costs and reduces profitability.
    *   *Slack Resources Theory:* Firms engage in CSR only when they have surplus financial resources (slack) and can afford the additional costs.
    *   *Enlightened Value Maximization:* Managing trade-offs between stakeholders to minimize risks (e.g., avoiding community opposition) and ensure long-term profit maximization.
2.  **Competitive Advantage:** Leveraging CSR opportunities to gain market superiority.
    *   *Strategic Philanthropy (Porter & Kramer):* Using philanthropy to improve the company's competitive context (e.g., Cisco training computer network administrators through its Academy).
    *   *Base of the Pyramid (BoP) Approach:* Creating products/services for the poorest socioeconomic groups, turning social challenges into business opportunities.
3.  **Reputation and Legitimacy:** Exploiting CSR to build brand value.
    *   *Social License to Operate:* Earning the community's trust and approval. Crucial in industries like mining and pulp manufacturing to avoid disruptions due to local protests.
    *   *Cause-Related Marketing (CRM):* Linking corporate donations to consumer transactions (e.g., donating a percentage of sales to charity) to boost sales, loyalty, and brand affinity.
4.  **Synergistic Value Creation:** Creating value for multiple stakeholders through value-based networks.
    *   *Sustainable Local Enterprise Network (SLEN):* Collaborative network of NGOs, local communities, and businesses working together to create economic and social value (e.g., **Honey Care Africa** and **Grameen Shakti**).

#### 3. Honey Care Africa & Grameen Shakti Case Studies
*   **Honey Care Africa (Kenya):** Partnered with rural farmers, NGOs, and microfinance institutions. Farmers bought hives through loans and sold honey at a guaranteed price to Honey Care. This model generated rural income, protected biodiversity (incentivizing reforestation), and attracted World Bank investments.
*   **Grameen Shakti (Bangladesh):** Operating like a social business, it provided solar energy systems to rural households using microloans. By partnering with Grameen Bank, it scaled green energy access, improving education and health (reducing biomass fuel use).

### UNIT 7: CSR IN GLOBAL CONTEXT

#### 1. CSR in Global Supply Chains
*   **The Issue:** Multinational Corporations (MNCs) outsource production to developing countries with weak regulations to cut labor costs.
*   **Ethical Challenges:** Exploitative working conditions (sweatshops), child labor, and human rights violations (e.g., Rana Plaza disaster in Bangladesh).
*   **Supply Chain CSR:** MNCs must implement strict Supplier Codes of Conduct and regular audits to trace materials, protect human rights, and safeguard their global reputation.

#### 2. Prevalent Forms of Social Activism
*   **Consumer Activism:** Boycotting brands involved in unethical practices (e.g., child labor, pollution) and choosing ethical/fair-trade products.
*   **CSO/NGO Activism:** Campaigns, media exposes, and legal filings to force corporate accountability.
*   **Shareholder Activism:** Investors filing resolutions or voting at AGMs to push corporate boards toward ESG compliance and sustainability.
*   **Employee Activism:** Employees organizing internal walkouts, petitions, or whistleblowing to protest corporate actions (e.g., selling technology to military groups).

#### 3. CSR and Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
*   The UN's 17 SDGs serve as a global framework for sustainable development.
*   MNCs align their CSR initiatives with SDGs to address global challenges like climate change, poverty, hunger, and gender inequality, facilitating international compliance and partnerships.

### UNIT 8: BUSINESS ETHICS AND CSR: LINKAGES

#### 1. Drawing the Linkages
*   **Business Ethics:** The conceptual core and philosophical foundation. It defines the values, morals, and standards (what is right vs. wrong).
*   **CSR:** The practical application and strategic manifestation of business ethics. It is the visible action taken by a firm to demonstrate its ethical commitments.
```
  [Business Ethics: Conceptual Core] ───► [CSR: Practical Action]
```

#### 2. Corporate Benefits of Ethical Codes and CSR
*   **Financial Performance:** A positive correlation exists between Corporate Social Performance (CSP) and Corporate Financial Performance (CFP) through intangible assets.
*   **Intangible Value Assets:**
    *   *Reputation:* Higher trust allows premium pricing and builds customer loyalty.
    *   *Talent Attraction:* Motivates employees, improves productivity, and reduces turnover costs.
    *   *Capital Access:* Attracts ESG-focused investors and lowers the cost of capital.
    *   *Risk Mitigation:* Prevents legal sanctions, public shaming, and operational disruptions.
