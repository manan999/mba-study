4.1  INTRODUCTION

In  practice  the  core  of  marketing  is  considered  to  be  the  marketing  mix.
Borden, while quoting from an article of Culliton, wrote that a marketer is
viewed as a “decider”, or an “artist” or a “mixer of ingredients” who plans
various means of competition. “He may follow a recipe prepared by others,
or prepare his own as he goes along, or adopt a recipe to the ingredients
immediately available, or experiment with or invent ingredients no one else
has tried.” If a marketer was a “mixer of ingredients”, what he designed was
a marketing mix.

Borden further wrote that “it was logical to proceed from a realization of the
existence of a variety of marketing mixes to the development of a concept
that  would  comprehend  not  only  this  variety,  but  also  the  market  forces
that cause managements to produce a variety of mixes. It is the problems
raised by these forces that lead marketing managers to exercise their wits in
devising mixes or programmes to fight competition.”

As discussed in Unit 2, the marketing mix in services includes 7 Ps. This
unit covers one of the marketing mix elements i.e., Product.

4.2  THE SERVICE PRODUCT

Product,  in  the  marketing  context  is  anything  which  is  offered  to  the
market for exchange or consumption. In goods marketing we always say
that  there  is  a  tangible  component  to  which  some  intangibles  like  style,
aftersales  service,  credit,  etc.,  are  integrated.  In  the  case  of  services,  on
the contrary, the tangible component is nil or minimal. Conventionally, we
describe  a  product  as  an  object,  which  is  developed,  produced,  delivered
and consumed. However, in services there is no or a little tangible element.
Therefore, the services are considered to be as benefits which are offered to
the target market. There are two important things to note. First, a service is
a bundle of features and benefits and secondly, these benefits and features
have relevance for a specific target market. Therefore, while developing a
service  product  it  is  important  that  the  package  of  benefits  in  the  service
offer must have a customer’s perspective.

level-1 The Basic Service Package

The ‘package’ concept of service product suggests that what you offer to the
market is a bundle of different services, tangible and intangible but there is
a main or substantive or ‘core’ service and around it are built the auxiliary
or peripheral or facilitator services. It is important to note that facilitating
services  are  mandatory,  and  if  they  are  left  out,  the  entire  service  would
collapse. In the service package there are yet other types of services called
supporting  services.  The  basic  difference  between  these  services  from
facilitating services is that these services do not facilitate the consumption
of core service, but are used to increase the value, and, thus, differentiate it
from competition.

For  example,  in  a  500-room  hotel  the  core  service  is  lodging  and  room
service,  bell boy service is facilitating service, and health club, car rental are
supporting  services. However, it may not be always possible to draw a line

in a typical city hotel, business center might be the supporting service, but
in a business and convention hotel, the same service would be facilitating
service.

Nevertheless, it is important while developing the service product package
to consider all the three levels of service: core, facilitating and supporting.

4.4  SERVICE BRANDING

Choosing a brand name for a consumer product or service is one of the most
important decisions. A well-chosen brand name can provide a number of
specific advantages to the organisation. These include suggesting product
benefits,  evoking  feeling  of  trust,  confidence,  security  and  simplifying
shopping. The American Marketing Association defines a brand as follows:
‘A brand is a name, term, sign, symbol, or design, or a combination of these,
intended to identify the goods or services of one seller or group of sellers
and  to  differentiate  them  from  those  of  competitors.’  Though  branding
has  received  considerable  attention  from  marketers  and  academicians,
the main focus has been on physical goods rather than services. However,
services branding has started to receive considerable attention lately. The
intangibility factor associated with services has led to the suggestion that
branding and image creation may be even more critical for services. For a
service brand to be effective it should possess distinctiveness, relevance and
memorability.

5.2  PRICING AND SERVICE
CHARACTERISTICS

In  determining  the  prices  of  services,  the  one  characteristic  which  has
great impact is their perishability and the fact that fluctuations in demand
cannot be met through inventory. Hotels and airlines offering low rates in
off-season are examples of how pricing strategy can be used to offset the
perishable characteristics of services.

Another  characteristic  of  services  that  creates  a  problem  in  price
determination  is  the  high  content  of  the  intangible  component.  The
higher the intangibility, the more difficult it is to calculate cost and greater
the  tendency  towards  non-  uniform  services,  such  as  fees  of  doctors,
management consultants, lawyers. In such cases, the price may sometimes
be settled through negotiation between the buyer and seller. On the other
hand, in services such as dry cleaning, the tangible component is higher, and
the service provided is homogeneous. It is easier to calculate the cost on a
unit basis and have a uniform pricing policy.

In general, the more unique a service the greater the freedom to fix the price
at any level. Often the price may be fixed according to the customer’s ability
to pay. In such cases price may be used as an indicator of quality.

The  third  characteristic  to  be  kept  in  mind  while  determining  prices  is
that in many services, the prices are subject to regulations, either by the
government  or  by  trade  associations.  Bank  charges,  electricity  and  water
rates, fare for rail and air transport in India are controlled by the government.
In many other cases, the trade or industry association may regulate prices in
order to avoid undercutting and to maintain quality standards. International
air fares are regulated by international agreement of airlines, similarly sea
freight fares are regulated. In all such cases, the producer has no freedom to
determine his own price.

5.4  PRICING OBjECTIVES

Pricing objectives serve as the foundation for pricing methods and policies,
and  a  better  comprehension  of  pricing  objectives  can  guide  a  company’s
entire  pricing  process.  Pricing  objectives  can  be  classified  based  on
their  content,  desired  level  of  achievement,  and  time  horizon.  Financial
indicators  such  as  profit,  sales,  market  share,  and  cost  coverage,  as  well
as  qualitative  factors  such  as  customer  and  competitor  relationships,  the
firm’s long-term survival, and the achievement of social goals, can all play a
role in developing pricing objectives for services. The primary objectives of
pricing can include making a profit and covering costs, increasing demand
and developing a user base, and supporting the firm’s positioning strategy.
Wirtz and Lovelock have classified the pricing objectives of services into
three basic categories: Revenue and profit objectives, Patronage and User
Base-Related  objectives  and  Strategy  related  objectives.  Let-us  describe
these pricing objectives.

Revenue and Profit Objectives

 A price is determined by taking into account both the profit and the cost
of  providing  services.  Furthermore,  profit  gain  objectives  may  include
maximising long-term profit, achieving a specific target level, or increasing
revenue from a fixed capacity by varying prices and target segments over
time. Cost coverage may include the following: costs of providing a single
service,  excluding  overhead;  fully  allocated  costs,  including  corporate
heads; and incremental costs of selling one additional unit or serving one
additional customer.

Patronage and User Base-Related Objectives

Price can be used effectively to build demand and develop a user base by
developing customer loyalty and relationships. Demand is often increased
to realise full capacity utilisation by increasing usage rates and providing
value to customers. The larger user base can take advantage of economies
of scale to gain a competitive cost advantage. By encouraging service trial
and adoption, a user base can be developed.

Strategy-Related Objectives

Pricing can help with positioning and differentiation strategies. For example,
as a price leader or a premium-quality provider. Pricing can also be used to
support the firm’s competitive strategy. It can be used to discourage potential
new competitors from entering the market, as well as to discourage existing
competitors from expanding capacity.

These  objectives  provide  a  sense  of  direction  for  a  service  firm’s  price
setting.  These  should  be  adaptable  and  change  over  time  in  response  to
changing  environmental  conditions  and  market  characteristics  such  as:

monopolistic competition), degree of governmental intervention, barriers to
entry and exit from the market, rate of technological change, and so on.

Cost- Based Pricing

Cost-based pricing assumes that the cost of a unit of output can be determined
and that a certain profit element is added to that to determine the price. The
basic formula for cost-based pricing is-

Price = Fixed Cost + Overhead Cost + Profit Margin

However, the issue with cost-based pricing in services is that determining
the  cost  of  each  unit  of  service  is  difficult.  Because  the  unit  of  output
for  services,  unlike  goods,  is  not  a  physical  product  unit.  For  example,
consultancy,  education,  and  legal  services  are  sold  in  terms  of  time.  As
a result, the valid unit for service pricing is input units, such as hours of
training or personnel time required to provide services. Cost-based pricing
is  used  in  services  such  as  advertising,  consulting,  utilities,  contracts,
project works (construction and engineering) where costs must be predicted
in advance. The service provider calculates the cost of services by adding
up material, labour, and overhead costs, and then they add a profit margin.
Professionals like consultants, psychologists, accountants, lawyers, etc. use
the  fee-for-service  pricing  model,  which  is  based  on  the  amount  of  time
required to offer the service.

For  service  firms  with  high  fixed  costs  and  product  lines  with  shared
infrastructure, such as retail banking, air services, and hospitals, the more
complex activity-based costing (also known as “ABC”) approach may be
worth considering. It is a more precise method of allocating indirect costs
(i.e., overheads) to specific product lines and services. When calculating the
indirect cost of a service, a firm considers the resources required to perform
each activity associated with the service, and then allocates the indirect cost
to  the  service  based  on  the  quantities  and  types  of  activities  required  to
perform the service.

Competition-Based Pricing

The main characteristics that trigger pricing decisions in competition-based
pricing are competitors’ prices and actions. Under this approach, firms have
three main options: fix the price at the level that the competitor is charging,
price above the competition, or price lower to increase market share. The
decision is influenced by the extent to which their services differ from those
of their competitors, the level of competition, and the firm’s market position.

Similar  service-selling  companies  frequently  use  it.  Telecom  service
providers, for instance, frequently employ this strategy because consumers
frequently compare switching costs between competitors and the services are
generally comparable in terms of offers. Other examples include platform-
based taxi services and airline services. In these industries, where a small
number  of  major  players  dominate,  when  one  service  provider  changes
(lowers or raises) the price, others promptly match the change in price to
that of their competitor.

Value-Based Pricing

When determining the price to be charged, the value-based pricing approach
takes the customer’s perspective into account. The price to be charged is
determined by the customer’s perception of the value that a product or service
provides  and  what  they  are  willing  to  pay  for  that  value.  The  perceived
value of a service varies depending on the customer group. The four most
important value perceptions among service customers and suitable pricing
strategies for these group of customers are being discussed in this section.

1.   Value  as  low  Price:  Some  customers  may  perceive  value  if  the

Discount  Pricing  refers  to  the  practice  of  offering  a  commission

Psychological  Pricing  is  used  to  influence  consumers  by  setting

customers  the  psychological  impression  that  they  are  receiving
services at a low cost.

Penetration Pricing refers to the practise of introducing a service at
a low initial price in order to attract the price-sensitive market. For
example, Reliance Jio Infocomm Limited (RJIL) entered the Indian

price but one which includes no soft drink or sweet dish. Once the customer
is attracted because of the initial low price he may be tempted to buy a drink
or an ice-cream or an additional dish. Thus, he may end up buying more
than just the basic meal.

Guaranteed Pricing refers to pricing strategy in which payment is to be
made only after the results are achieved. Employment agencies charge their
fee only when a person actually gets a job, a property dealer charges his
commission only after the deal is actually transacted.

High Price Maintenance Pricing strategy is used when the high price is
associated with the quality of the service. Many doctors, lawyers and other
professionals follow this pricing strategy.

loss  leader  Pricing  strategy  is  one  in  which  a  low  initial  price  is
charged in the hope of gaining more business at later better prices. Some
supplementary services are generally provided at a very low cost in order to
attract customers to the service outlet or website. The loss on some offers
is offset by the increased turnover generated by this strategy. For example,
the launch of Jio.

Offset Pricing is quite similar to diversionary pricing in which a basic low
price is quoted but the extra services are rather highly priced. A gynaecologist
may charge a low fee for the nine months of pregnancy through which she
regularly  checks  her  patient,  but  many  charge  extra  for  performing  the
actual delivery and post-delivery visits.

Captive Pricing refers to providing a basic service or product as well as
ongoing  supplies  or  peripheral  services  as  needed.  In  the  case  of  a  DTH
(Direct-to-Home) service, the set-up box is provided for a price or a deposit,
and subsequent monthly payments for the channels of choice are collected.

Two-part Pricing: The price of the services is divided into two parts under
this strategy. In the case of a DTH service, the basic package is compulsory,
and the second part is determined by the use of add-on pack offers, VOD
(Video on Demand), and interactive services.

6.2  PlACE OR DISTRIBUTION

Distribution or place decisions play an important role in service delivery.
The  primary  goal  of  service  distribution  is  to  make  services  easily  and
conveniently  available  to  consumers.  Service  characteristics  such  as
intangibility,  inseparability,  variability,  and  perishability,  as  well  as  the
levels of service products (core or peripheral), all contribute to the design of
effective and efficient distribution decisions. Wirtz and lovelock (2022) have
suggested  three  modes  of  service  distribution  or  transactions:  Customers
visit the service site, Service providers go to their customers, and Service
transactions are conducted remotely.

93

Customers visit the service site: In this transaction, customers must visit

theatres, health clubs, etc.

Service providers go to their customers: For some services, the service

Service  transactions  are  conducted  remotely:  In  remote  transaction,

Distribution Strategy

Depending  on  the  nature  of  the  service  products,  the  market,  and  the
objectives,  service  organisations  can  employ  one  of  three  distribution
strategies:  extensive  distribution,  selective  distribution,  or  exclusive
distribution.

Extensive  distribution  makes  services  available  to  as  many  people  as
possible  by  utilising  a  larger  distribution  network.  Banking  services,  for
example,  are  moving  toward  intensive  distribution  through  the  use  of
Internet and mobile banking, as well as retail branches.

Exclusive  distribution  offers  services  through  a  very  limited  number
of  outlets  that  are  specifically  designed  for  a  specific  customer  segment.
Following  an  exclusive  distribution  strategy,  highly  specialised  and  non-
substitutable  services  are  provided.  For  example,  specialised  coaching
services for professional education and administrative services; specialised
services provided by a hospital; and entertainment theme parks.

Selective  distribution  strategies  fall  somewhere  between  extensive  and
exclusive  distribution  strategies.  For  example,  when  a  company  states  in
its  communication  that  a  product  is  only  available  in  a  limited  number
of outlets, the company is using selective distribution. Many services are
selectively  distributed  through  franchise  models  or  the  establishment  of
multi-site operations. Certain hotel chains, for example, Ginger, selectively
reach out to the market in this manner. PVR, an entertainment theatre chain,
builds theatres in catchment areas on a selective basis.

6.6  SUMMARY

  Distribution  or  place  decisions  are  important  in  the  service  industry  as
these decisions relate to location, delivery, and coverage. Three modes of
service  transactions  include:  customers  visiting  the  service  site;  service
providers going to their customers; or service transactions being conducted
remotely. Depending on the nature of the service products, the market, and
the objectives, service organisations can employ one of three distribution
strategies:  extensive  distribution,  selective  distribution,  or  exclusive
distribution. When it comes to the distribution of services, the channels are

Services  can  also  be  distributed  through  intermediaries  like  agents  or
brokers. Furthermore, if the distribution system is properly designed, it can
be used as a truly effective competitive edge.

7.4  CHAllENGES OF SERVICE

COMMUNICATIONS

Let  us  look  at  some  of  the  promotion  challenges  that  service  firms  face
when designing communications messages.

Service Intangibility:

The  intangibility of  services  causes  issues  for  customers  both  before  and
after  they  purchase  them.  Consumers  have  difficulty  understanding  what
they will be purchasing and evoking names and types of services to consider
before purchasing services. During the purchasing process, consumers are
frequently unable to distinguish between services. Consumers have difficulty

107

Promotion Decisionsevaluating their service experiences after purchase. Intangibility, according to
Banwari Mittal, causes five problems for service marketing communication:
incorporeal existence, abstractness, generality, nonsearchability, and mental
impalpability.

Incorporeal existence: It is difficult to demonstrate the service because it
is  not  made  of  physical  matter  and  does  not  occupy  physical  space. The
delivery mechanism occupies space, but the service does not.

Abstractness:  It is difficult to visualise and comprehend the service benefits
of  financial  security,  entertainment,  and  health  because  these  concepts  of
service do not have a one-to-one correspondence with physical objects. As
a result, service providers may find it difficult to connect their services to
intangible concepts.

Generality: Many services and service promises are described in generalities
(amazing  experience,  superior  quality,  completely  satisfied  customers),
making  it  difficult  to  distinguish  them  from  competitors’  offerings.
Specificity refers to specific objects, people, or events, whereas generality
refers to a class of things, people, events, or properties.

  Nonsearchability:  It  refers  to  the  inability  of  intangibles  to  be  searched
or  inspected  prior  to  purchase.  In  the  case  of  healthcare  services,  for
example, physical service attributes such as the appearance of a hospital and
medical facilities can be checked in advance, but the personal experience of
healthcare services can only be determined after receiving treatment from
the doctors.

Mental impalpability: Many services are often complex, multidimensional,
or  novel,  and  consumers,  particularly  new  prospects,  find  it  difficult  to
interpret and understand what the experience will be like and what benefits
will result.

7.6  INTEGRATED SERVICE MARkETING

COMMUNICATIONS

As discussed earlier in this unit, today’s consumers receive communications
from  a  variety  of  channels,  such  as  websites,  social  media,  mobile
advertising, blogs, virtual communities, direct mail, e-mail solicitation, and
a  host  of  sales  promotions.  Servicescapes,  customer  service  departments,
and  everyday  service  encounters  with  employees  all  provide  additional
communication  to  service  consumers. These  service  interactions  increase
the variety, volume, and complexity of information received by a customer.
Keeping messages consistent across all of these channels necessitates the
adoption of integrated service marketing communication (ISMC).

ISMC’s  role  is  to  coordinate  with  various  departments  responsible  for
various  aspects  of  a  company’s  service  market  communications.  For
example,  the  marketing  department  in  association  with  an  advertising
agency is in charge of advertising; the PR department of public relations;
functional  specialists  are  in  charge  of  a  company’s  website  and  its  direct
marketing  and  promotions  activities;  operations  personnel  for  customer
service; and human resources for training. ISMC connects and reinforces
communication so that everyone involved understands both the company’s
marketing strategy and its consumer promises.

7.7  SUMMARY

It  is  now  widely  accepted  that  marketing  of  services  requires  a  different
emphasis  on  promotion  than  marketing  goods.  The  intangibility  of
services  causes  issues  for  customers  both  before  and  after  they  purchase
them.  Other  service  communications  challenges  include  management  of
service promises, customer expectations, customer education, and internal
marketing communication. Along with the traditional promotional mix of
advertising, public relations (PR), publicity, and personal selling, the internet
and  online  communication  channels  play  an  important  role  in  Service
marketing communications. In order to achieve effective coordination and
consistent message delivery, traditional and online promotion mixes must be
effectively synchronised by implementing an integrated service marketing
communication strategy.
