13.2 WHAT IS BIG DATA

When we search for something on search engines like Google, Yahoo, and
Bing, they know what we have searched for. Similarly, every time we access
a computer, use a phone, or open a mobile app, we leave a digital footprint
and add to the generation of data. So, now you can imagine how much data
is being generated. An estimated 2.5 quintillion bytes (2.5 x 1018) of data
are produced every day. This rate of data creation will increase as the IoT
(Internet of Things) gains popularity.

Customer  databases,  transaction  processing  systems,  documents,  emails,
internet clickstream logs, mobile apps, and social networks are just a few
examples of the many sources of big data. Additionally, it contains data that
is produced by machines, including log files from servers and networks, as
well as data collected from sensors on machines used for manufacturing,
business, and the internet of things. Along with data from internal systems,
big  data  settings  usually  include  external  data  on  consumers,  financial
markets, and other topics. Along with data from internal systems, big data
settings usually include external data on consumers, financial markets, and
other topics of interest. Big data can also include audio, video, and image
files, and many big data applications use streaming data, which is analyzed
and gathered continuously.

Big  data  can  be  understood  same  as  data  but  it  is  present  in  very  huge
amount and number. It consists of the information provided by both humans
and computer. Big data is so large and complex in its size and storage that
it  is  unable  to  be  decoded  and  understood  efficiently  by  the  traditional
data management tools. In simple words, big data can be defined as data
that has a wider variety, arrives at a faster rate, and arrives in higher
quantities.  Such  data  can  be  collected  through  various  sources  such  as
blogs,  comments,  app  permissions,  online  surveys,  virtual  check-ins  on
e-commerce portals etc. Big data is often stored in computer databases and
it is analyzed using specialized software.

13.3 TYPES OF BIG DATA

Big data can be divided on different types. Let us explain it.

Structured Data

Structural  data  is  defined  as  data  that  can  be  collected,  saved,  retrieved,
and processed in a specific format.  It is simpler to organize and analyze
structured data since it has some predetermined characteristics. Since each
field is distinct, data from other fields can also be retrieved separately from
it. Numbers and letters that are exactly aligned with the rows and columns
of tables make up structured data. Data in an Excel sheet that is marked in
a consistent manner and is simple to sort, update, edit, and extract is one
example of structured data. Additional examples include sales records, train
or airplane reservation systems, transactions, financial data, etc. Due to the
data’s same format, organizations derive the most value from it. Because of
this, structured data is very beneficial.

13.5 BIG DATA AND MARKETING RESEARCH

As we discussed the stages of the marketing research process in unit 1, we
learnt that the purpose of marketing research has been to identify research
problems from decision problems and, accordingly, collect data and analyze
it into information and insights that are useful to decision makers. However,
big data has made it more challenging for marketers to recognize and assess
markets.  Big  data  settings  have  an  effect  on  how  marketing  research  is
conceptualized,  necessitating  the  adoption  of  a  larger  perspective  as  big
data analytics become more common. In this section first we will discuss
the  key  differences  of  big  data  analytics  and  marketing  research  then  we
will explain the process of extracting insights from big data.

You  may  be  able  to  forecast  Mr.  Sanjeev’s  future  behavior  based  on
the fact that he has been getting his groceries delivered on Wednesdays
between  4  and  6  p.m.  for  the  past  3  years,  as  shown  by  your  big  data
analytics. You might even target him with offers based on the items he
frequently purchases by using the knowledge of what’s on his shopping
list. However, big data analytics cannot tell you why a customer shops in a
particular manner. Because people and their environments change through
both time and space, the predictions we can make using big data do not
take  unusual  behavior  into  consideration.  Again,  using  Mr.  Sanjeev’s
example, you would never understand why he chose to shop one week
on Friday morning as opposed to Wednesday afternoon, or why he is not
shopping for a few weeks or months. Market research can help with that.
Marketing research comes to the rescue in this situation.

13.6 GAINING INSIGHTS FROM BIG DATA

As we have seen in the previous section, the emergence of big data is gradually
reshaping and complementing the research process. Big data implies high
dimensionality  and  a  significant  sample  size.  These  two  characteristics
present  three  special  difficulties:  High  dimensionality  causes  issues  like
noise  accumulation,  erroneous  correlations,  and  incidental  homogeneity;
high  dimensionality  combined  with  a  large  sample  size  results  in  issues
like  high  computational  cost  and  algorithmic  instability;  and  the  massive
samples in big data are typically combined from various sources at various
times using various technologies. We must therefore design more flexible
and  reliable  processes  because  this  raises  concerns  about  heterogeneity,
experimental variance, and statistical biases. To process big data, big data
analytics combines technologies including database and data mining tools,
as well as analytical approaches, to evaluate large-scale, complicated data
for  a  variety  of  applications  designed  to  improve  business  performance

technologies. We must therefore design more flexible and reliable processes
because  this  raises  concerns  about  heterogeneity,  experimental  variance,
and statistical biases.

15.6 SOCIAL MEDIA RESEARCH INSIGHT

The usage of social media by students for academic purposes:

Every  aspect  of  life  has  been  impacted  by  social  media  and  its  content,
whether it takes the shape of text, photographs, videos, or any other format,
thanks to the advancement of media and the internet in our daily lives. The
educational sector has a similar situation. In addition to books and teachers
who are physically present, technology has created a number of new learning
opportunities.  Prior  to  the  widespread  adoption  of  technology,  children’
lives were governed by chalk and duster, actual classrooms, students, and
teachers. Traditional schooling was once seen as the best option. One could
say that a teacher’s value in a student’s life cannot be overstated, and this
is true in all respects. However, though a teacher’s role in a student’s life
can never be replaced, technology has changed how students interact with a
teacher and makes a big difference. Social media and its content, as well as
the availability of resources on the internet, have done just that. Social media
and the internet have eventually evolved as  a  new  industry of  their own,
changing the interface of traditional study materials and their distribution.

Today’s students can utilize the internet to share knowledge and pick up skills
from anywhere in the world without having to leave their home country or
even the countryside. One can connect and deliver lectures using the same
technology.   The way people used to feel about sharing their knowledge
has also altered as a result of the advent of blogging and YouTube. To share
their valuable information with students around the world, many professors
and specialists are turning to these two for assistance.
