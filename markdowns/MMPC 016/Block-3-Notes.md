### UNIT 7: INTERNATIONAL ENTRY STRATEGIES

#### 1. Modes of Entry into Foreign Markets
When expanding globally, a firm must select an entry mode that balances control, risk, and resource commitment:
1.  **Exporting:**
    *   *Indirect Exporting:* Selling through domestic-based exporters/agents. Low risk, low control, minimal resource commitment.
    *   *Direct Exporting:* Handling own exports through overseas distributors or agents. Better control and market feedback, but higher cost.
2.  **Contractual Modes:**
    *   *Licensing:* Granting a foreign firm the right to use intellectual property (patents, copyrights, technology) for a royalty fee. Low risk and capital requirement, but risks creating a future competitor and offers zero operational control.
    *   *Franchising:* A specialized form of licensing where the franchisor provides the brand name, business model, and operating systems (e.g., McDonald's, Subway) in exchange for fees. Rapid expansion potential, but quality control can be difficult.
3.  **Investment Modes (FDI):**
    *   *International Joint Venture (IJV):* Establishing a new entity jointly owned by two or more independent parent firms (one local, one foreign). Offers access to local partner’s market knowledge, shared risks/costs, and regulatory compliance. However, it suffers from potential partner conflicts and shared profits.
    *   *Wholly Owned Subsidiary (WOS):* 100% ownership of foreign assets.
        *   *Greenfield Investment:* Building a brand-new facility from scratch. Allows customized setup but is slow and high-risk.
        *   *Acquisition (M&A):* Buying an existing local firm. Fast entry and immediate market access, but integration of organizational cultures is complex.

#### 2. Factors Influencing the Choice of Entry Mode
The decision is shaped by a mix of internal (firm-specific) and external (environment-specific) factors:

| Factor Type | Variable | Impact on Entry Mode Choice |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Internal Factors** | **Firm Size & Resources** | Large firms with abundant resources prefer high-commitment modes (FDI/WOS). Small firms prefer low-commitment modes (Exporting/Licensing). |
| | **International Experience** | High experience reduces perceived risk, encouraging direct entry or FDI. |
| | **Product Complexity / Tech** | High-tech or proprietary products require high control (WOS) to protect intellectual property from leakage. |
| **External Factors** | **Market Size & Growth** | Large, high-growth markets justify high-commitment modes (FOS/JV). Small markets are served via exporting. |
| | **Government Policies & Tariffs**| High tariffs or investment restrictions push firms toward Licensing, JVs, or local manufacturing (FDI) instead of exporting. |
| | **Socio-Cultural Distance** | High cultural differences increase uncertainty, making Joint Ventures or Licensing attractive to leverage local partner expertise. |

#### 3. Theories of Internationalization
*   **The Uppsala Model (Stages Model):** Argues that internationalization is an incremental process of learning and commitment. Firms start by exporting to *psychically close* countries (similar language, culture, legal systems) and gradually move to psychically distant markets. They start with low-commitment modes (agents) and scale up to sales subsidiaries and manufacturing plants as they gain experiential knowledge.
*   **The Network Model:** Views internationalization as a process of building, maintaining, and developing relationships within global business networks. A firm's international expansion is driven by its network partners rather than sequential stages.
*   **Born-Global / International Entrepreneurship Theory:** Challenges the Uppsala model. Refers to firms (often high-tech startups) that internationalize rapidly from or near their inception. They leverage digital platforms, niche global demands, and entrepreneurial networks to bypass incremental stages.

### UNIT 8: ORGANIZATIONAL STRUCTURES

#### 1. Organizational Factors & Elements
To execute a global strategy successfully, firms must align four core organizational elements:
1.  **Structure:** The formal division of tasks, reporting relationships, and coordination mechanisms.
2.  **Management Processes:** Planning, budgeting, information flows, and performance appraisal systems.
3.  **People:** Human resources, recruitment, expatriation policies, and training.
4.  **Culture:** Shared values, norms, and behaviors within the MNE.

#### 2. Types of International Organizational Structures
*   **International Division Structure:** A separate division handles all foreign activities. Domestic divisions remain structured by product or function. Used in early stages of internationalization; prevents foreign operations from being neglected, but creates conflict between domestic and international managers.
*   **Global Functional Structure:** Organized around core business functions (Production, Marketing, R&D, Finance).
    *   *Characteristics:* High centralization; all key decisions made at HQ. Country managers have minimal operational autonomy.
    *   *Pros:* High specialization, operational efficiency, economies of scale, avoids duplication.
    *   *Cons:* Fails when markets are highly diverse and require rapid, local adaptation.
*   **Geographic (Area) Division Structure:** Organized by regions (e.g., Asia-Pacific, Europe, Americas).
    *   *Characteristics:* High decentralization. Country/Regional managers have complete autonomy over local marketing and operations.
    *   *Pros:* Excellent local responsiveness and customization to local tastes.
    *   *Cons:* Duplication of functional activities across regions, leading to high overheads; hard to maintain global product standardization.
*   **Multi-Business Global Product Division Structure:** Organized by product lines. Each division has global responsibility for its product.
    *   *Pros:* Focuses on product innovation and global cost standardization.
    *   *Cons:* Duplication of functional resources within each product division.
*   **Hybrid Structure:** Combines different structural designs (e.g., using a functional structure for R&D but a geographic structure for marketing) to suit complex, multi-product global businesses.
*   **Transnational Structure:** A highly integrated network model (N-form) built to balance global efficiency, local responsiveness, and organizational learning.
    *   *Characteristics:*
        *   Assets and resources are dispersed but mutually interdependent.
        *   No single dimension (geography, product, function) dominates.
        *   Uses dual reporting lines and inter-unit decision-making forums.
        *   Relies heavily on a strong corporate culture of collaboration and sharing.
    *   *Limitation:* Extremely management-intensive; requires a collaborative mindset rather than rigid structural boxes.

### UNIT 9: STRATEGIC ALLIANCES

#### 1. Concept of Strategic Alliances
A strategic alliance is a voluntary, cooperative agreement between independent firms to share resources, capabilities, or technologies to achieve mutual strategic goals while remaining separate legal entities.

#### 2. Types of Strategic Alliances
*   **Equity-Based Alliances:**
    *   *International Joint Ventures (IJVs):* Partners create a third, legally independent company, contributing equity and sharing management control (e.g., Sony-Ericsson).
    *   *Minority Equity Alliances:* One partner takes a minority equity stake in the other to cement a long-term commercial relationship.
*   **Non-Equity (Contractual) Alliances:**
    *   Partners cooperate through contracts without sharing equity or creating a new firm.
    *   *Examples:* R&D agreements, co-marketing/cross-distribution partnerships, contract manufacturing, and licensing.

#### 3. Strategic Value & Sources of Value Creation
Strategic alliances create value through:
1.  **Complementary Resource Synergy:** Combining different, non-overlapping strengths (e.g., one partner brings advanced technology, while the other brings local distribution and government relations).
2.  **Risk and Cost Sharing:** Sharing heavy R&D costs or capital investments in volatile industries.
3.  **Speed to Market:** Bypassing slow, organic development by leveraging a partner's established infrastructure.
4.  **Learning and Technology Transfer:** Gaining access to new operational skills, managerial practices, or technical processes.
5.  **Regulatory Navigation:** Partnering with a local firm to enter restricted sectors (e.g., defense, banking, or aviation in certain countries).

#### 4. Stages of Alliance Formation
The lifecycle of establishing an alliance involves four major phases:

    A[1. Partner Analysis & Strategy] --> B[2. Negotiation & Contract]
    B --> C[3. Implementation & Management]
    C --> D[4. Evaluation & Exit/Restructuring]

1.  **Partner Analysis & Strategic Fit:**
    *   Defining the firm's strategic objectives and motives for partnering.
    *   Scanning and evaluating potential partners.
    *   **Strategic Fit:** Aligning goals, resources, capabilities, and organizational cultures.
2.  **Negotiation & Contract Design:**
    *   Determining the structure of the alliance (Equity vs. Non-equity).
    *   Drafting the contract: allocating ownership stakes, management roles, operational responsibilities, and profit-sharing ratios.
    *   Establishing dispute resolution mechanisms and exit clauses (termination guidelines).
3.  **Implementation & Management:**
    *   Setting up operational workflows, communication channels, and joint steering committees.
    *   Building trust and relational capital.
    *   Managing cultural integration (bridging differences in corporate and national cultures).
4.  **Evaluation & Exit/Restructuring:**
    *   Monitoring performance against initial goals.
    *   Restructuring, expanding, or winding down the alliance based on performance or changing strategic priorities.
