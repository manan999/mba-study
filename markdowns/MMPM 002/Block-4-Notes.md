### Unit 11: Sales Planning, Forecasting and Budgeting

#### 1. Sales Planning Process
Sales planning is the process of setting sales objectives, identifying market opportunities, and mapping out the activities required to achieve revenue goals. It operates as a three-stage loop within the **MOST** (Mission, Objectives, Strategies, Tactics) framework:

```
[ Stage 1: Sales Forecasting & Budgeting ]
                  │
                  ▼
[ Stage 2: Territory Design & Allocation ]
                  │
                  ▼
[ Stage 3: Setting Quotas & Performance Standards ]
                  │
                  └─► (Feedback for control & re-forecasting)
```

*   **Strategic vs. Tactical Sales Planning**:
    *   *Strategic Planning*: Long-term resource commitment (e.g., determining sales force size, entering new geographic markets).
    *   *Tactical Planning*: Short-term operational planning (e.g., product-wise monthly sales targets, customer-wise call routing plans).

#### 2. Key Terms in Sales Demand Estimation
It is critical to distinguish between different levels of market demand:
*   **Market Potential**: The maximum expected sales of a product/service for an *entire industry* in a specified market during a stated period, under ideal marketing efforts.
*   **Sales Potential**: The maximum share of the market potential that an *individual company* can realistically expect to achieve under ideal conditions.
*   **Sales Forecast**: The expected sales (in value or units) that a company expects to achieve during a future period under a *proposed marketing plan* (conditions are realistic, not just ideal).
*   **Sales Quota / Target**: A quantitative performance standard assigned to a sales unit (e.g., salesperson, branch) to motivate and evaluate them.

#### 3. Sales Forecasting Techniques

| Method Category | Forecasting Technique | Operational Description | Pros | Cons |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Qualitative / Judgmental** | **Executive Opinion** | Consensus of senior executives from sales, finance, and marketing. | Fast, utilizes experienced management intuition. | Prone to groupthink, lacks objective statistical basis. |
| | **Sales Force Composite** | Grassroots approach where each salesperson estimates future sales in their territory, which are then aggregated. | Capitalizes on direct customer/field contact; highly motivating. | Salespeople may deliberately lowball estimates to get easy quotas. |
| | **Delphi Method** | Panel of anonymous experts answer questionnaires in successive rounds. A coordinator synthesizes replies to build consensus. | Eliminates peer pressure and dominant personality bias. | Time-consuming; requires cooperative external experts. |
| | **Survey of Buyer Intentions** | Direct surveys asking customers about their buying plans for the upcoming period. | Highly accurate for industrial B2B markets with few buyers. | Less reliable for consumer markets where buyers change minds. |
| **Quantitative / Statistical** | **Moving Averages** | Uses the arithmetic average of a set number of past periods to predict the next period. | Easy to compute; smoothes out random fluctuations. | Lags behind rapid trends or seasonal shifts; gives equal weight to old data. |
| | **Exponential Smoothing** | A weighted moving average where more weight is given to the most recent data using a smoothing constant ($\alpha$). | Requires minimal historical data; highly responsive to recent changes. | Selecting the optimal smoothing constant requires trial and error. |
| | **Regression Analysis** | Establishes a mathematical relationship between sales (dependent variable) and economic indicators/promotional spend (independent variables). | Explains cause-and-effect relationships; statistically objective. | Complex; requires reliable external data indicators. |
| **Experimental** | **Market Testing** | Introducing the product in a limited geographic test market to observe actual consumer buying behavior. | Provides actual behavioral data; highly reliable for new launches. | Expensive, time-consuming, and alerts competitors. |

#### 4. Sales Forecasting for New Products
Forecasting sales for a brand-new product category is challenging due to the **absence of historical data**. Recommended approaches include:
1.  **Market Testing**: Conducting pilot launches in select cities to measure initial trial and repeat purchase rates.
2.  **Survey of Buyer Intentions**: Assessing concept acceptance and purchase intent through focus groups and prototype demonstrations.
3.  **Analogous Product Method**: Drawing parallels from the historical sales curves of similar or related products launched in the past.
4.  **Delphi Method**: Leveraging expert panels to predict market adoption rates.

#### 5. Role & Importance of Forecasting in Sales Planning
*   **Production & Inventory Control**: Prevents stockouts or costly inventory build-ups.
*   **Budgetary Allocation**: Determines cash flow requirements and expenditure limits.
*   **Salesforce Management**: Directs territory planning, quota setting, and sales force sizing.
*   **Recession Planning**: During recessions, accurate forecasting helps identify resilient market niches, optimize resource allocation, and plan defensive cost-reduction strategies to maintain viability.

#### 6. Sales Budgeting & Phasing
*   **Sales Budget**: A financial plan detailing the projected sales revenue and the budgeted expenses (sales force salaries, commissions, travel, promotions, administrative) required to achieve it.
*   **Bottom-Up vs. Top-Down Budgeting**:
    *   *Top-Down*: Top management allocates budget limits to departments; quick but can be unrealistic.
    *   *Bottom-Up*: Field managers build budgets based on operational costs, which are aggregated and approved; realistic but time-consuming.
*   **Budget Phasing**: Dividing the annual sales budget into proportional monthly or quarterly chunks (expressed in value, units, or percentages) to account for market seasonality.
*   **Budgetary Control & Deviations**: The continuous monitoring of actual sales and expenses against the phased budget. A **deviation** (variance) flags operational issues:
    *   *Positive sales deviation* could indicate a market surge or successful campaign.
    *   *Negative sales deviation / Over-budget expenses* trigger immediate corrective action to protect net profit margins.

### Unit 12: Territory Management and Sales Quota

#### 1. Sales Territories
*   **Definition**: A customer group, industry category, or geographic area assigned to an individual salesperson.
*   **Need & Goals for Carving Territories**:
    1.  **Proper Market Coverage**: Ensures all existing and potential customers are visited systematically.
    2.  **Effective Salesforce Deployment**: Matches the salesperson's unique capabilities with specific territory requirements.
    3.  **Efficient Customer Service**: Builds long-term relations, leading to regular call schedules and high responsiveness.
    4.  **Objective Evaluation**: Compares performance against the unique potential of the territory, eliminating bias.
    5.  **Improving Productivity**: Reduces travel time, coordinates local promotional activities, and lowers cost-per-call.

#### 2. Scientific Steps in Territory Planning
An effective territory design follows a systematic process:

```
[ Step 1: Assess Salesperson Capacity ]
                 │
                 ▼
[ Step 2: Set Customer Call Frequency (Categorize A/B/C) ]
                 │
                 ▼
[ Step 3: Establish Parity in Sales Potential ]
                 │
                 ▼
[ Step 4: Minimize Travel Time (Design Territory Shape) ]
                 │
                 ▼
[ Step 5: Suitably Station the Representative (Headquarters) ]
```

*   **Step 1: Salesperson's Capacity**: Calculate how many customers a rep can visit daily, multiplied by net working days (e.g., 8 calls/day $\times$ 25 days = 200 accounts/month).
*   **Step 2: Frequency of Calls**: Classify accounts:
    *   *Class A (High potential)*: Visited twice a month.
    *   *Class B (Medium potential)*: Visited once a month.
    *   *Class C (Low potential)*: Visited once in two months.
*   **Step 3: Parity in Potential**: Ensure territories have comparable market opportunities so that appraisal remains fair. If one territory has ₹10 Lakh potential and another has ₹5 Lakh, direct comparison of raw sales is invalid.
*   **Step 4: Minimize Travel Time (Territory Shapes)**:
    *   *Circle Shape*: Used when customer density is uniform. Salesperson is stationed at the center; travel times in all directions are equal. Common in FMCG and Pharma.
    *   *Cloverleaf Shape*: Used when customers are scattered randomly. The rep starts from a central base, covers one "leaf" loop over a week, returns home for the weekend, and takes a different leaf the next week. Common in industrial goods.
    *   *Wedge Shape*: Outer-ring expansion. Suitable when combining a highly dense urban market with surrounding satellite rural towns.
*   **Step 5: Stationing**: Place the salesperson's headquarters in the town with the highest concentration of business. Stationing a rep in a low-business town increases travel time, stress, and lodging expenses.

#### 3. Approaches to Territory Design
*   **Market Build-Up Approach**:
    1.  Identify all present and potential customers in a geographic control unit.
    2.  Determine their purchase volume and call frequencies.
    3.  Aggregate this data to calculate the total potential for the area.
    4.  Combine control units to form territories with equal potential and workload.
    *   *Best Suited For*: B2B/Industrial firms where customer directories and consumption norms are easily available.
*   **Workload Approach (Talley's Method)**:
    1.  Classify customers into size categories based on sales volume.
    2.  Define optimum call frequencies for each class.
    3.  Locate customers geographically.
    4.  Multiply number of customers by call frequencies to get total required calls per geographic unit.
    5.  Group units so that the total annual workload (number of calls) is equal for each salesperson.
    *   *Limitation*: Ignores market potential growth (low-volume accounts stay low-volume due to low call allocation); assumes travel time is uniform across different geographies.

#### 4. Account Prioritization & Profitability Models
*   **Single-Factor Model**: Accounts are categorized strictly by one metric (e.g., past sales volume) into Class A, B, or C. Highly simple, but ignores account growth rate and cost of sales.
*   **Portfolio Model**: Classifies accounts on a two-dimensional grid: **Account Opportunity** (growth potential) vs. **Competitive Position** (firm's strength).

```
                      HIGH  |------------------------|------------------------|
                            |        SEGMENT 1       |        SEGMENT 2       |
                            |      High Opportunity  |      High Opportunity  |
                            |      Weak Position     |     Strong Position    |
                            |     (Build strength)   |   (Commit resources)   |
        ACCOUNT             |------------------------|------------------------|
      OPPORTUNITY           |        SEGMENT 3       |        SEGMENT 4       |
                            |      Low Opportunity   |      Low Opportunity   |
                            |      Weak Position     |     Strong Position    |
                            |      (Divest/Drop)     |   (Maintain/Harvest)   |
                       LOW  |------------------------|------------------------|
                                                LOW                     HIGH
                                            COMPETITIVE POSITION
```

*   **Decision Model**: Uses mathematical response curves (typically S-shaped) to relate sales volume to call frequency. It helps allocate calls to maximize incremental sales.
*   **Sales Process Model**: Maps opportunities through the sales funnel: Open Opportunities $\rightarrow$ Qualified Opportunities (need verified, budget approved, timeline set) $\rightarrow$ Best Opportunities (negotiation stage).
*   **Sales & Profit Dilemma (Cost per Call)**:
    High sales volume does not guarantee profitability. The cost of visiting customers must be calculated:
    $$\text{Average Cost per Call} = \frac{\text{CTC of Salesperson} + \text{Direct Travel/Lodging Expenses}}{\text{Net Active Selling Days} \times \text{Average Calls per Day}}$$
    *   **Breakeven Sales Volume**: The minimum sales required from a customer account to cover the cost of visiting them:
        $$\text{Breakeven Sales Value} = \frac{\text{Cost of Sales Call}}{\text{Salesforce Cost as a \% of Sales}}$$
        *Example*: If a sales call costs ₹1,500 and the company's average salesforce cost is 9% of sales, a rep must make at least $\frac{1500}{0.09} = ₹16,666.67$ in sales per call to justify the visit.

#### 5. Sales Quota Systems
*   **Types of Quotas**:
    *   *Sales Volume Quota*: Set in units, rupee value, or points. Points are useful when prices fluctuate or when the firm wants to incentivize selling specific high-priority products.
    *   *Financial Quota*:
        *   *Net Profit Quota*: Focuses the rep on high-margin products rather than high-volume, low-margin items.
        *   *Expense Quota*: Restricts selling expenses to a percentage of sales to prevent wasteful travel and entertainment.
    *   *Activity Quota*: Tracks operational inputs (e.g., cold calls made, demos conducted, new accounts opened, collection of dues). Helpful for missionary or pre-sales roles.
    *   *Weighted Combination Quota (Point Score)*: Combines multiple metrics (e.g., Volume achieved + New Accounts + Demo counts) into a weighted score.
*   **Attributes of a Good Sales Quota Plan**:
    *   *Realistic Attainability*: Challenging but reachable to maintain motivation.
    *   *Objective Accuracy*: Derived from potential data and forecasting rather than executive guesswork.
    *   *Ease of Understanding*: Simple for the salesperson to track and calculate progress.
    *   *Flexibility*: Adjusted mid-year if major environmental shifts (e.g., sudden regulations, supply shortages) occur.
    *   *Fairness*: Adjusted for differences in territory competition, geography, and rep experience.
*   **Case Analysis: Raising Quotas and Compensation Proportionately to Address Declining Market Share**:
    *   *Strategic Risks*:
        *   If the market is in structural decline, raising quotas creates an unrealistic target, causing frustration, burnout, and high attrition.
        *   Salespeople may resort to "inventory loading" (dumping stock on dealers via extreme discounts), which artificially inflates sales volume but destroys long-term profitability.
        *   Increasing variable compensation payout structures raises the cost of sales, increasing the company's financial breakeven point.
    *   *Strategic Benefits*:
        *   In high-potential growth markets, it motivates top-performing stars to capture market share aggressively.
        *   Links organizational growth objectives directly to salesperson income upside.

### Unit 13: Sales Organisation

#### 1. Need and Purpose of a Sales Organization
A sales organization is a structured network of authority, responsibility, and communication designed to execute the personal selling strategy. Its core purposes include:
*   **Specialization**: Divides complex tasks (training, key account management, lead generation) among specialists.
*   **Clear Authority & Communication**: Prevents role ambiguity and outlines clear channels for vertical and horizontal information flow.
*   **Coordination**: Balances personal selling efforts with pricing, logistics, and advertising.
*   **Span of Control & Executive Time**: Restricts the number of direct reports per manager, allowing senior executives to focus on strategic planning rather than routine operations.

#### 2. Process of Designing a Sales Organization

```
[ Step 1: Define Corporate & Sales Objectives ]
                      │
                      ▼
[ Step 2: Identify Necessary Sales Activities ]
                      │
                      ▼
[ Step 3: Group Activities into Positional Levels ]
                      │
                      ▼
[ Step 4: Assign Specific Personnel to Positions ]
                      │
                      ▼
[ Step 5: Establish Authority, Reporting Lines & Span of Control ]
```

#### 3. Factors Influencing Organization Structure & Size
*   *Company Size & Resources*: Small firms use simple structures; large firms require multi-layered structures.
*   *Product Diversity*: Diversified or highly technical products require product specialists.
*   *Market Coverage*: Widespread markets favor geographic specialization.
*   *Management Philosophy*: Centralization (control from HQ) vs. Decentralization (autonomy to branch managers).

#### 4. Basic Organizational Structures

| Structure Type | Operational Concept | Advantages | Disadvantages |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Line Sales Organisation** | Vertical chain of command; authority flows directly from VP to Sales Rep. Subordinates report to one immediate boss. | • Rapid decision-making.<br>• Simple and low administrative cost.<br>• Highly clear lines of authority. | • Overburdens top executives with operational details.<br>• Lacks specialized staff support.<br>• Poorly suited for diversified product lines. |
| **Line & Staff Sales Organisation** | Line managers retain authority, but staff specialists (e.g., Training Manager, Sales Promotion Manager) are added to advise and assist. | • Frees line managers to focus on planning.<br>• Brings specialized expertise to problem-solving.<br>• Better-planned sales training and promotion. | • High overhead cost.<br>• Potential for line-staff conflict (staff overstepping to command; line ignoring staff advice).<br>• Confuses line reps if roles are poorly written. |
| **Functional Sales Organisation** | Sales reps receive direct orders from multiple functional specialists (e.g., direct orders from training, promotion, and recruiting managers). | • Maximizes specialized supervision.<br>• Ideal for highly narrow, specialized fields. | • Violates the "unity of command" principle.<br>• Creates high confusion and conflict for field staff.<br>• High administrative friction. |

#### 5. Field Sales Specialization Designs
When structuring the field sales force, organizations choose between three main specializations:

1.  **Geographic Specialisation**:
    *   *Concept*: Reps are assigned to unique geographic zones and sell the entire product line to all customers in that territory.
    *   *Pros*: Small operating area, low travel costs, high local responsiveness, deep customer relationships.
    *   *Cons*: Reps must know all products, which is difficult for complex product lines; reps may neglect hard-to-sell items.
2.  **Product Specialisation**:
    *   *Concept*: Reps are assigned to specific product lines and sell only those items across a wider geographic area.
    *   *Pros*: High product/technical expertise; handles complex B2B queries effectively.
    *   *Cons*: Travel duplication (two reps from the same company call on the same client); high travel expenses.
3.  **Customer / Market Specialisation**:
    *   *Concept*: Reps are assigned to specific customer segments (e.g., government accounts, retail outlets, key institutional clients).
    *   *Pros*: Highly customer-oriented; deep understanding of segment-specific buying behavior.
    *   *Cons*: Overlapping territories; high cost of administration.

#### 6. Determining Sales Force Size
Three standard methods are used to calculate the required number of sales representatives ($N$):

1.  **Breakdown Method**:
    $$N = \frac{\text{Total Forecasted Sales Volume}}{\text{Estimated Productivity of One Salesperson}}$$
    *   *Pros*: Extremely simple to calculate.
    *   *Cons*: Lead-lag error (assumes sales productivity is constant, ignoring the fact that adding reps changes sales volume).
2.  **Workload Method**:
    $$N = \frac{\text{Total Number of Calls Required per Year}}{\text{Average Annual Calls One Salesperson Can Perform}}$$
    *   *Pros*: Focuses on the actual work needed to cover the market.
    *   *Cons*: Assumes all calls require equal travel and preparation time.
3.  **Incremental Method**:
    Add salespeople as long as the marginal profit contribution ($MP$) of the new rep exceeds the marginal cost ($MC$) of hiring them:
    $$\text{Add Rep if: } MP > MC$$
    *   *Pros*: Economically optimal; maximizes profits.
    *   *Cons*: Estimating the exact incremental revenue of an additional rep is highly difficult in practice.

#### 7. Contemporary Developments
*   **Horizontal / Team Selling**: Hierarchy-free, cross-functional teams (sales, technical, finance) aligned to manage large key accounts (common in IT and consulting).
*   **Sales Process Automation**: Leveraging CRM, AI chatbots, and handheld ordering gadgets to automate routine tasks, allowing reps to focus on core relationships and reducing the physical sales force size required.
*   **Outsourcing**: Handing over retail distribution or lead generation to third-party Direct Selling Agents (DSAs) to convert fixed sales costs into variable costs.

### Unit 14: Sales Control, Analysis and Sales Audit

#### 1. Sales Control: Meaning and Purpose
Sales control is the process of setting performance standards, measuring actual results, comparing the two to identify deviations, and taking corrective action. Its primary purposes are to:
*   Ensure the productivity of the sales face and keep them aligned with corporate objectives.
*   Prevent wasteful spending of sales budgets.
*   Identify underperforming territories or products to implement timely interventions.

#### 2. The Sales Control System (Steps)
The control process operates as a continuous cycle:

```
[ Step 1: Set Standards (e.g., ₹10 Lakh Sales, 9% Expense limit) ]
                                │
                                ▼
[ Step 2: Measure Actual Performance (Sales reports, CRM logs) ]
                                │
                                ▼
[ Step 3: Compare Performance & Analyze Deviations ]
                                │
                                ▼
[ Step 4: Take Corrective Actions (Exception Reporting / Training) ]
```

*   **Management by Exception (MBE)**: Managers ignore minor deviations and focus energy strictly on critical, large variances (e.g., a territory falling 20% below quota).

#### 3. Core Methods of Sales Control
Sales managers use three primary analytical methods to maintain control:

#### A. Sales Analysis
The detailed examination of sales volume by territory, salesperson, product, or customer to find underlying patterns. It is guided by two key principles:
*   **The 80-20 Rule (Pareto Principle)**: Typically, 80% of sales or profits are generated by only 20% of the customers, products, or territories. Conversely, 80% of efforts might be wasted on the bottom 80%.
*   **The Iceberg Principle**: Aggregated sales figures conceal underlying problems. Just as 90% of an iceberg is hidden underwater, a branch achieving 100% of its overall target may hide the fact that 5 out of 6 products are failing, while one single product is over-performing.

```
       ▲  [ Overall Target Achieved: 100% ]  <-- Visible surface (10%)
   ───┼───────────────────────────────────
       ▼  [ Flaws Hidden Underwater: ]       <-- Hidden reality (90%)
          • Product A: 140% (Over-performing)
          • Product B: 20% (Failing)
          • Territory X: 120%
          • Territory Y: 40% (Failing)
```

*   *Gulani Case Example*: A salesperson (Gulani) achieves only 82% of their overall quota. A detailed sales analysis reveals:
    *   *By Product Line*: He met 100% of his desktop and spare parts quotas, but achieved only 20% on Xerox machines.
    *   *By Customer*: His Xerox machine sales to banks, educational, and industrial sectors were 100% of target, but sales to his largest account—a Government Department (which represented 80% of his Xerox quota)—dropped to 0% because a competitor won the contract.
    *   *Lesson*: Aggregated volume tracking fails to pinpoint the root cause (customer loss); segment-wise sales analysis is required.

#### B. Marketing Cost Analysis
A detailed examination of marketing and selling costs relative to sales volume to determine segment profitability.
*   **Key Expense Items**: Travel allowances, sales salaries, commission payouts, showroom rent, sample expenses, catalog printing, bad debt collections.
*   **Usefulness**:
    1.  Determines which products, territories, and pack sizes are profitable vs. unprofitable.
    2.  Identifies minimum order quantities below which a sale incurs a loss.
    3.  Evaluates the financial efficiency of promotional campaigns.
*   **Methodological Approaches**:
    *   *Full-Cost Approach*: Allocates all costs—both direct (salesperson travel) and indirect/fixed (corporate office rent, accounting overhead)—to each segment. Provides net profit per segment but relies on arbitrary cost allocation methods.
    *   *Contribution-Margin Approach*: Allocates only direct and variable costs to the segment, leaving fixed common overheads unallocated. Avoids arbitrary allocations, focusing on the margin contribution of the segment.

#### C. Sales Management Audit
A comprehensive, systematic, independent, and periodic evaluation of the firm's sales objectives, strategies, policies, organization, and procedures.
*   **Primary Objectives**: Evaluate the validity of strategic assumptions, identify future opportunities/threats, and align sales practices with corporate strategy.
*   **Key Audit Parameters (Benchmarks)**: Sales quotas, average calls per day, conversion/strike rates, average order size, customer acquisition costs, and recruitment policies.
*   **Audit Process**:

```
[ Formulate Audit Policy (Scope, evaluators, frequency) ]
                           │
                           ▼
[ Gather MIS Data (CRM records, daily call reports, financials) ]
                           │
                           ▼
[ Compare Performance against Industry Benchmarks ]
                           │
                           ▼
[ Submit Independent Audit Report (Restructuring recommendations) ]
```

*   *Example Outcome*: A sales audit of a consumer durables firm may reveal that the functional organization structure is causing slow response times, prompting a restructure to a Product Manager system, or a shift from direct distribution to retail partners to save costs.
*   *Key Criticism*: It relies heavily on historical data, which may not predict future performance in highly volatile and fast-changing markets.
