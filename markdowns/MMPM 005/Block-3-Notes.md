## Unit 8: Managing People

### 1. Frontline Employees as Boundary Spanners
* **Concept of Boundary Spanners**: Service employees who operate at the boundary of the organization, linking the internal operations with external customers and the market environment.
* **Key Challenges**:
  * **Emotional Labor**: The gap/mismatch between how an employee feels internally (e.g., tired, stressed) and the positive emotions (smiles, warmth) they are expected to display to customers. E.g., Air hostesses, front desk executives.
  * **Role Conflicts**: Frontline staff must balance multiple competing expectations, leading to three types of conflict:
    1. **Person-Role Conflict**: Clash between the employee's personal values/personality and the job demands (e.g., wearing specific uniforms, smiling at rude customers).
    2. **Organization-Client Conflict**: Conflict between company policies (e.g., efficiency, upsell targets) and customer demands (e.g., speed, personalized service, being left alone to shop).
    3. **Inter-Client Conflict**: Arises when serving multiple customers with heterogeneous demands simultaneously (e.g., a teacher in a classroom accommodating different learning paces, or a restaurant waiter handling a noisy group and a quiet couple at adjacent tables).

### 2. Internal Marketing
* **Definition (Gronroos)**: A managerial philosophy that treats employees as the *first internal market* of the organization. The goal is to motivate and train employees to be service-minded and customer-oriented through active, coordinated marketing-like internal approaches.
* **Strategic vs. Tactical Objectives (Gronroos)**:
  | Level | Objectives | Action Items |
  | :--- | :--- | :--- |
  | **Strategic Level** | Create a supportive internal environment. | Align management styles, personnel policy, and planning/control procedures to promote customer consciousness and sales-mindedness. |
  | **Tactical Level** | Sell company services, campaigns, and values internally. | Treat employees as the first market; ensure they accept and understand new services/campaigns before external launch; ensure clear internal communications. |
* **Internal Marketing Action Checklist (Berry & Parasuraman)**:
  1. *Compete for talent aggressively* (recruit the best people).
  2. *Offer a compelling vision* (bring purpose/meaning to the workplace).
  3. *Prepare people to perform* (ongoing training in technical/interactive skills, teaching "why" and not just "how").
  4. *Stress team play* (foster coordination and support).
  5. *Leverage the freedom factor* (empowerment).
  6. *Measure and reward achievement* (fair and visible performance metrics).
  7. *Listen to employees* (conduct internal research on attitudes).

### 3. The Service Profit Chain
* **Framework Concept (Heskett et al.)**: Establishes a direct, causal link between internal quality, employee satisfaction, customer value, customer satisfaction, customer loyalty, and ultimately, firm profitability and growth.
* **Flow of the Chain**:
  $$\text{Internal Service Quality} \rightarrow \text{Employee Satisfaction} \rightarrow \text{Employee Retention \& Productivity} \rightarrow \text{External Service Value} \rightarrow \text{Customer Satisfaction} \rightarrow \text{Customer Loyalty} \rightarrow \text{Revenue Growth \& Profitability}$$
* **Key Lesson**: Happy, empowered employees lead to happy, loyal customers.

### 4. Cycle of Failure vs. Cycle of Success (Schlesinger & Heskett)
* **Cycle of Failure**: 
  * *Characteristics*: Low pay, narrow jobs, minimal training, high employee turnover, customer dissatisfaction, and low profitability.
  * *Mechanism*: Managers design low-cost, simplified jobs -> recruit low-skilled staff with low wages -> high employee turnover -> poor service quality -> customer churn -> low profits -> further pressure on wages.
* **Cycle of Success**:
  * *Characteristics*: Fair pay, broad jobs, extensive training, employee empowerment, high retention, satisfied/loyal customers, and high profitability.
  * *Mechanism*: Invest in employee selection and training -> higher employee satisfaction -> high employee retention -> stable and high-quality service -> customer satisfaction & loyalty -> higher revenues -> reinvestment in staff.
* **Human Resource Strategies for Delivering Service Quality**:
  1. *Hire the Right People*: Compete for talent, test for service competencies & service inclination.
  2. *Develop People*: Train technical & interactive skills, promote teamwork, and empower employees.
  3. *Provide Support Systems*: Equip staff with tools/technology, measure internal service quality, design service-oriented internal processes.
  4. *Retain the Best People*: Treat employees as internal customers, involve them in the vision, align rewards with service performance.
  5. *Use Automation*: Integrate ATMs, chatbots, or self-service tools to reduce human dependency and ensure consistency.

## Unit 9: Managing Physical Evidence

### 1. Role of Physical Evidence (Servicescape)
* **Definition**: The physical environment where the service is delivered and where the firm and customers interact.
* **Servicescape Classification (Julie Baker's Model)**:
  * **Ambient Factors**: Background conditions affecting the five senses (e.g., temperature, air quality, music, scent).
  * **Design Factors**: Visual/functional stimuli at the forefront of awareness (e.g., architecture, layout, signage, colors, comfort, functionality).
  * **Social Factors**: The human elements present in the environment (e.g., appearance and behavior of frontline employees and other customers).
* **Six Roles of Physical Evidence (Parasuraman et al.)**:
  1. *Shaping First Impressions*: Creating positive cues prior to consumption.
  2. *Managing Trust*: Reducing anxiety by projecting credibility and professional quality.
  3. *Facilitating Service Quality*: Supporting operations and comfort for both customers and staff.
  4. *Changing the Image*: Upgrading facilities (e.g., adding air-conditioning or modern layouts) to reposition the brand.
  5. *Providing Sensory Stimuli*: Arousing the senses (e.g., premium aroma/music) to encourage longer stays.
  6. *Socializing Employees*: Creating environments that foster healthy social ties and collaboration.

### 2. Tangibilizing the Service and the Message
* **Tangibilizing the Service**: Highlighting physical assets in communications or creating tangible markers to represent service attributes (e.g., showing a courier company's modern delivery trucks, or offering a physical credit card with distinct branding).
* **Tangibilizing the Message**: Using concrete evidence (before/after weight loss metrics), service guarantees (warranties), or promoting positive word-of-mouth to make abstract claims credible.

### 3. Case Application: Designing Physical Evidence for a Coffee Shop
* **Ambiance / Ambient Factors**:
  * *Scent*: Strong, warm aroma of freshly ground coffee beans to stimulate appetite.
  * *Sound*: Soft, acoustic, or lo-fi instrumental background music at low volume to encourage quiet conversations or working.
  * *Lighting*: Warm, low-intensity yellow lighting to create a cozy, relaxing environment.
* **Design / Functional Factors**:
  * *Furniture*: Comfortable upholstered armchairs, wooden work tables, and high-stools for laptop users with accessible power outlets.
  * *Layout*: Spacious pathways that prevent crowding near the counter; distinct zones for quick takeaways, social chats, and quiet study.
  * *Signage*: Clear, handwritten-style blackboard menus, directional signs for washrooms, and visible Wi-Fi credentials.
* **Social / Human Factors**:
  * *Baristas' Appearance*: Uniformed aprons with friendly nametags.
  * *Behavior*: Empathetic customer greetings, efficient order taking, and knowledgeable recommendations.
  * *Crowd Management*: Attracting a target audience (e.g., students, remote workers) through appropriate pricing and design cues.

## Unit 10: Managing Service Process

### 1. Structure of Service Processes: Complexity vs. Divergence
According to Shostack, service processes are structurally defined by two dimensions:
* **Complexity**: The number of sequential steps and sub-steps required to execute a service.
* **Divergence**: The level of freedom, latitude, or customization allowed in executing those steps.
* **Four Strategic Paths for Process Engineering**:
  1. *Reduced Divergence (Standardization)*: Reduces costs, increases speed and reliability, but lowers customization.
  2. *Increased Divergence (Customization)*: Creates a premium niche positioning, charges higher margins, but is harder to manage and control.
  3. *Reduced Complexity (Specialization)*: Narrows the service offering, making it easier to control and distribute (e.g., a clinic focusing only on eye surgery).
  4. *Increased Complexity (Market Penetration)*: Adds more services/steps to capture higher revenue per customer, but can lead to confusion and quality drops if unmanaged.

### 2. Classification of Service Processes (Lovelock's 4 Categories)
Services can be classified based on whether the action is tangible/intangible and directed at people/possessions:
| Nature of Act / Object | Directed at People's Bodies | Directed at Physical Possessions |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tangible Actions** | **People Processing**<br>Must be physically present.<br>*Examples*: Passenger transport, healthcare, lodging, fitness centers. | **Possession Processing**<br>Object is present; customer can be absent.<br>*Examples*: Car repair, dry cleaning, freight transport. |
| **Intangible Actions** | **Mental Stimulus Processing**<br>Customer must be mentally present.<br>*Examples*: Education, entertainment, consulting, religion. | **Information Processing**<br>Intangible assets processed via ICT.<br>*Examples*: Banking, insurance, legal services, accounting. |

### 3. Service Blueprinting
* **Definition**: A visual mapping tool that simultaneously depicts the service delivery process, points of customer contact, roles of customers and employees, and visible physical evidence.
* **Key Components**:
  * *Customer Actions*: Journey steps of the customer.
  * *Onstage Contact Person Actions*: Face-to-face interactions visible to the customer.
  * *Backstage Contact Person Actions*: Interactions behind the scenes (not visible to the customer, e.g., preparing food).
  * *Support Processes*: Internal systems/departments supporting contact staff (e.g., billing database).
  * *Line of Visibility*: Boundary separating onstage (visible) and backstage (invisible) activities.
  * *Physical Evidence*: Cues encountered by customers at each step.
* **Benefits of Blueprinting**:
  * Identifies bottlenecks and failure points.
  * Clarifies roles and training needs.
  * Helps coordinate across departments by showing interdependencies.
  * Helps design distinct cost/responsibility centers to optimize quality vs. efficiency.

### 4. Customer Roles in Service Co-Creation
Customers are active participants in service processes and can play several roles (Manfred & Dominik):
1. **The Specifier**: Defining specific custom requirements (e.g., sharing medical history with a doctor).
2. **The Transferer**: Performing preliminary tasks (e.g., driving a car to the service center).
3. **The Abandoner**: Giving up time/control to let the service happen (e.g., using alternative transport while their car is in the workshop).
4. **The Co-Producer**: Actively producing the value (e.g., performing physiotherapy exercises, or submitting tax documents on time).
5. **The Co-User**: Sharing the service space with other consumers (e.g., in a flight cabin, cinema, or gym).

### 5. Why Customers Switch Service Providers (Susan Keaveney's Model)
Marketers must address controllable factors to prevent customer defection:
* **Pricing**: Excessive prices, unexpected price increases, or deceptive pricing policies.
* **Inconvenience**: Poor location, long wait times for appointments or execution.
* **Core Service Failures**: Technical mistakes, billing errors, or unfulfilled promises.
* **Service Encounter Failures**: Rude, uncaring, unknowledgeable, or unresponsive staff.
* **Response to Service Failures**: Ignored complaints, negative responses, or slow, reluctant recovery.
* **Competition**: Competitors offering superior quality or value.
* **Ethical Problems**: Cheating, safety violations, or pressure selling.
* **Involuntary Switching**: Customer relocates or provider shuts down.

### 6. Service Recovery and Complaint Management Systems
* **Service Recovery**: Actions taken by a service provider in response to a service failure to restore customer goodwill (e.g., upgrading a customer's rental car to a luxury sedan when the reserved economy car is unavailable).
* **Six Service Recovery Strategies (Hart et al.)**:
  1. *Measure the costs* of lost customers to justify recovery budgets.
  2. *Break customer silence* by encouraging complaints (making feedback easy).
  3. *Anticipate failure-prone zones* to proactively prepare solutions.
  4. *Act fast* with immediate resumption, explanation, or apology.
  5. *Train and empower frontline employees* to solve issues on the spot without management escalations.
  6. *Close the loop* by letting customers know what corrective actions were taken.
* **Features of a Good Complaint Management System (ISO/TC 176 Standards)**:
  * **Visibility**: Customers know exactly where to complain.
  * **Accessibility**: Simple, free, and multiple modes of lodging complaints (toll-free, email, app).
  * **Responsiveness**: Quick resolution and regular status updates.
  * **Customer-Focused Approach**: Active listening, showing empathy, and treating complaints as gifts.
  * **Accountability**: Dedicated staff assigned to manage and resolve complaints.
  * **Continuous Improvement**: Logging complaint data to analyze root causes and permanently improve operational processes.
