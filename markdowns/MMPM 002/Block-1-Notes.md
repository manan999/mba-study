### UNIT 1: SALES MANAGEMENT: ROLE, NATURE AND ETHICS

#### 1. Nature and Scope of Sales Management
*   **Definition:** Sales Management is the planning, direction, and control of personal selling activities of a business unit. This includes recruiting, selecting, training, equipping, assigning, routing, supervising, paying, motivating, and evaluating the sales force.
*   **Nature:**
    *   *Integration with Marketing:* Personal selling is the implementation arm of the marketing mix (Promotion). Sales planning must align with overall marketing strategy.
    *   *Revenue Generation:* Unlike other departments (HR, Admin) which are cost centers, the sales department is the primary direct revenue-generating division.
*   **Three Levels of Sales Managers:**
    1.  *Top-Level:* Focused on strategic planning, policy formulation, and sales budgeting.
    2.  *Middle-Level:* Focused on tactical implementation, regional goals, and monitoring.
    3.  *First-Level (Field/Line Managers):* Focused on daily supervision, territory management, and direct sales force coaching.

#### 2. Evolution of Sales Management
*   **Pre-Industrial Era:** Production was small-scale and local. The main challenge was supply, not demand. A single owner managed production and sales.
*   **Industrial Revolution:** Mass production led to surplus goods. Specialized sales departments were created to identify and expand into new geographical markets.
*   **Middlemen Era:** Growth of distribution channels (wholesalers, retailers) made direct manufacturer-to-consumer contact difficult.
*   **Modern Strategic Era:** Sales management expanded beyond simple selling to include strategic planning, sales forecasting, customer relationship management (CRM), territory design, and information communication technology (ICT) integration.

```
[Pre-Industrial: Localized/Simple] ──► [Post-Industrial: Market Expansion] ──► [Modern: Strategic/ICT Driven]
```

#### 3. Role of Sales Function in Marketing
*   **Selling vs. Marketing:**
| Parameter | Selling (Vikray) | Marketing (Vipanan) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Focus** | Product-focused (Seller's needs). | Customer-focused (Customer's needs/wants). |
| **Philosophy** | "Get cash for the product." | "Create value-satisfying goods." |
| **Horizon** | Short-term volume and transaction-oriented. | Long-term relationship and brand loyalty. |
| **Sequence** | Begins after production is complete. | Begins before production (needs assessment) and continues post-sale. |

#### 4. Managing Ethics in Sales Environment
*   **Common Sales Ethical Vulnerabilities:** Bribes/kickbacks, puffery (exaggerated claims), moonlighting, padding expense accounts, withholding product safety info, and using customers to get competitor secrets.
*   **Ethical Work Climate Dimensions (Four Aspects):**
    1.  *Policies and Rules:* Formally documented and communicated ethical codes of conduct.
    2.  *Trust and Responsibility:* Empowering salespersons to act responsibly without excessive micromanagement.
    3.  *Peer Behavior:* Extent to which coworkers demonstrate high moral standards.
    4.  *Sales Emphasis:* Balance between profit pressure and ethical behavior. Heavy pressure to meet quotas with strict controls worsens the ethical climate.

### UNIT 2: DIVERSITY OF SELLING SITUATIONS

#### 1. Growing Importance of Personal Selling (Indian Context)
*   **Personal Selling:** Direct, two-way communication between paid representatives and prospects to close a transaction.
*   **Key Drivers in the Indian Market:**
    *   *Product Complexity:* Rise in technical/appliances sales (e.g., water purifiers, solar panels) requiring demonstration.
    *   *Low Literacy / Consumer Education:* Personal selling serves as an educational tool for rural/semi-urban markets.
    *   *High marginal value of Rupee:* Indian consumers demand maximum value for money, which requires face-to-face trust building.
    *   *Affordability:* Small firms that cannot afford massive mass advertising budgets rely on targeted personal selling.

#### 2. Essential Qualities and Skills of a Successful Salesperson
*   **Key Traits (Kotler's Essentials):**
    *   *Empathy:* Ability to step into the customer's shoes and understand their actual needs/feelings.
    *   *Ego Drive / Persuasion:* Inward need to make the sale, not just for money, but for self-actualization and personal triumph.
*   **Other Qualities:** Neat professional appearance, curiosity (desire to learn), clear speech/tone, quantitative/writing ability (for reporting), poise, integrity, and self-motivation (ability to work without constant supervision).

#### 3. Classifying Selling Situations and Roles
*   **Service Selling:** Getting repeat orders from existing customers whose buying habits are known.
*   **Developmental Selling:** Creating new customers from resistant prospects who may use competitors.
*   **Specific Roles:**
    1.  *Delivery Salesperson:* Primary job is physical delivery (e.g., milk/bread); selling is secondary.
    2.  *Inside Order Taker:* Retail counter assistant (e.g., retail store staff) where customer comes with buy intent.
    3.  *Outside Order Taker:* Calls on regular retailers (e.g., FMCG sales reps); does low creative/hard selling.
    4.  *Missionary Salesperson:* Order creators who do not take direct orders but build goodwill/educate (e.g., Medical Representatives/MRs for pharma companies).
    5.  *Sales Engineer / Technical Sales:* Sells high-tech products (e.g., factory machinery) requiring deep technical knowledge.
    6.  *Salesperson of Non-Competing Companies:* Cooperating salespersons who share prospect information to mutual benefit (e.g., a steel rod seller sharing a lead with a cement seller).
    7.  *Industrial Selling (B2B):* Selling raw materials, components, or heavy machinery to other businesses, government bodies, or re-sellers. Features high transaction value, rational decision-making, and multiple buying influencers.

### UNIT 3: THEORIES OF SELLING AND SELLING PROCESS

#### 1. Comparison of Theories of Selling
*   **AIDAS Theory (Seller-Oriented):** Assumes the buyer's mind goes through five sequential stages:
    *   *Attention:* Break the customer's routine mind blocks.
    *   *Interest:* Keep them engaged using visual aids, brochures, or demonstrations.
    *   *Desire:* Convert features into benefits to match customer motivations.
    *   *Action:* Overcome hesitation/inertia and close the deal.
    *   *Satisfaction:* Post-purchase reassurance and service to prevent cognitive dissonance.
*   **Right Set of Circumstances Theory (Situation-Oriented):** *"Everything was right for the sale."* Focuses on the salesperson controlling external factors to create a situation where the prospect is naturally led to buy.
*   **Buying Formula Theory (Buyer-Oriented):** Focuses on the internal mental sequence of the buyer. The path is:
$$\text{Need} \longrightarrow \text{Solution} \longrightarrow \text{Purchase} \longrightarrow \text{Satisfaction}$$
    *   The salesperson must establish the link: Need $\to$ Product/Service $\to$ Brand $\to$ Purchase.
*   **Behavioral Equation Theory (Learning-Oriented):** J. Howard's formula:
$$B = P \times D \times K \times V$$
    *   Where $B$ = Brand purchase response, $P$ = Predisposition/Force of habit, $D$ = Drive/Motivation level, $K$ = Incentive potential (satisfaction value), $V$ = Cue intensity (sales presentation quality). Since variables are multiplicative, if any variable (like Motivation $D$ or satisfaction $K$) is 0, no sale happens.

#### 2. The Personal Selling Process
```
[Prospecting & Qualifying] ──► [Pre-approach] ──► [Approach] ──► [Presentation & Demo] 
                                                                        │
[Follow-up & Service] ◄── [Closing the Sale] ◄── [Objection Handling] ◄─┘
```
1.  **Prospecting and Qualifying:** Searching for leads (random leads: cold calling; selective leads: referrals, networking) and qualifying them using the **NAME** formula (Need, Authority, Money, Eligibility).
2.  **Pre-approach (The Planning Stage):** Researching the prospect (buying procedures, current products, budget). Key steps:
    *   *Seeding:* Preparing the prospect for the call.
    *   *Objective setting:* Determining the goal of the call (appointment or direct sale).
    *   *Strategy choice:* Preparing presentation styles and rehearsing.
3.  **Approaching the Prospect:** Creating a positive first impression. Strategies:
    *   *Non-product related:* Reference of mutual acquaintance, self-introduction, free gift.
    *   *Piquing interest:* Customer benefit statement, curiosity-inducing statements.
    *   *Product-related:* Straight into product demonstration or showing a model/sample.
4.  **Sales Presentation and Demonstration:**
    *   *Attention Probes:* Using **Open Probes** (prompts detailed answers: "How do you feel about...") rather than **Closed Probes** (simple Yes/No answers) to discover needs.
    *   *Translation:* Translating product **features** (attributes, e.g., aluminum engine) into customer **benefits** (what they gain, e.g., higher mileage).
5.  **Negotiating Sales Resistance (Objection Handling):**
    *   *Sincere Objections:* Real obstacles (e.g., "no budget right now", request for guarantees).
    *   *Insincere Objections:* Trivial excuses (e.g., complaining about packaging color).
    *   *Objection Handling System:* **Listen** carefully $\to$ **Ask** for details/clarify $\to$ **Respond** (offer finance options, facts) $\to$ **Confirm** answer satisfaction ("Does that solve your problem?") $\to$ **Move** to close.
6.  **Closing the Sale:** Overcoming decision-making inertia. Techniques:
    *   *Alternative Close:* Offering choices ("Should I ship 1 box or 2 boxes?").
    *   *Summary Close:* Summarizing benefits and asking for the order.
    *   *Picture Close:* Imprinting brand image creatively.
    *   *Closing with Empathy:* Aligning the final pitch with the client's actual perspective.
7.  **Follow-up and Servicing:** Maintaining contact after delivery to resolve grievances, build trust, and ensure repeat purchases.

#### 3. Consultative Selling
*   **Concept:** A highly collaborative selling approach where the salesperson acts as a business consultant. Instead of selling a generic product, the salesperson works with the client's team to understand their strategic goals and custom-builds a solution.
*   **Example:** A software firm selling a customized ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) system to a manufacturing plant. The salesperson meets multiple departments (Finance, Production, HR) to study their workflow bottlenecks and tailors the software package specifically to optimize their margins.
