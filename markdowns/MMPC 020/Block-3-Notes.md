### UNIT 9: CSR IN INDIAN CONTEXT

#### 1. Four Historical Phases of CSR in India
*   **Phase 1 (1850 - 1914): Merchant Charity & Philanthropy:**
    *   Mainly driven by family-owned businesses (Tatas, Birlas, Godrej) and religious values.
    *   Charity was unstructured and targeted toward setting up temples, schools, community water tanks, and relief during famines.
*   **Phase 2 (1914 - 1960): Trusteeship Model & Freedom Struggle:**
    *   Deeply influenced by Mahatma Gandhi’s concept of **Trusteeship**, which urged rich industrialists to manage their wealth as "trustees" for the benefit of the common people.
    *   CSR was aligned with national interests, focusing on establishing educational institutions, rural development, and supporting the independence movement.
*   **Phase 3 (1960 - 1990): Mixed Economy & State-Led Regulations:**
    *   Characterized by high government regulation (the "License Raj"). The state assumed primary responsibility for social development, and private businesses faced heavy restrictions.
    *   CSR was seen as peripheral, with corporate contributions shifting to public sector enterprises (PSUs) and state-directed welfare.
*   **Phase 4 (1990 onwards): Globalization & Strategic Integration:**
    *   Economic liberalization opened the Indian market to MNCs. CSR transitioned from voluntary charity to sustainable business practices integrated into corporate strategy.
    *    culminated in the **Companies Act, 2013**, which made India the first country to mandate CSR spending.

#### 2. Key CSR Initiatives of Indian Companies
*   **Tata Group:** Pioneer in CSR (e.g., Tata Steel rural development, Tata Memorial Hospital, and primary funding of institutions like IISc and TIFR).
*   **ITC Limited:** Famous for the **e-Choupal** initiative, which empowered rural farmers by establishing internet kiosks in villages, removing intermediaries, and enhancing agricultural productivity.
*   **Infosys Foundation:** Supports projects in healthcare, education, rural development, arts, and culture across India.
*   **Wipro (Azim Premji Foundation):** Strongly focused on improving the quality and equity of school education.

### UNIT 10: CSR LEGISLATION AND POLICY GUIDELINES

#### 1. Section 135 of the Companies Act, 2013 (Applicability & Rules)
*   **Applicability Criteria:** Every company (including private, public, and foreign offices in India) satisfying at least one of the following in any financial year:
    1.  **Net Worth:** ₹500 crore or more.
    2.  **Turnover:** ₹1000 crore or more.
    3.  **Net Profit:** ₹5 crore or more.
*   **Spending Requirement:** At least **2% of the average net profits** of the company made during the three immediately preceding financial years.
*   **CSR Committee:**
    *   Mandated to have 3 or more directors, with at least 1 independent director.
    *   *Exemption:* Under recent amendments, if the CSR obligation does not exceed **₹50 lakh**, the requirement for a CSR Committee is waived, and the Board of Directors discharges its responsibilities.
*   **Treatment of Unspent Amount:**
    *   *Ongoing Projects:* Unspent amount must be transferred to a special bank account called "Unspent CSR Account" within 30 days and spent within 3 financial years. Otherwise, it goes to a government fund listed in Schedule VII.
    *   *Other Projects:* Must be transferred to a Schedule VII fund (e.g., PM National Relief Fund) within 6 months of the end of the financial year.

#### 2. Schedule VII: Permitted CSR Activities
Schedule VII lists activities that qualify for CSR spend, including:
*   Eradicating hunger, poverty, and malnutrition; promoting healthcare and sanitation.
*   Promoting education, vocational skills, and livelihood enhancement.
*   Promoting gender equality, empowering women, and setting up homes for orphans and seniors.
*   Ensuring environmental sustainability, ecological balance, and animal welfare.
*   Protection of national heritage, art, and culture.
*   Contributions to the PM National Relief Fund, Clean Ganga Fund, Swachh Bharat Kosh, etc.

#### 3. CSR Policy, Monitoring, and Impact Assessment
*   **CSR Policy Components:** Outline of projects/activities to be undertaken, implementation schedules, budget allocations, and monitoring mechanisms.
*   **Duties of the Board:** Approve CSR policy, disclose the policy on the company's website, ensure the 2% spend is met, and justify any unspent amount in the Board's Report.
*   **Impact Assessment (Amendment Rules):**
    *   Mandatory for companies with an average CSR obligation of **₹10 crore or more** in the 3 preceding financial years.
    *   Must be conducted through an independent agency for projects with outlays of **₹1 crore or more** and completed at least 1 year prior.
    *   Impact assessment expenses can be booked as CSR spend, capped at 2% of total CSR spend or ₹50 lakh (whichever is higher).

### UNIT 11: CSR IN PUBLIC SECTOR UNITS (PSUs)

#### 1. Department of Public Enterprises (DPE) Guidelines for CPSEs
*   Central Public Sector Enterprises (CPSEs) have a double mandate: economic profitability and social development (acting as model employers).
*   **Key DPE Guidelines:**
    1.  **Mandatory Budget Allocation:** Aligned with Section 135 of the Companies Act, but CPSEs must prioritize local areas around their plants/stations.
    2.  **CSR and Sustainability Integration:** CSR projects must be planned in alignment with sustainable development parameters (e.g., renewable energy, water conservation).
    3.  **Social Audit:** Must be conducted periodically to verify project implementation and obtain community feedback.

#### 2. National Voluntary Guidelines (NVGs), 2011
*   Formulated by the Ministry of Corporate Affairs, India. Consists of **9 Core Principles** on Social, Environmental, and Economic Responsibilities of Business:
    1.  Conduct and govern with Ethics, Transparency, and Accountability.
    2.  Provide goods/services that are safe and contribute to sustainability throughout their life cycle.
    3.  Promote the well-being of all employees.
    4.  Respect the interests of, and be responsive to, all stakeholders, especially the vulnerable.
    5.  Respect and promote human rights.
    6.  Respect, protect, and make efforts to restore the environment.
    7.  Be responsible when influencing public and regulatory policy.
    8.  Support inclusive growth and equitable development.
    9.  Provide value to customers in a responsible manner.

#### 3. Global CSR Guidelines
*   **UN Global Compact (UNGC):** 10 principles covering human rights, labor, environment, and anti-corruption.
*   **OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises:** Recommendations from governments to MNCs on responsible business conduct.
*   **ISO 26000:** An international standard providing guidance on social responsibility (not for certification).
*   **GRI (Global Reporting Initiative):** Comprehensive framework for sustainability reporting.
