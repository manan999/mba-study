6.1  INTRODUCTION

The new era of industrialization and supportive government policies resulted
in expansion of boundaries and horizon of the companies.  The aim of every
firm is to more towards internationalization. Therefore, it becomes important
to  understand  the  process  of  internationalization.  Internationalization
is  termed  to  be  a  process  where  business  organizations  get  involved  in
international markets. Most of the business houses in contemporary world
initiate their operations domestically and as they grow and start expanding
they slowly internationalize resulting in thinning boundaries and changing
landscapes.

This  unit  aims  to  help  you  explain  the  process  of  internationalization
and  the  reasons  for  internationalization;  describe  the  various  stages  and
phases  of  internationalization;  discuss  the  operating  advantages  and
disadvantages of MNCs; and understand the importance of various models

6.3  STAGES OF INTERNATIONALIZATION

Most companies pass through different stages of internationalization. There
are,  of  course,  many  firms  which  have  international  business  since  the
beginning, including hundred percent export-oriented firms. Even in the case
of many of the hundred per cent export-oriented firms, the development of
their international business would pass through different stages of evolution.

A firm which is entirely domestic in its activities normally passes through
different stages of internationalization before it becomes a truly global one.

There  are  many  firms  which  enthusiastically  and  systematically  go
international as part of their corporate plan. However, in the case of many
firms the initial attitude towards international business is passive and they
get into international business in response to some external stimuli. A firm
may start exports on an experimental basis and if the results are satisfying
in  due  course  it  would  establish  offices,  branches  or  subsidiaries  or  joint
ventures  abroad.  The  expansion  process  may  also  be  characterized  by
increasing  the  product  mix  and  the  number  of  market  segments,  markets
and countries of operation. In this process the company could be expected
to become multinational and finally global.

Many experts have given different views on stages of internationalization.
For  our  understanding  we  will  consider  the  following  stages  of
internationalization. There are basically five stages of internationalization
and these are:

in just exporting goods to the foreign countries which has high demand.

international to Multinational and Transnational firms.

6.4  OPERATING ADVANTAGES AND
DISADVANTAGES OF MNCs

Multinational  Corporations  (MNCs)  by  themselves  have  couple  of
distinctive pros and cons with respect to their operations which differentiate
them from firms which operate domestically. Some of the firms have the
capability  to  internationalize  efficiently  and  overcame  disadvantages  and
capitalize on the internal advantages to a larger extent as suitable to the type
of individual corporations and the type of business involved.

A.  Operational Advantages of MNCs

Superior  Technical  Know-how  :  Improved  technical  knowledge  is  the
most important advantage MNCs enjoy. These firms have access to better

113

Internationalization Processtechnology  either  self-developed  or  acquired  by  the  firm  in  the  areas  of
Management, Operations and Production.

(i)  Large  Size  and  Economies  of  Scale  -  MNCs  being  very  large  in
size have the advantage of economies of scale. Volume of production
reduces  per  unit  fixed  cost  and  ultimately  reduces  the  cost  of
production making the product more competitive and easily saleable
in the market. This situation is specifically true in capital intensive
sectors viz., Steel, Petrochemical and automobile.

(ii)  Lower  Input  Costs  Due  to  Large  Size  -Multinationals  as  a  result
of large production levels purchase raw materials in bulk and thus,
able to bargain with the suppliers for a lesser price and also obtain
substantial amount of price advantage as a result of bulk buying.

(iii)  Ability to Access Raw Materials Overseas -MNCs are capable of
reducing production costs by reducing input costs through procuring
raw materials from countries where they are abundantly and cheaply
available.

(iv)  Ability to Access Raw Materials Overseas -MNCs are capable of
shifting  their  production  to  other  countries  and  thus  enhance  their
international competitiveness.

(v)  Economies  of  Scale  for  Shipment,  Distribution,  and  Promotion
Costs-  MNCs  attain  lower  costs  for  Shipment,  Distribution  and
Promotion  as  they  deal  with  large  volume  of  freight.  Because  of
sizeable amount of business these firms are in a position to negotiate for
lower rates with the service providers. Large corporations especially
firms dealing with petroleum justify purchase of their ownership due
to the huge transport requirement they have. Therefore they are not
in  a  position  to  depend  on  third  party  service  providers  and  show
self-owned  transport  as  an  effective  method  to  reduce  cost.  Large
volume of production also leads to lower promotion and distribution
costs. These organizations charge lower tariffs for distribution across
countries and still make profits as a result of large scale operations.
MNCs  also  get  ads  for  a  lower  tariff  as  they  have  huge  ad-spend
budget  which  helps  in  a  lowering  their  accrued  cost  of  promotions
and advertisements.

(vi)  Brand  Image  and  Goodwill-  Many  of  the  MNCs  possess  product
lines that have established a good reputation for quality, performance,
value,  and  service.  This  reputation  spreads  abroad  through  exports
and promotion, which adds to an MNC’s arsenal of potent weapons
in  the  form  of  brand  image  or  goodwill,  which  it  is  able  to  use  to
differentiate  its  own  products  from  others  in  its  genre.  MNCs  are
able to leverage this goodwill or brand image by standardizing their
product lines in different countries and achieving economies of scale.
For example, Playstations do not have any special modifications for
different  countries  (except  for  voltage)  and  the  home-based  plant
churns out standardized products for the world market.

Access to Low-Cost Financing - MNCs are large scale organizations and
require large amount which they procure from the financial institutions at
comparatively lower rates. They borrow huge money at lower rates because

Information Advantages - MNCs as an outcome of operating in various

efficiently bear risks emerging out of cyclic economic declines. Generally
these cycles are country specific and not the same in all the countries at one
point of time. Thus Losses in one country can be offset by gains in other
countries.

B.  Operational Disadvantages of MNCs

Business  Risks  -  MNCs  come  across  serious  unforeseen  risks  than  the
firms which are entirely domestic in nature. This is because they carry out
business in multiple countries. These firms deal with foreign exchange and
as a result the biggest risk they face is fluctuation in foreign exchange which
directly affects their profit margins.

Regulations  in  the  Host  Country  -  MNCs  while  operating  in  different
countries face difficulties due to varied regulations in different host countries.
In many cases these regulations vary significantly from country to country.
MNCs face tough task in understanding and familiarizing themselves with
varied  and  dynamic  regulations  of  different  countries. Adhering  to  these
regulations require modification in its operations to ensure that it does not
overstep them.

Different Legal Systems - MNCs are bound to operate under the different
legal systems of different countries. Some countries have very cumbersome
legislative and judicial processes and may contain certain nuances which
are  not  easily  understood  by  non-natives.  Some  legislation  may  prohibit
certain business operations which are otherwise very normal in the home
country.

and problems in dealing with business associates, government officials and

International Product Life Cycle Model (IPLC) is intended to advance trade
theory beyond David Ricardo’s static framework of comparative advantage.
Author  in  this  model  came  up  with  an  experiment  of  simple  economic
benefits  to  any  country  engaged  in  international  trade  even  if  the  firm  is
able to produce all products at lowest possible cost does not appear to have
any need to enter into international trade.

Vernon focuses on the dynamics of comparative advantage in trade and drew
inspiration from the product life cycle to explain the change in trade patterns
over time. Through this model the author presented that international trade
is advantageous for a country which specialize in product categories with
highest added value and are capable of producing such products at low cost
and allow its workforce to specialize.

According  to  this  model  a  local  manufacturer  in  a  developed  country
starts  by  selling  a  new  technologically  advanced  product  to  high  income
group consumers in its home country market. To start with the firm wants

production capacity is increasingly shifted to foreign countries enabling the
firm to maximize production and get the advantage of economies of scale
and  bypass  trade  barriers. As  the  product  reaches  the  maturity  stage  the
competition increases and it becomes more of a commodity. Towards the
end the innovator of the developed country becomes challenged in its own
home  market  and  makes  the  developed  nation  which  actually  developed
the product a net importer of the product. This product is then produced by
competitors in lesser developed countries or if the innovator has developed
into a MNC it may get manufactured in its foreign based production facility.

Transaction Theory

Coase (1937) developed a theory which is the foundation of the Transaction
Cost Analysis (TCA) model. The theory behind this model takes the cost
as an important component in decision making. Author argued that a firm
will tend to expand until the cost of organizing an extra transaction within
a firm will become equal to the cost of carrying out the same transaction
in  the  open  market.  This  predicts  that  a  firm  will  perform  internally
all  those  activities  it  can  undertake  at  a  lower  cost  through  a  system  of
efficient internal management control systems while the same firm relies on
independent outsiders viz., intermediaries, agents or distributors for a low
cost advantage. Transaction costs emerge in situation when markets fail to
operate under perfect competition (friction free). The cost of operating in
perfect completion market results in zero transaction cost and there would be
little or no incentive to impose any impediments to free exchange and trade.
However,  in  reality  there  is  always  some  friction  between  buyer  and  the
seller resulting in transaction cost. The friction between buyer and seller is
explained by opportunistic behavior. The Transaction Cost Analysis (TCA)
framework states that cost minimization explains structural decisions. Firms
internalize, that is, integrate vertically, to reduce transaction costs.

Transaction  cost  is  further  divided  into  different  types  of  cost  related  to
transactional  relationship  between  the  buyer  and  the  seller.  The  equation
presented below provides the description of the cost elements in the TCA
equation.

Transaction cost = ex-ante costs (search costs + contracting costs) + ex-

post costs (monitoring costs + enforcement costs)

TCA theory is built on the assumption that the firm internationalizes until
the transaction cost in the firm balances with the cost of the same transaction
based  on  the  market  (Hollensen  2008).  Transaction  cost  evaluates  the
divergent interests and opportunistic behavior of exporter’s decision about
internationalization  and  is  taken  after  proper  analysis  of  the  transaction
costs.  The  results  of  the  analysis  state  that  if  the  cost  of  transaction  in
the  foreign  market  is  lesser  than  the  cost  of  the  firm  when  they  are  only
operating  internally  the  firm  will  opt  for  internationalization  and  will  try
to have business relationship with foreign partners via licensing, exports,
subcontracting and joint ventures.

The key features of the Transaction Cost Theory are:

It  believes organization structure as a single important arrangement

Risk Assessment Process

Risk assessment in a foreign trade is a two stage process. The first being
riskiness of the host country as a pace to do business and the second one
is the risk involved in the particular type of investment. An investment can
only be made if the level of both the risks is manageable and acceptable.

a)  Assessing Country Risk- Country risk is a broad measure that focuses
on the riskiness of the country as a whole and as a place for MNCs to
conduct business. One prime time consideration is the level of current
and future political stability of the country. A stable country obviously
provides better business environment and opportunities to the MNCs
to  conduct  business. Assessment  of  political  conditions  is  made  by
gathering relevant information from several sources viz., national and
international media, diplomatic assessments or professional agencies
that specialize in monitoring development in certain countries.

There  are  some  professional  agencies  which  develop  ratings  for
different types and degrees of risk in various countries with regard to
foreign direct investment by MNCs. These ratings are developed by
assigning weights to different political, social, and economic factors
that could lead to political instability and disorder. These weights are
then added and averaged according to a particular formula to arrive at a
final rating of a country’s level of risk. This system consists of arbitrary
assignment of weights thus having a strong element of subjectivity in
the analysis where dependability is questioned. General phenomena
is that western countries being industrialized countries carry low level
of risk and the yet to develop and low-income countries are riskier.
Exceptions  to  the  case  being  countries  with  larger  middle  income
group population because these countries are prone to socio-political
turmoil and carry an even greater risk than some of the lower-income
countries.

b)  Assessing  Investment  Risk  -  Initiating  the  risk  assessment  is  to

them do not want to invest and thus, reject the potential investment.
Rejection may also occur because of disagreement in the terms and
conditions  between  host  country  and  MNCs.  Sometimes  the  host

2.

Long term Agreements - MNCs find a way to reduce political risk
by  negotiating  with  the  host  governments  and  agreeing  upon  long
term  commitments.  Negotiating  with  these  safe-guards  requires
skill and foresight. A balance must be struck between achieving the
safest possible terms for the firm and recognizing the current national
policies of the host government. The limitation of these safeguards
however, is that there is no practical way to enforce them in the event
that the host government reneges on its part of contractual obligations.
A government is less likely to take any adverse action once it’s a legal
binding through a legal agreement as compared to a situation in which
it has not given any assurances.

Lobbying - MNCs many a times resort to lobbying politicians and

of the firm establishes contacts with local officials and politicians and

7.6  SUMMARY

Investment  in  international  business  requires  a  cost  benefits  analysis  of
the  benefits  gained  versus  the  risks  encountered  by  the  investing  firm.
Influencing  the  decision  to  expand  internationally  are  the  opportunities
to tap larger markets, host-country regulations requiring local production,
achieving economies of scale, competition, implementing quality controls,
raw  materials  sourcing,  forward  integration  to  eliminate  middlemen,  and
the acquisition of new types of technologies.

Counterbalancing  these  factors  are  the  political  risks  MNCs  face  from
unilateral actions or expropriation by host-country governments. Political
risks increase when the MNC and the host country have different economic
objectives  or  conflicting  monetary  and  fiscal  policies.  Circumstantial
political risks may occur when the host government changes and the policies
of the preceding government are reversed, or when the current government
facing political difficulties or social unrest must amend its prior policies to
the detriment of the MNC.

Environmental Constraints in International Business Host governments may
also impose a variety of national controls on MNCs’ activities, including
limitation  on  the  repatriation  of  profits  and  dividends,  efforts  to  curb
transfer  pricing,  implementation  of  price  controls,  restrictions  on  foreign
ownership,  local  staffing  and  management  requirements,  import  content
rules, and labor and social controls. Assessing political risk involves first
assessing the riskiness of the host country as a place to conduct operations
and then identifying the level of risk assumed by the MNC for making a
particular  investment.  Political  risk  cannot  be  eliminated  completely,  but
management  techniques  can  help  reduce  the  level  of  political  risk.  Such
techniques include not investing in particular countries, establishing long-
term  agreements  with  host-country  governments,  lobbying,  legal  action
where  a  well-developed  legal  system  exists  within  the  host  country,
obtaining political pressure and assistance from the MNC’s home-country
government,  providing  for  local  ownership  or  joint  venturing,  promoting
host-government objectives, developing contingency plans, and purchasing
insurance coverage for political risk.

8.4  SUMMARY

Basic  functional  and  operational  activities  in  international  business  are
same as domestic business. The difference is across boundaries operations
encounters different economies, cultures, legal systems, governments and
languages  which  must  be  integrated  into  business  policies  and  practices.
Entry and expansion of a firm may necessitate entry into international trade.
Entry into international business varies among continuum ranging from basic
entry  levels  like  exporting  to  the  rest  like  licensing,  franchising,  contract
manufacturing, direct investment, joint ventures, wholly owned subsidiaries,
globalized  operations,  and  portfolio  investments  in  a  continuum.  Some
countries have low risk and give lots of support to international enterprisers
and  others  have  dismal  interest  and  policy  support.  Governments  in  the
host  country  play  a  crucial  role  in  deciding  either  to  restrict  or  support
international business. Often international business policy of a government
is in alignment with monetary and economic goal along with maintenance
of national security, improve health, safety and employment level or support
specific  political  objectives.  Protectionism,  tariffs  non-tariff  barriers  and
government interventions are the methods of restricting international trade.

8.5   KEY WORDS

International Business: A cross boarder transaction between individuals,
business or boarder entities for anything such as goods, services, technology,
knowledge, etc.

Licensing:  A  licensor  (i.e.  the  firm  with  the  technology  or  brand)  can
provide  their  products,  services,  brand  and/or  technology  to  a  licensee
via an agreement. This agreement will describe the terms of the strategic
alliance,  allowing  the  licensor  affordable  and  low  risk  entry  to  a  foreign
market while the licensee can gain access to the competitive advantages and
unique assets of another firm.

Franchising: Parent firm gives right to other firms to carry on business in
its name.

Outsourcing and Off-shoring: Giving contracts to international firms for
certain business purpose.

Multinational Firms (MNCs): The firms that are conducting business in
more than one country.

Foreign  Direct  Investment  (FDI):  Investment made  by  an  individual or
a  firm  located  in  one  country  to  the  business  interest  located  in  another
foreign country.

Investing directly into production in a country by a firm located in another
country  either  by  buying  a  firm  in  the  target  country  or  by  expanding
operations of an existing business in that country.

Franchisee: A holder of a franchise, a person who is granted a franchise.

Franchisor: A firm or person who grants franchises.

Franchising:  is a business set up in which an organization (The franchiser)
has the option to grant entrepreneurial rights to a local firm (Franchisee) to
access its brand, trademarks and products for value.

Barter: Exchange of goods and services without involving currency.

Counter Purchase: Sale of goods and services to one firm in another country
by a firm that promises to make future purchase of a specific product from
the same firm in that country.

Switch Trading: Practice in which one firm sells to another firm to fulfill
the obligation made to make purchase in a given country.

Joint  Venture:  A  cooperative  partnership  between  two  individuals  or
businesses in which profits and risks are shared.

Contract  Manufacturing:  Is  a  business  model  in  which  firm  hires  a
contract manufacture to produce components or final products based on the
hiring firms design.

Exporting:  The  sale  of  capital  goods  and  services  across  international
borders or territories or in other words, act of selling to a foreign country.

Brownfield Strategy:  The entering of a foreign market via the purchase of
an existing firm.

Foreign Exchange:  Transactions involving the exchange of one currency
for another.
