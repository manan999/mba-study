COURSE  INTRODUCTION

Dear Learners

Rural markets in India have significant importance, primarily because the majority
of our population is based in rural India. One can easily understand that rural India’s
market potential is enormous given that more than 60 percent of the population
lives in approximately 6,50,000 villages. Improvements in infrastructure, increased
information technology usage, the widespread use of smartphones, informed rural
customers, and, most importantly, a more progressive and reform-focused rural
environment have all contributed to changes in the rural market environment during
the past few decades. To understand rural India and its markets, it is important to
explore them through a variety of perspectives instead of a single one. With people
from different socioeconomic classes, a variety of housing styles, and agriculture
as the main line of work, rural India is a very diverse place. Alternative occupations
that are not agricultural in nature are also on the rise, as is the use of digital and
smart phones, and self-help groups are a visible entity in rural India.You will learn
about some of these aspects of rural marketing in this course as we explore the
vastness and heterogeneity of rural India’s markets. This will help you gain a better
understanding of this fascinating course.

The following learning outcomes are expected to be accomplished by the course,
which consists of four blocks spread over 15 units. After studying this course, you
should be able to:

 Distinguish between the characteristic features of rural and urban markets.

 Analyze and utilize the implications of the rural environment for marketers.

 Describe the buying process and consumption trends for rural consumers.

 Discuss the importance of marketing research for the rural market.

 Analyze rural marketing mix strategies.



Identify the issues and challenges in accessing rural markets.

 Discuss the scope and nature of physical distribution and retailing in rural

areas.

 Apply the concepts learned through illustrated case studies.

BLOCK 1 RURAL MARKETS - AN OVERVIEW

The first block on “Rural Markets - An Overview” consists of three units.

Unit 2, “Understanding Rural Environment: I,” explains the environment in rural

rural markets. The unit covers the social, political, and technological aspects that

like India is unique and complex, and rural India very much resonates with that.

that  has  guided  and  supported  a  society.  On  the  other  hand,  the  economic
environment includes factual economic realities and related issues. There have been
massive developments, and technology has been a game changer that has changed
lives and added value to rural markets and the people around them. These factors
help in closely evaluating the possibilities of a given area and also enable an
understanding of the heterogeneity of rural markets in general. All these components
are important facets for a deep dive into understanding real India and planning for

service merely 200,000 of them. There are still 450,000 villages that are yet to be

1.2 DEFINING  ‘RURAL’

The first thing which requires clarity even before we dig deeper into the concept
and principles of rural marketing, is defining rural markets. How does one define a
rural market? The Census of India defines rural as any area with a population density
of 400 people per square kilometre and where at least 75 per cent of the male
working population is engaged in agriculture and where there exists no municipality
or board.The definition provides a working sense of how you can understand any
rural market. If the municipality exists it becomes a city. Take for instance the city

the new capital of the country in a newer part then called as Raisina hills and was

1.3 DIFFERENCES  IN  RURAL  AND  URBAN

MARKETS

Urban and rural markets have differences which are both physical in nature and
there are differences which are invisible but largely social in nature. It is wrong to
believe which many marketers have misunderstood that rural remains the poor cousin
of urban. The origins of urban actually lies in rural. Let us dissect the rural and
urban a little more to understand them better. Let us first start with the basic difference
in terms of the physical differences that exists. You will notice that the rural part of
the India has unique traits from where the physical differences will emerge.

1. Dependence predominantly on agriculture remains an important point of
departure. Urban counterparts have multiple occupations to choose from
blue collar to white collar roles. Rural is about agriculture and the several
roles that emerge from there. Not everyone in rural India is a landowner
and there are cultivators and landless farmers too. There are stakeholders
in agriculture sector who are suppliers of various kinds of Agri equipment
to the sector like pumps, harvesting machine, ploughing machines, irrigation
machines  etc.  In  the  last  few  years,  the  concept  of  rental  (of Agri
equipment’s) has become more formalised with a lot of companies now
delving into this. Earlier, the famer community and their camaraderie led
to these equipment’s etc. being borrowed. Today, there are full-fledged
agencies dealing in them. There are micro lenders in the agriculture sector
who are involved in providing small credits to the farmers too. In short,
there is a complete ecosystem which is directly and indirectly involved in
the primary sector. However, recent trends do indicate that the farm size
holding over time has been reducing. Look at Table 1.1 and 1.2 which
showcases data holding between 1995-96 and 2015-16. There is a rise
in the data holding in the smaller size farm holding and reduction in the
larger size farm holding.  However, as you go through this course you will
realise that non-farm occupations are becoming increasingly dominant as
income generation activities in rural India.We shall discuss these issues in
detail in Unit 2 in-depth.

3. Role of MFI (micro-finance institutions) in the rural markets are more
pronounced. They play a key role and have been there since ages (history
of India always talks of sahukaars or money lenders). Hindi cinema has
perhaps best captured the spirit of these micro lenders (Lala Sukhi Ram
in Mother India is iconic). Earlier there were individual money lenders but
today they have now been supplemented with MFI’s who have been playing
a stellar role. These MFIs’ help the farmers in buying Agri inputs, seasonal
shopping, festival shopping and many other emergency requirements. The
community of small farmers in India is quite large and there is always a
perennial requirement of fund, as their income is harvesting month dependant.
This is where the micro lenders are timely help. It is not that these kinds
of lenders do not exist in the urban markets, however the context and needs
are different.

4. The increasing role of Self-Help Groups (SHGs) are something unique to
rural markets. SHGs are communities of villagers coming together for a
common cause. Often the cause is related to internal or external driven
activity. Internal activities can be related to daily chores or assistance required
in fulfilment of those chores. For instance, agriculture and related activities
often require community support and this is where SHGs come in for
collaborative work. For external activities like selling the Agri produce, SHGs
come together for sharing logistics cost or for creating local products of
handloom or handicraft and share resources and services. Often banks are
more comfortable providing loans to SHGs rather than an individual.

5. The rural market is more heterogenous in terms of regions. The farmers
of Punjab, Haryana, Western Uttar Pradesh versus the farmers of Bihar,
West Bengal and further eastern states versus the farmers of Maharashtra,
Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh are not similar in terms of social or economic
background. They often have different orientation in terms of how the crops
are to be looked at or the size of the land holding they have or the kind of
ownerships they have. Often disparity remains a concern here and the same
is manifested differently across regions.

6. Rural market and the context of retailing with respect to SKUs (Stock
Keeping Units) are also different. Unlike urban markets large SKUs do
not sell and are restricted to smaller units and pack sizes. One of the most
popular examples of the mid 2000 era is Coca Cola launching a small pack
of 200 ml costing Rs.5/-. The pack size and pricing were ideal since absence
of refrigerator and storage issue are perfectly compensated for. Products
like shampoo, packaged goods like condiments, pulses, detergents and toilet
soaps etc. sell well in the smaller SKU formats.

7. Word of mouth plays a critical role in rural markets. Though, this often
remains a feature also in urban areas where the population is below
subsistence levels and is underprivileged, but rural areas show this as a
more pronounced feature. This is partly because of lack of education and
exposure to the world. Perhaps this leads to dependence on the peer group
for all kinds of validation. Observe that in your neighbourhood and adjacent
areas how do the workforce (especially support services workers like Aya,
Bai, Cook and drivers) and you shall get understanding of the mindset.
This remains an important trend and often marketers rely on word of mouth
for product sales.

8. Rural markets buying patterns have seasonality. Though in the last few years
they have been bucking this trend yet purchases mostly takes place during
the post - harvest season. The purchase patterns are interesting since durables
to Agri related purchases to automobiles to tractors, the range is diverse.
The rural haats and melas are also integral to rural markets and often play
an important role. There are some of the biggest rural fairs in the country
like the Nauchandi Mela in western U.P., the cattle fair in Pushkar Rajasthan
and Sonepur Bihar, the annual Kumbh Mela and the weekly haats and fairs

9. Land, gold, and automobiles are usually the asset base rural markets look
at. The order of priority is the same as written. Land is often considered a
holy asset and it cannot be sold unless there are pressing needs. Most often
if there is surplus income which gets invested in land followed by gold.
Tractors, jeep, bikes, and the recent surge in smartphone penetration have
shown that rural consumers do invest here.

Traditionally, households in   rural India were joint families. But with the rise in
population, the resulting pressure on land, and several other socio-economic factors
(education, separate sources of income, migration, etc.), joint families are now breaking
apart. Families are now gradually moving towards the nuclear model in rural areas.
A new concept, that of ‘individualized joint families’, is emerging, in which families
stay in the same house, but have separate kitchens in order to avoid conflicts, pursue
individualized choices, and nurture their children better. With the increase in
‘individualized joint’ and nuclear families, the range and number of branded products
entering the family is likely to increase. In terms of age and as per Census 2011,
approximately 31 per cent of the rural population is less than 15 years of age, while
around 60 per cent of rural population is in the age bracket of 15-59 years.The
Millennial population of India is around 450 million out of which around 300 million
Millennials (67 per cent of total population) live in Rural India. The 300 million
Rural Millennials (36 per cent of the Rural Population) represent a significant market.
However, there is a distinct trend of migration amongst the millennials. More number
of millennials leave their home for urban cities from the states of Bihar and Uttar
Pradesh in pursuit of education and employment opportunities.

1.5.1  Role of Women in Rural Settings

Cultural and social norms are slowly changing, with nuclear families creating new
roles for women. Traditionally, males were the dominant decision-makers for all

markets, but now women do participate in decision-making for the items they use,
such as cooking utensils, food items, and soaps, as a result of greater exposure.There

to advertising and the products available.The 50 per cent reservation for women
in the Panchayati Raj has also played a key role in women empowerment. This
greater level of exposure influences purchase behaviour for the complete household,
and creates a willingness to try out new products, services, and brands. Some
corporations have already identified this sizeable new target consumer and are
designing appropriate communication to create awareness for their brands.Companies
should focus on creating awareness and excitement for their brands amongst the
women, youth, and children, rather than focusing on the older generations which
remain more resistant to change.

the first place to report for health problems are the PHC (Primary Health
Centres) where the first line of treatment and medicines are available. In
case there are issues or challenges (related to availability etc.) then the patients
can visit the CHC (Community Health Centre). These are in the vicinity
of a bigger village and cater to multiple villages. The facilities are bigger
and even AYUSH facilities can be availed. Finally, if the patient does not
get the right kind of service, then they can visit the nearest hospital in the
closest district. Unfortunately, in this case there is an accessibility issue as
nearly 2/3rd rural Indians do not have access to critical medicine. An NCAER
study shows that 25 percent of the healthcare centres cater to 75 percent
population. So, there are two challenges, one being accessservices to health
and second, coverage. Rural people have to travel the distance to avail
healthcare facilities (that can be up to30km or more). Healthcare in India
has seen spending but the same does not translate to benefits. The government
spending hasn’t really translated to real time benefits and the private spending
always tries to recover full costs from the patients, making private healthcare
expensive. Take for instance the AIIMS facility in the heart of the national
capital, considered the best in the country. Patients have to wait for months
to get some kind of basic attention, leave aside treatment. One can imagine
that the conditions are quite dismal.

3.2 CULTURAL  ENVIRONMENT

What is culture? It is a set of values and beliefs which are endorsed by the society,
family and places of interaction, which gets reflected through the individual behaviour.
A baby born to a family will be instilled with the values and beliefs of the family
and will continue to do so till death. As the child starts going to school, college,
workplace and interacts socially, there are new set of values and beliefs being instilled
continuously. In his activities of life, he will continue to reflect the, through his
consumption, behaviour, approach to other people and interaction with society in
general. Culture therefore is the invisible hand and cannot be seen but experienced.
Marketers when trying to market and sell their products often have to understand
the consumer culture. If the product or service does not fit in their cultural framework,
there is a likelihood that the acceptance will be low or negative. There have been
many marketing failures as the product did not fit the cultural traits of the Indian
consumer. Remember the brand SunfeastPazzta which was introduced in the middle

IMPLICATIONS  ON ADMINISTRATION AND
GOVERNANCE

Finally, let us try to connect the entire discussion from the context of strategic decision
making for rural markets. How will marketers be able to take decision to penetrate
the rural market? Unfortunately, in the last 70 years or so there has been very little
attempt made to enter these markets, primarily because of perceived low potential
or logistical aspects of reaching these markets. Some of the MNC brands like Unilever
or Colgate have met with success because their efforts have been sustained, built
over time. On the other hand, in India brands like Dabur and some of the regional
players like Balaji, Toluram, Acchi, Bisk Farm, Anmol to name a few have met
with limited success. Rural markets are difficult to crack, which is a given. It is not
easy to simply enter the market and start selling. It is not a Momo counter that
you simply put up at the roadside kerb and start selling. The rural market with
its social, economic cultural and physical environment features requires a different
kind of orientation. The consumers are shy, apprehensive, suspicious, and unaware.
Remember  the  4A’s  of  rural:  Affordability,  Availability,  Awareness  and
Acceptability. Well, the rural markets are guided by these four principles all the
time. The product has to be affordable in the sense, value for money. This does
not mean cheap low value products. Remember, Bolero from Mahindra or Fortuner/
Endeavour (Toyota/Ford) sells the most in rural markets and on the other hand
there are people there who survive on one meal a day. The diversity is indeed striking.
Availability remains another challenge for rural marketers. Often logistical challenges
means that the product is not regularly available Hard candies like Alpenliebe (from
Perfetti Van Melle) is a popular product which often remains short supply in rural
markets. We also talked about the Frooti story in the previous unit and how in
peak summers, it remains short supply. Sometimes, awareness may a concern.
Consumers may simply not be aware of the product.  There are either comprehension
issues or may be logistically marketers simply cannot deliver. The third dimension

of delivering value added services. Some of the discussions above, especially in

and are not open to changes. It sometimes becomes a challenge indeed. Peer group
